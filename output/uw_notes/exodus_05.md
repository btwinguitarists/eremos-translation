# Exodus 5 Chapter Introduction

## Structure and Formatting

  - v. 1–5: Moses gives Pharaoh Yahweh’s message
  - v. 6–21: Pharaoh makes the Israelites work harder
  - v. 22–23: Moses asks Yahweh why things went badly

## Exodus 5:1

**Quote:** וְ⁠אַחַ֗ר בָּ֚אוּ מֹשֶׁ֣ה וְ⁠אַהֲרֹ֔ן וַ⁠יֹּאמְר֖וּ אֶל־פַּרְעֹ֑ה
**Support:** rc://*/ta/man/translate/writing-newevent
The time phrase, **and afterward**, marks a transition in the narrative. Use the natural form in your language for introducing a new event. Alternate translation: [After that]

**Quote:** וְ⁠אַחַ֗ר בָּ֚אוּ מֹשֶׁ֣ה וְ⁠אַהֲרֹ֔ן וַ⁠יֹּאמְר֖וּ אֶל־פַּרְעֹ֑ה
**Support:** rc://*/ta/man/translate/grammar-connect-time-sequential
It is unclear how long Moses and Aaron waited before they went to see Pharaoh.

**Quote:** בָּ֚אוּ
**Support:** rc://*/ta/man/translate/figs-go
In this instance, depending on how they have to set scenes, some languages may need to use “came in.”

**Quote:** פַּרְעֹ֑ה
**Support:** rc://*/ta/man/translate/writing-participants
Pharaoh is reintroduced as a participant in the narrative for the first time since chapter two. Use the natural form in your language for reintroducing a character.

**Quote:** כֹּֽה־אָמַ֤ר יְהוָה֙ אֱלֹהֵ֣י יִשְׂרָאֵ֔ל שַׁלַּח֙ אֶת־עַמִּ֔⁠י וְ⁠יָחֹ֥גּוּ לִ֖⁠י בַּ⁠מִּדְבָּֽר
**Support:** rc://*/ta/man/translate/figs-quotemarks
This is a direct quotation. It may be helpful to your readers to indicate this by marking it with first-level quotation marks or with whatever other punctuation or convention your language uses to indicate a quotation. There is a second-level quotation fully contained within this portion which is addressed elsewhere. There may be other quotations in this chapter; they will be marked by the same punctuation, but they will not have their own notes unless there is something unique about them.

**Quote:** כֹּֽה־אָמַ֤ר יְהוָה֙
This quotation formula is used to introduce commands from Yahweh. See Chapter 4 Introduction for more information.

**Quote:** וְ⁠יָחֹ֥גּוּ
This is a celebration to worship Yahweh. Alternate translation: [so they can worship me with eating and drinking]

## Exodus 5:2

**Quote:** וַ⁠יֹּ֣אמֶר
**Support:** rc://*/ta/man/translate/grammar-connect-logic-contrast
The result here is exactly as God has said; therefore, a contrastive transition word is reasonable but not required, given the immediate human context.

**Quote:** מִ֤י יְהוָה֙ אֲשֶׁ֣ר אֶשְׁמַ֣ע בְּ⁠קֹל֔⁠וֹ לְ⁠שַׁלַּ֖ח אֶת־יִשְׂרָאֵ֑ל
This is not a rhetorical question, though Pharaoh may have spoken with contempt. Pharaoh would have considered himself a god. The Egyptians had several gods, and most people groups were assumed to have their own gods. Therefore, Pharaoh had truly not heard of Yahweh (who has just revealed his name to Moses).

**Quote:** אֶת־יִשְׂרָאֵ֑ל & אֶת־יִשְׂרָאֵ֖ל
**Support:** rc://*/ta/man/translate/grammar-collectivenouns
Here the nation is spoken of as an individual. If your language does not use singular nouns in that way, you can use a different expression. Alternate translation: [the nation of Israel]

**Quote:** בְּ⁠קֹל֔⁠וֹ
**Support:** rc://*/ta/man/translate/figs-metonymy
Here, **his voice** represent the words God spoke. If it would be helpful in your language, you could use an equivalent expression or plain language. Alternate translation: [to what he says]

**Quote:** וְ⁠גַ֥ם
This functions to strengthen Pharaoh’s statement about his decision. Alternate translation: [and most definitely]

## Exodus 5:4

**Quote:** לָ֚⁠מָּה מֹשֶׁ֣ה וְ⁠אַהֲרֹ֔ן תַּפְרִ֥יעוּ אֶת־הָ⁠עָ֖ם מִ⁠מַּֽעֲשָׂ֑י⁠ו
**Support:** rc://*/ta/man/translate/figs-rquestion
Pharaoh uses this rhetorical question to express his anger toward Moses and Aaron for taking the Israelites away from their work. If you would not use a rhetorical question for this purpose in your language, you could translate his words as a statement or an exclamation and communicate the emphasis in another way. Alternate translation: [Stop distracting the people from doing their work!]

**Quote:** לְכ֖וּ לְ⁠סִבְלֹתֵי⁠כֶֽם
**Support:** rc://*/ta/man/translate/figs-youdual
This **your** could refer to Moses and Aaron, or it could mean “all you Israelite people.” The second meaning is more likely. If your language uses different forms of “you” depending on the number of people addressed, use a dual or plural form here.

## Exodus 5:5

**Quote:** וַ⁠יֹּ֣אמֶר פַּרְעֹ֔ה
**Support:** rc://*/ta/man/translate/grammar-connect-words-phrases
It is unusual for the same speaker to be reintroduced like this. Some translations omit the repetition, but there is another option. This statement may be more connected to the next couple of verses, so it might be appropriate to make a minor break before this verse.

**Quote:** וַ⁠יֹּ֣אמֶר פַּרְעֹ֔ה הֵן־רַבִּ֥ים עַתָּ֖ה עַ֣ם הָ⁠אָ֑רֶץ וְ⁠הִשְׁבַּתֶּ֥ם אֹתָ֖⁠ם מִ⁠סִּבְלֹתָֽ⁠ם
**Support:** rc://*/ta/man/translate/figs-aside
This statement by Pharaoh may have been him commenting to himself, or he may have only thought this statement silently; either way, it led to his next actions. If this would be confusing in your language, consider stating it in a way that clarifies that his statement or thoughts here impacted his next actions.

**Quote:** הֵן
**Behold** is a term meant to focus the attention of the listener on what the speaker is about to say.

**Quote:** וְ⁠הִשְׁבַּתֶּ֥ם
**Support:** rc://*/ta/man/translate/figs-youdual
Here, **you** means Moses and Aaron. If your language uses different forms of “you” depending on the number of people addressed, use a dual form if you have one, or if not, use a plural form here.

## Exodus 5:6

**Quote:** וַ⁠יְצַ֥ו פַּרְעֹ֖ה & אֶת־הַ⁠נֹּגְשִׂ֣ים בָּ⁠עָ֔ם וְ⁠אֶת־שֹׁטְרָ֖י⁠ו לֵ⁠אמֹֽר
Pharaoh may have sent a messenger or messengers to the taskmasters and officers or spoken to one or a few representatives of them at his court rather than speaking to them directly. Alternate translation: [And … Pharaoh gave an order to the taskmasters among the people and officers. He said,]

**Quote:** אֶת־הַ⁠נֹּגְשִׂ֣ים
**Taskmasters** were Egyptians whose job it was to force the Israelites to do hard work. These are probably the same as the “overseers” of labor gangs from [Exodus 1:11](../01/11.md), but here the term is harsher. Alternate translation: [oppressors]

**Quote:** וְ⁠אֶת־שֹׁטְרָ֖י⁠ו
**Officers** were probably Israelite slaves who were in charge of the other Israelites but who answered to the Egyptian taskmasters.

**Quote:** לֵ⁠אמֹֽר
Here, **saying** is a way of introducing a direct quotation in Hebrew; most translations can omit it without harm.

## Exodus 5:10

**Quote:** וַ⁠יֵּ֨צְא֜וּ
**Support:** rc://*/ta/man/translate/writing-newevent
This begins a new scene, which may need to be marked in a certain way in your language.

**Quote:** נֹגְשֵׂ֤י
**Taskmasters** were Egyptians whose job was to force the Israelites to do hard work. See how you translated this in [5:6](../05/06.md).

**Quote:** כֹּ֚ה אָמַ֣ר פַּרְעֹ֔ה
This is the same quotation introduction as is used when Yahweh gives a direct command. See Chapter 4 Introduction for more information.

**Quote:** וַ⁠יֹּאמְר֥וּ אֶל־הָ⁠עָ֖ם לֵ⁠אמֹ֑ר כֹּ֚ה אָמַ֣ר פַּרְעֹ֔ה
**Support:** rc://*/ta/man/translate/figs-quotations
It might be helpful in some languages to translate **thus says Pharaoh** as an indirect quote so that his conveyed command is not a quote within a quote. Alternate translation: [and told the people that Pharaoh says]

**Quote:** אֵינֶ֛⁠נִּי
**Support:** rc://*/ta/man/translate/figs-quotesinquotes
This is the beginning of a second-level quotation that continues until the end of [verse 11](../05/11.md). It may be helpful to your readers to indicate this with an opening second-level quotation mark or with whatever other punctuation or convention your language uses to indicate the start of a quotation within a quotation. However, you could also translate this as an indirect quote. See UST.

**Quote:** אֵינֶ֛⁠נִּי נֹתֵ֥ן לָ⁠כֶ֖ם תֶּֽבֶן
**Support:** rc://*/ta/man/translate/grammar-connect-logic-contrast
The structure of this phrase and the one that opens [the next verse](../05/11.md) create a strong contrast between what **I** (Pharaoh) and **you** (you all, the Israelites) will do. Translate this in a way that emphasizes, if possible, the contrast between **I** and **you**. Alternate translation: [It is not I who will give you straw.]

**Quote:** אֵינֶ֛⁠נִּי נֹתֵ֥ן לָ⁠כֶ֖ם תֶּֽבֶן
**Support:** rc://*/ta/man/translate/figs-metonymy
Pharaoh uses **I** to refer to his ownership and commands. Pharaoh does not physically give the Israelites straw. Rather, he had commanded his servants to take straw that belonged to Pharaoh and give it to the Israelites. Now he told his servants to stop providing straw. If it would be helpful in your language, you could use an equivalent expression or plain language. Alternate translation: [My servants will no longer give you my straw]

**Quote:** לָ⁠כֶ֖ם
**Support:** rc://*/ta/man/translate/figs-youdual
**You** is plural and refers to the Israelite people. If your language uses different forms of “you” depending on the number of people addressed, use a plural form here.

## Exodus 5:11

**Quote:** אַתֶּ֗ם לְכ֨וּ קְח֤וּ לָ⁠כֶם֙ תֶּ֔בֶן מֵ⁠אֲשֶׁ֖ר תִּמְצָ֑אוּ
**Support:** rc://*/ta/man/translate/grammar-connect-logic-contrast
The structure of this phrase serves as the opposing side of the contrast Pharaoh sets up in the last phrase. By explicitly using a pronoun with a command, he is emphasizing who will go get straw. If possible, translate this in a way that emphasizes the contrast between **I** and **you.** See the previous verse. Alternate translation: [Rather, it is you yourselves who must go get straw from wherever you might find it]

**Quote:** אַתֶּ֗ם
**Support:** rc://*/ta/man/translate/figs-youdual
Here, **you** is plural and refers to the Israelite people. If your language uses different forms of “you” depending on the number of people addressed, use a plural form here.

**Quote:** אַתֶּ֗ם & לָ⁠כֶם֙
**Support:** rc://*/ta/man/translate/figs-rpronouns
The repetition of **you** (as **your**) emphasizes that the Egyptians will no longer help the slaves. See the UST.

**Quote:** כִּ֣י אֵ֥ין נִגְרָ֛ע מֵ⁠עֲבֹדַתְ⁠כֶ֖ם דָּבָֽר
**Support:** rc://*/ta/man/translate/figs-doublenegatives

## Exodus 5:14

**Quote:** נֹגְשֵׂ֥י
**Taskmasters** are Egyptians whose job it was to force the Israelites to do hard work. See how you translated this in [Exodus 5:6](../05/06.md).

**Quote:** וַ⁠יֻּכּ֗וּ שֹֽׁטְרֵי֙ בְּנֵ֣י יִשְׂרָאֵ֔ל
**Support:** rc://*/ta/man/translate/figs-activepassive
The text does not specify who beat the officers. It was probably either the taskmasters or someone the taskmasters ordered to beat the officers. If your language does not use the passive form in this way, you can express the idea in active form or in another way that is natural in your language. Alternate translation: [And the taskmasters beat the officers of the sons of Israel]

**Quote:** מַדּ֡וּעַ לֹא֩ כִלִּיתֶ֨ם חָקְ⁠כֶ֤ם לִ⁠לְבֹּן֙ כִּ⁠תְמ֣וֹל שִׁלְשֹׁ֔ם גַּם־תְּמ֖וֹל גַּם־הַ⁠יּֽוֹם
**Support:** rc://*/ta/man/translate/figs-rquestion
The taskmasters used this question to show they were angry with the lack of bricks. If you would not use a rhetorical question for this purpose in your language, you could translate his words as a statement or an exclamation and communicate the emphasis in another way. Alternate translation: [You are not producing enough bricks, either yesterday or today, as you did in the past!]

**Quote:** כִלִּיתֶ֨ם חָקְ⁠כֶ֤ם
**Support:** rc://*/ta/man/translate/figs-youdual
Here, **you** and **your** are plural. If your language uses different forms of “you” depending on the number of people addressed, use a plural form here.

**Quote:** לֹא֩ כִלִּיתֶ֨ם חָקְ⁠כֶ֤ם
**Support:** rc://*/ta/man/translate/figs-synecdoche
Here, **you** and **your** do not simply refer to the officers; they refer to the groups of Israelite workers the officers are in charge of. If it would be helpful in your language, you could translate the meaning plainly. Alternate translation: [have your teams not finished their required]

**Quote:** כִּ⁠תְמ֣וֹל שִׁלְשֹׁ֔ם
**Support:** rc://*/ta/man/translate/figs-idiom
This is a Hebrew idiom meaning “in the past.” Here it specifically refers to the time before the Egyptians stopped giving the Israelites straw. See how you translated this in [5:7](../05/07.md). Alternate translation: [before] or [previously]

**Quote:** גַּם־תְּמ֖וֹל גַּם־הַ⁠יּֽוֹם
This time expression refers to more recent days, ones since the Egyptians stopped providing the straw to the Israelites.
