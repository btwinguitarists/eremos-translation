## Chapter intro

# Job 34 Chapter Introduction

## Structure and Formatting

This chapter is a continuation of Elihu’s speech. In this chapter, Elihu speaks first to Job’s friends and others who may be listening, then from [verse 16](../34/16.md) onward he addresses Job directly, and then he speaks again to the others about Job starting in [verse 34](../34/34.md).

The ULT sets the lines of this chapter farther to the right on the page than the rest of the text because it is poetry.

## Religious and Cultural Concepts in This Chapter

### Elihu speaking harshly about Job

It may be puzzling to your readers that while Elihu promises Job in [33:7](../33/07.md) that he will be gentle with him, in this chapter, in [verses 7–8](../34/07.md) and [35](../34/35.md), Elihu speaks very harshly about Job. However, this is really a matter of interpretation rather than translation, so it is not necessary to offer an explanation within the text of your translation.

## Translation Issues in This Chapter

### Elihu quoting Job and his friends

In several places in this chapter, Elihu directly quotes Job or his friends. He does this to affirm what the friends said and to challenge what Job said. To help your readers appreciate that Elihu is doing this, you may wish to translate his expressions the same way you translated them when Job or his friends used them.
In [34:3](../34/03.md), Elihu quotes what Job said in [12:11](../12/11.md).
In [34:5](../34/05.md), Elihu quotes what Job said in [27:2](../27/02.md).
In [34:6](../34/06.md), Elihu quotes what Job said in [6:4](../06/04.md), [16:13](../16/13.md), and [27:4](../27/04.md).
In [34:7](../34/07.md), Elihu quotes what Eliphaz said in [15:16](../15/16.md).
In [34:12](../34/12.md), Elihu quotes what Bildad said in [8:3](../08/03.md).

### “man” and “men” with generic meaning

In several places in this chapter, Elihu uses the words “man” and “men” in a generic sense that is clearly inclusive of both men and women. It may be helpful in your translation to say “men and women” or to use a term in your language that is clearly inclusive of both men and women. Notes suggest ways in which you might do this. (See: [[rc://*/ta/man/translate/figs-gendernotations]])

### “For” at the start of a verse introducing a reason

Several times in this chapter, Elihu says “For” at the beginning of a verse to introduce the reason for something he said in the previous verse. Elihu does this in [verses 3](../34/03.md), [5](../34/05.md), [9](../34/09.md), [11](../34/11.md), [21](../34/21.md), and [37](../34/37.md). If it would be helpful to your readers, you could refer back more explicitly to the previous verse in order to show what Elihu is doing. The UST models ways to do this in each case. (“For” at the beginning of [verse 23](../34/23.md) introduces a new consideration, as the UST also illustrates.) (See: [[rc://*/ta/man/translate/grammar-connect-words-phrases]])

## Job 34:1

**Quote:** וַ⁠יַּ֥עַן אֱלִיה֗וּא וַ⁠יֹּאמַֽר
**Support:** rc://*/ta/man/translate/figs-hendiadys
This phrase expresses a single idea by using two words connected with **and**. The word **answered** tells for what purpose a person **said** something. In this case, Elihu said more things in light of what he had already said and thus, in a sense, in answer to them. If it would be more natural in your language, you could express this meaning with an equivalent phrase that does not use “and.” Alternate translation: [And Elihu said further, in light of what he had already said]

**Support:** rc://*/ta/man/translate/figs-metonymy

**Support:** rc://*/ta/man/translate/figs-nominaladj

## Job 34:3

**Quote:** אֹ֭זֶן מִלִּ֣ין תִּבְחָ֑ן וְ֝⁠חֵ֗ךְ יִטְעַ֥ם לֶ⁠אֱכֹֽל
**Support:** rc://*/ta/man/translate/figs-simile
As the introduction to this chapter discusses, Elihu is using the same phrase that Job used to tell his friends in [12:11](../12/11.md) that he had considered and rejected their perspective. Elihu is quoting Job in order to tell him that, for his part, he has considered Job’s perspective and rejected it. To help your readers recognize this, you may wish to use the same language in your translation here as you did in [12:11](../12/11.md).

**Quote:** אֹ֭זֶן מִלִּ֣ין תִּבְחָ֑ן וְ֝⁠חֵ֗ךְ יִטְעַ֥ם לֶ⁠אֱכֹֽל
**Support:** rc://*/ta/man/translate/grammar-connect-words-phrases
Elihu is using the word **and** to indicate that the phrase it introduces is just as true as the previous phrase. If it would be helpful to your readers, you could indicate this in your translation. Alternate translation: [the ear tests words, just as the palate tastes food]

**Quote:** אֹ֭זֶן מִלִּ֣ין תִּבְחָ֑ן וְ֝⁠חֵ֗ךְ יִטְעַ֥ם לֶ⁠אֱכֹֽל
**Support:** rc://*/ta/man/translate/figs-personification
Elihu is speaking of the **ear** as if it could **test words** by itself. He is using the ear to represent hearing, and he means that people themselves test or consider the words of others when they hear them. If it would be helpful in your language, you could state the meaning plainly. Alternate translation: [people consider others’ words when they hear them, just as people discern with their mouths the taste of their food]

**Quote:** אֹ֭זֶן מִלִּ֣ין תִּבְחָ֑ן וְ֝⁠חֵ֗ךְ יִטְעַ֥ם לֶ⁠אֱכֹֽל
**Support:** rc://*/ta/man/translate/figs-explicit
Though Elihu is making a general statement, he is referring implicitly to what Job has said and what he has decided about it. You could indicate that in your translation if it would be helpful to your readers. Alternate translation: [I have heard what Job has said and I have considered it and decided that it is not true, just as people discern with their mouths the taste of their food]

**Quote:** מִלִּ֣ין
**Support:** rc://*/ta/man/translate/figs-metonymy
Elihu is using the term **words** to mean what people say by using words. If it would be helpful in your language, you could state the meaning plainly. Alternate translation: [what people say]

**Quote:** וְ֝⁠חֵ֗ךְ יִטְעַ֥ם לֶ⁠אֱכֹֽל
**Support:** rc://*/ta/man/translate/figs-personification
Elihu is speaking of the **palate** or mouth as if it could **taste** by itself. He means that with their mouths, people discern the taste of the food that they eat. If it would be helpful in your language, you could state the meaning plainly. Alternate translation: [just as people discern with their mouths the taste of their food]

**Support:** rc://*/ta/man/translate/figs-metonymy

## Job 34:15

**Quote:** כָּל־בָּשָׂ֣ר
**Support:** rc://*/ta/man/translate/figs-metonymy
Elihu is using the term **flesh** by association to mean the creatures that God made, which generally have flesh. If it would be helpful in your language, you could state the meaning plainly. Alternate translation: [all creatures]

**Quote:** יִגְוַ֣ע & יָ֑חַד
**Support:** rc://*/ta/man/translate/figs-euphemism
Elihu is using the word **expire** to mean “die.” This is a mild way of referring to death. Your language may have a similar expression that you can use in your translation. You could also use plain language. Alternate translation: [would pass away together] or [would die at the same time]

**Quote:** וְ֝⁠אָדָ֗ם עַל־עָפָ֥ר יָשֽׁוּב
**Support:** rc://*/ta/man/translate/figs-explicit
Elihu does not mean that people would actively **return** to the **dust** on their own. Rather, as in [33:6](../33/06.md), he is referring to the way that God originally formed people from the dust of the earth, and he means that humans would die and their bodies would become dust again. You could indicate that in your translation if it would be helpful to your readers. Alternate translation: [and people would die be buried and their bodies would decompose and become part of the dirt again]
