## Chapter intro

# Exodus 11 Chapter Introduction

## Structure and Formatting

Chapter 11 is a transition point in the book. The previous nine plagues (in their three sequences of three) have come to an end and the last plague, the death of the firstborn, is about to occur, along with the Passover and the Exodus itself. It is difficult to know if the events in chapter 11 all are recorded in the order in which they happened. For translation, you should follow the order of the text if it will not confuse your readers.

It may be helpful to think that after the end of [chapter 10](../10/01.md), Yahweh spoke to Moses immediately, before he actually left Pharaoh’s presence ([11:1–2](../11/01.md)). [11:3](../11/03.md) is a comment suggesting that the Egyptians will be willing to do as the Israelites are told to ask them to do in [v. 2](../11/02.md). In verses [11:4–7](../11/04.md) Moses gives Pharaoh and his officials a message from Yahweh that Yahweh is giving to Moses right at that moment. At the end of [v. 8](../11/08.md) Moses leaves Pharaoh’s presence (completing the interaction from the [end of ch. 10](../10/28.md)). [11:9](../11/09.md) may either be Yahweh telling Moses what is going to happen, or it could be part of the summary that occurs in [v. 10](../11/10.md). Verse 10 summarizes what has happened in [chapters 5–10](../05/01.md) (especially the plagues in [chapters 7–10](../07/01.md)) in preparation for the great last plague.

## Exodus 11:1

**Quote:** אֶל־מֹשֶׁ֗ה
**Support:** rc://*/ta/man/translate/figs-quotemarks
After this phrase, a direct quote begins that continues until the end of [11:2](../11/02.md). It may be helpful to your readers to indicate this with an opening first-level quotation mark or with whatever other punctuation or convention your language uses to indicate the beginning of a quotation. There may be other quotations in this chapter; they will be marked by the same punctuation, but they will not have their own notes unless there is something unique about them.

**Quote:** כְּ⁠שַׁ֨לְּח֔⁠וֹ & יְגָרֵ֥שׁ
**Support:** rc://*/ta/man/translate/grammar-connect-time-sequential
These verbs (**lets go** and **drive away**) are meant to immediately follow one another. When you translate, make sure they are almost simultaneous in time; the letting go happens and then immediately the driving away happens.

**Quote:** כָּלָ֕ה גָּרֵ֛שׁ יְגָרֵ֥שׁ
The wording here is very forceful. Use strong words, forms, or phrasing when you are translating.

**Quote:** אֶתְ⁠כֶ֖ם & אֶתְ⁠כֶ֖ם
**Support:** rc://*/ta/man/translate/figs-youdual
Each occurrence of the word **you** in this verse is plural and refers to Moses and the rest of the Israelites. If your language uses different forms of “you” depending on the number of people addressed, use a plural form here.

## Exodus 11:2

**Quote:** נָ֖א
Here, **now** conveys urgency, indicating that Moses should speak soon, without delaying for other things. If you have a way of giving a command that shows that it is important for someone to do it quickly, it would be better to translate in that way than in a way that means “now” in a time sense (versus earlier or later).

**Quote:** בְּ⁠אָזְנֵ֣י הָ⁠עָ֑ם
Alternate translation: [so the people hear you] or [when all the people are listening]

**Quote:** כְּלֵי־כֶ֖סֶף וּ⁠כְלֵ֥י זָהָֽב
These could be any sort of thing made from silver or gold (for instance: utensils, cups, pitchers, plates, candleholders), not just jewelry.

**Quote:** וְ⁠יִשְׁאֲל֞וּ אִ֣ישׁ ׀ מֵ⁠אֵ֣ת רֵעֵ֗⁠הוּ וְ⁠אִשָּׁה֙ מֵ⁠אֵ֣ת רְעוּתָ֔⁠הּ כְּלֵי־כֶ֖סֶף וּ⁠כְלֵ֥י זָהָֽב
[Exodus 3:22](../03/22.md) contains similar instructions; see how you translated there.

## Exodus 11:3

**Quote:** וַ⁠יִּתֵּ֧ן יְהוָ֛ה אֶת־חֵ֥ן הָ⁠עָ֖ם בְּ⁠עֵינֵ֣י מִצְרָ֑יִם גַּ֣ם ׀ הָ⁠אִ֣ישׁ מֹשֶׁ֗ה גָּד֤וֹל מְאֹד֙ בְּ⁠אֶ֣רֶץ מִצְרַ֔יִם בְּ⁠עֵינֵ֥י עַבְדֵֽי־פַרְעֹ֖ה וּ⁠בְ⁠עֵינֵ֥י הָ⁠עָֽם
**Support:** rc://*/ta/man/translate/figs-aside
In this verse the author interjects a comment about the situation. Some translations choose to put the verse in parenthesis to indicate this. You may mark it in a way that is natural to your language.

**Quote:** אֶת־חֵ֥ן הָ⁠עָ֖ם בְּ⁠עֵינֵ֣י מִצְרָ֑יִם
**Support:** rc://*/ta/man/translate/figs-idiom
Here, **in the eyes of the Egyptians** is an idiom for the Egyptian’s feelings or opinion. **Favor** means those feelings are positive. Taken together, this means that when the Egyptians see the Israelites leaving Egypt, they will gladly help them. (Because the Egyptians have suffered under God’s judgment, the Egyptians want so badly to see them leave). If your language has the same or a similar idiom, you can translate or use it. Otherwise, you can translate the meaning. See how you translated this in [3:21](../03/21.md).

**Quote:** בְּ⁠עֵינֵ֥י עַבְדֵֽי־פַרְעֹ֖ה וּ⁠בְ⁠עֵינֵ֥י הָ⁠עָֽם
**Support:** rc://*/ta/man/translate/figs-idiom
Here, **in the eyes of the servants of Pharaoh and in the eyes of the people** is an idiom for their feelings or opinions. If your language has the same or a similar idiom, you can translate or use it. Otherwise, you can translate the meaning. See how you translated a similar idiom in [3:21](../03/21.md).

**Quote:** וַ⁠יִּתֵּ֧ן יְהוָ֛ה אֶת־חֵ֥ן הָ⁠עָ֖ם בְּ⁠עֵינֵ֣י מִצְרָ֑יִם
[Exodus 3:21](../03/21.md) contains a related phrase; see how you translated it there.

## Exodus 11:5

**Quote:** בְּכוֹר֮ & מִ⁠בְּכ֤וֹר & בְּכ֣וֹר & בְּכ֥וֹר
The “firstborn” always refers to the oldest male offspring.

**Quote:** מִ⁠בְּכ֤וֹר פַּרְעֹה֙ הַ⁠יֹּשֵׁ֣ב עַל־כִּסְא֔⁠וֹ עַ֚ד בְּכ֣וֹר הַ⁠שִּׁפְחָ֔ה אֲשֶׁ֖ר אַחַ֣ר הָ⁠רֵחָ֑יִם וְ⁠כֹ֖ל בְּכ֥וֹר בְּהֵמָֽה
**Support:** rc://*/ta/man/translate/figs-merism
This is a merism. First, two extremes are mentioned: the highest of society (**Pharaoh, who sits on his throne**) and the lowest (**the slave girl who is behind the mill**). Then the animals (**beasts**) are added making it a three-item list of parts of society and even the economy to show the totality of the coming judgment. If it would be helpful in your language, you could use an equivalent expression or plain language.

**Quote:** אֲשֶׁ֖ר אַחַ֣ר הָ⁠רֵחָ֑יִם
Alternate translation: [who is grinding at the handmill] or [who is behind the handmill grinding grain]

## Exodus 11:8

**Quote:** וְ⁠הִשְׁתַּֽחֲוּוּ־לִ֣⁠י
**Support:** rc://*/ta/man/translate/translate-symaction
This does not mean to bow for worshiping. **They will bow to** Moses to beg him to leave. They will do this to show how desperate they will be for Moses and the Israelites to go. If it would be helpful to your readers, you could express that explicitly.

**Quote:** וְ⁠הִשְׁתַּֽחֲוּוּ־לִ֣⁠י
**Support:** rc://*/ta/man/translate/figs-explicit
**They will bow to** Moses to beg him to leave. They will do this to show how desperate they will be for Moses and the Israelites to go. If this action would not mean the same thing in your culture, you may need to make the reason they are bowing explicit. Alternate translation: [and they will bow down to me to beg]

**Quote:** וְ⁠כָל־הָ⁠עָ֣ם אֲשֶׁר־בְּ⁠רַגְלֶ֔י⁠ךָ
**Support:** rc://*/ta/man/translate/figs-metaphor
Here, the people are pictured as below Moses, which means they are his followers. The reference to **feet** also can mean they go the same place he goes, again, meaning “follower.” If your language uses a similar image to mean follower, you can translate the image; if not, you may use another image from your culture or translate the meaning. Alternate translation: [and all the people who are following you]

**Quote:** וְ⁠אַחֲרֵי־כֵ֖ן אֵצֵ֑א
**Support:** rc://*/ta/man/translate/figs-explicit
This means that Moses and the Israelites will leave Egypt. If it would be helpful to your readers, you could express that explicitly. Alternate translation: [After that I will leave here] or [After that I will go out from Egypt]

**Quote:** בָּ⁠חֳרִי־אָֽף
**Support:** rc://*/ta/man/translate/figs-idiom
This is an idiom meaning that Moses is angry. If it would be helpful in your language, you could use an equivalent idiom or use plain language. Alternate translation: [enraged]

## Exodus 11:10

**Quote:** וּ⁠מֹשֶׁ֣ה וְ⁠אַהֲרֹ֗ן עָשׂ֛וּ אֶת־כָּל־הַ⁠מֹּפְתִ֥ים הָ⁠אֵ֖לֶּה לִ⁠פְנֵ֣י פַרְעֹ֑ה וַ⁠יְחַזֵּ֤ק יְהוָה֙ אֶת־לֵ֣ב פַּרְעֹ֔ה וְ⁠לֹֽא־שִׁלַּ֥ח אֶת־בְּנֵֽי־יִשְׂרָאֵ֖ל מֵ⁠אַרְצֽ⁠וֹ
**Support:** rc://*/ta/man/translate/writing-endofstory
This verse is summarizing and wrapping up the story of the plagues. If your language has a way of summarizing information at the end of a story, try to translate this verse (and possibly [verse 9](../11/09.md)—see the introductory notes to this chapter) in this way.

**Quote:** וַ⁠יְחַזֵּ֤ק יְהוָה֙ אֶת־לֵ֣ב פַּרְעֹ֔ה
**Support:** rc://*/ta/man/translate/figs-metaphor
This means God made him stubborn. His stubborn attitude is spoken of as if his **heart** were **strong**. If the **heart** is not the body part your culture uses to refer to a person’s will, consider using whichever organ your culture would use for this image. See how you translated this in [4:21](../04/21.md). Alternate translation: [But Yahweh caused Pharaoh to be stubborn]
