## Chapter intro

# Esther 10 Chapter Introduction

## Religious and Cultural Concepts in This Chapter

### Mordecai’s new position

Through the power of Yahweh, Mordecai was given a new position in the Persian Empire. Mordecai was now the second in command in the kingdom of Persia and he used his position to help other Jews.

## Esther 10:1

**Quote:** וַ⁠יָּשֶׂם֩ הַ⁠מֶּ֨לֶךְ אֲחַשְׁוֵר֧וֹשׁ
**Support:** rc://*/ta/man/translate/writing-newevent
This introduces a new event in the story. Use a natural way to introduce a new event in your language.

**Quote:** וַ⁠יָּשֶׂם֩ & מַ֛ס
Alternate translation: [Then … levied a tax]

**Quote:** עַל־הָ⁠אָ֖רֶץ וְ⁠אִיֵּ֥י הַ⁠יָּֽם
**Support:** rc://*/ta/man/translate/figs-merism
The purpose of this chapter is to describe the greatness of Mordecai. It does that by showing that he was second in command to a very powerful emperor. Referring to both the land and the sea is a way to include everything that lives in a very large area of the earth. If it would be helpful in your language, you could express the general meaning by saying something like “everyone throughout his empire.” Alternate translation: [on all the people in the empire … even the people who lived on the islands in the Mediterranean Sea] or [on everyone throughout the land and even the far-away islands]

**Quote:** וְ⁠אִיֵּ֥י הַ⁠יָּֽם
**Support:** rc://*/ta/man/translate/figs-explicit
The phrase **the islands of the sea** likely refers to the fact that the Persian kings had conquered territories reaching all the way to the Mediterranean Sea. If it would be helpful in your language, you could say that explicitly. Alternate translation: [which reached all the way to the Mediterranean Sea.]

**Quote:** עַל־הָ⁠אָ֖רֶץ וְ⁠אִיֵּ֥י הַ⁠יָּֽם
**Support:** rc://*/ta/man/translate/figs-metonymy
These geographic features were not expected to pay the tax. The land and coastlands represent the people living there. The story is describing those people by reference to something associated with them, the places where they live.

## Esther 10:2

**Quote:** וְ⁠כָל־מַעֲשֵׂ֤ה תָקְפּ⁠וֹ֙ וּ⁠גְב֣וּרָת֔⁠וֹ
**Support:** rc://*/ta/man/translate/figs-doublet
**Power** and **might** mean essentially the same thing. They are used together to emphasize how powerful King Ahasuerus was. If it is more natural in your language, you can use one word with that meaning, with another word that gives it emphasis. Alternate translation: [all that he achieved because of how very powerful he was] or [all the great things that he did because of his great power]

**Quote:** תָקְפּ⁠וֹ֙ וּ⁠גְב֣וּרָת֔⁠וֹ
**Support:** rc://*/ta/man/translate/figs-abstractnouns
The abstract nouns **power** and **might** can be translated with an adjective. See the UST.

**Quote:** וּ⁠פָרָשַׁת֙
Alternate translation: [They also wrote a full account]

**Quote:** גְּדֻלַּ֣ת מָרְדֳּכַ֔י
Alternate translation: [of how important Mordecai was] or [of how the king had honored Mordecai for the great things he had done]

**Quote:** גִּדְּל֖⁠וֹ
See how you translated this phrase in [3:1](../03/01.md) and [5:11](../05/11.md). Alternate translation: [had promoted him]

**Quote:** הֲ⁠לוֹא־הֵ֣ם כְּתוּבִ֗ים עַל־סֵ֨פֶר֙ דִּבְרֵ֣י הַ⁠יָּמִ֔ים לְ⁠מַלְכֵ֖י מָדַ֥י וּ⁠פָרָֽס
**Support:** rc://*/ta/man/translate/figs-rquestion
This is actually a statement. The question form is used to emphasize the certainty of the statement. If questions are not used this way in your language, then use a statement instead, as in the UST.

**Quote:** הֲ⁠לוֹא־הֵ֣ם כְּתוּבִ֗ים
**Support:** rc://*/ta/man/translate/figs-activepassive
You can say this with an active form, and you can say who did the action. Alternate translation: [The king’s scribes made a record …]

**Quote:** הֲ⁠לוֹא־הֵ֣ם כְּתוּבִ֗ים
Because it comes first logically, you can put this first in the verse, if it would be helpful in your language.

**Quote:** סֵ֨פֶר֙ דִּבְרֵ֣י הַ⁠יָּמִ֔ים לְ⁠מַלְכֵ֖י מָדַ֥י וּ⁠פָרָֽס
**Support:** rc://*/ta/man/translate/figs-idiom
See how you translated this in [2:23](../02/23.md). Alternate translation: [the royal record books of Media and Persia]

## Esther 10:3

**Quote:** כִּ֣י
**Support:** rc://*/ta/man/translate/grammar-connect-logic-result
This word indicates that this verse will give the reason why the scribes made a record about Mordecai.

**Quote:** הַ⁠יְּהוּדִ֗י
**Support:** rc://*/ta/man/translate/figs-distinguish
This phrase gives clarifying information about Mordecai.

**Quote:** מִשְׁנֶה֙ לַ⁠מֶּ֣לֶךְ אֲחַשְׁוֵר֔וֹשׁ
Alternate translation: [was the second most important person after King Ahasuerus himself]

**Quote:** וְ⁠גָדוֹל֙ לַ⁠יְּהוּדִ֔ים
Alternate translation: [and a great leader of the Jews]

**Quote:** אֶחָ֑י⁠ו
**Support:** rc://*/ta/man/translate/figs-gendernotations
Here, **brothers** is a figurative way of describing fellow members of the same people group. Alternate translation: [fellow Jews]

**Quote:** דֹּרֵ֥שׁ טוֹב֙ לְ⁠עַמּ֔⁠וֹ וְ⁠דֹבֵ֥ר שָׁל֖וֹם לְ⁠כָל־זַרְעֽ⁠וֹ
**Support:** rc://*/ta/man/translate/figs-parallelism
These two phrases basically mean the same thing. The repetition is used to emphasize how hard Mordecai worked for the good of his people. If it would be helpful in your language, you could combine these phrases and say something like, “He worked hard so his people and their descendants would prosper.”

**Quote:** דֹּרֵ֥שׁ טוֹב֙ לְ⁠עַמּ֔⁠וֹ וְ⁠דֹבֵ֥ר שָׁל֖וֹם לְ⁠כָל־זַרְעֽ⁠וֹ
**Support:** rc://*/ta/man/translate/figs-abstractnouns
The abstract nouns **good** and **peace** refer in this context to prosperity and security. You could translate these ideas with verbs, for example, you could say, “He worked hard to make sure that his people would prosper and their descendants would be secure.”

**Quote:** דֹּרֵ֥שׁ טוֹב֙ לְ⁠עַמּ֔⁠וֹ
**Support:** rc://*/ta/man/translate/figs-metaphor
**Seeking** is a figurative way to describe actively trying to do something or work hard for something.

**Quote:** וְ⁠דֹבֵ֥ר שָׁל֖וֹם לְ⁠כָל־זַרְעֽ⁠וֹ
**Support:** rc://*/ta/man/translate/figs-idiom
**Speaking peace** is a figurative way of describing actions that benefit the general welfare of others.

**Quote:** לְ⁠כָל־זַרְעֽ⁠וֹ
**Support:** rc://*/ta/man/translate/figs-metaphor
As in [verses 27](../10/27.md) and [28](../10/28.md), **seed** means “descendants.” **seed** means “descendants.” Even if you combine the two parallel phrases, you can still convey the idea of “down through the generations” with a word such as “always.” Alternate translation: [to all of their descendants]
