# 2 Samuel 8 Introduction

## Structure and Formatting

This chapter continues the story of what David did as the king of Israel. It describes how he defeated the enemy nations that surrounded Israel and took great plunder from them, bringing safety and prosperity to the Israelites. It then gives some details of his government administration.

If you chose to present the lists in [3:2–5](../03/02.md) and [5:13–16](../05/13.md) as the lists would appear in your culture, you should present the list in [8:15–18](../08/15.md) in the same way. For example, you might put [verse 15](../08/15.md) on a separate line as a summary of David’s government. You might then put the name of each official on a separate line, like this:
And David was king over all of Israel. And it happened, David did justice and righteousness for all of his people.
Joab, the son of Zeruiah, was over the army
Jehoshapat, the son of Ahilud, was the recorder.
Zadok, the son of Ahitub, and Ahimelech, the son of Abiathar, were priests
Seraiah was the scribe.
Benaiah, the son of Jehoiada, was over both the Kerethites and the Pelethites.
The sons of David were priests.

## Translation Issues in This Chapter

### Numbers

There are large numbers in [verses 4](../08/04.md), [5](../08/05.md), and [13](../08/13.md). Decide how you will represent large numbers in your translation.

### “struck”

In several places in this chapter, the author says that David “struck” an opposing army. He means that David and his army defeated those opponents in battle.

### “to restore his hand at the River”

In [8:3](../08/03.md), the author says that Hadadezer, the ruler of the Syrian kingdom of Zobah, “went to restore his hand at the River.” Interpreters understand this expression in two main ways. (1) It could mean that Hadadezer led his army against the Israelites in order to ensure that he continued to control his own territory. The kingdom of Zobah lay between the Euphrates River on the east and Israel on the west. Hadadezer may have seen how David had conquered and subdued the Philistines and Moabites, and he may have launched a pre-emptive attack to keep David from conquering him as well. The author may be saying that Hadadezer felt that his authority and control over his territory (his “hand”) had been diminished by David’s military successes against other nations, and so he marched his army out to “restore” it. If you follow this interpretation, you might say in your translation that Hadadezer went out “to reassert his control over his territory, from its border with Israel all the way to the Euphrates River.” (2) The expression could also mean that Hadadezer had built a monument to himself at the Euphrates River, asserting his control over the territory west of the river, and that he led his army out to “restore” the monument in order to reassert that control. The term “hand” means “monument” in [1 Samuel 15:12](../../1sa/15/12.md), and that could also be the meaning here. However, in this interpretation Hadadezer and his army would have been marching away from Israel and would not necessarily have come into conflict with David. The further implication would have to be that David learned of this action by a hostile king to reassert control over his territory, and so he led the Israelite army against him to subdue him. If you follow this interpretation, you might say in your translation that Hadadezer went “to repair his monument at the Euphrates River.”

## Textual Issues in This Chapter

### Betah

In [8:8](../08/08.md), the author refers to a city by the name of **Betah**. However, in the parallel passage in [1 Chronicles 18:8](../../1ch/18/08.md), it is called Tibhath. It appears possible that at some point in the process of transmission, scribes transposed two of the letters in this name. However, this is not certain, and it is not clear which may have been the original form, so we do not recommend that you change the name either here or in 1 Chronicles so that it is the same in both places. Instead, if it would be helpful to your readers, you may wish to note the difference in a footnote.

**Quote:** דָּוִ֛ד
**Support:** rc://*/ta/man/translate/figs-synecdoche
Here and frequently in this chapter, the author uses **David**, one person who was involved in these actions, to represent everyone who was involved. See how you translated the similar expression in [3:1](../03/01.md), where “David” means “David and his supporters.” Alternate translation: [David and his army]

**Quote:** אֶת־מֶ֥תֶג הָ⁠אַמָּ֖ה
**Support:** rc://*/ta/man/translate/translate-names
The expression **Metheg Ammah** seems to be a description rather than the name of a place. No place is known by that name. The parallel passage in [1 Chronicles 18:1](../../1ch/18/01.md) says that David captured the city of Gath and its surrounding towns. **Metheg Ammah** means “the bridle of the mother city.” Gath was the capital city of the Philistine confederation, and the author may speak of it here as a “bridle” to mean that conquering it gave David the means to control the entire Philistine confederation. (The word “bridle” could also indicate that previously the Philistines had exercised control over the Israelites from their capital city of Gath.) In your translation, you may wish to represent the expression “Metheg Ammah” and spell it the way it sounds in your language. Alternatively, you could express the probable meaning and translate the expression as a reference to Gath. If a translation of the Bible exists in your region, you may wish to take the approach that it takes. Alternate translation: [Gath, their capital city]

**Quote:** מִ⁠יַּ֥ד פְּלִשְׁתִּֽים
**Support:** rc://*/ta/man/translate/figs-metonymy
Here, **hand** represents possession or control. If it would be helpful in your language, you could use an equivalent expression or state the meaning plainly. Alternate translation: [from the possession of the Philistines]

## 2 Samuel 8:4

**Quote:** אֶ֤לֶף וּ⁠שְׁבַע־מֵאוֹת֙ פָּרָשִׁ֔ים

At one point in the process of transmission, scribes may have accidentally left out the word “chariots,” and the letters or points that indicated numbers may also have been unintentionally altered. If a translation of the Bible exists in your region, you may wish to use the reading that it uses. If a translation of the Bible does not exist in your region, you may wish to use the reading of the ULT.

**Quote:** וַ⁠יְעַקֵּ֤ר דָּוִד֙ אֶת־כָּל־הָ⁠רֶ֔כֶב וַ⁠יּוֹתֵ֥ר מִמֶּ֖⁠נּוּ מֵ֥אָה רָֽכֶב
**Support:** rc://*/ta/man/translate/figs-metonymy
Here the term **chariots** represents the horses that pulled these chariots. If it would be helpful in your language, you could use an equivalent expression or plain language. Alternate translation: [And David hamstrung all of the chariot horses, but he left from them 100 horses]

**Quote:** וַ⁠יְעַקֵּ֤ר דָּוִד֙ אֶת־כָּל־הָ⁠רֶ֔כֶב וַ⁠יּוֹתֵ֥ר מִמֶּ֖⁠נּוּ מֵ֥אָה רָֽכֶב
**Support:** rc://*/ta/man/translate/grammar-connect-exceptions

Alternate translation: [And after choosing 100 horses from the chariots, David had his soldiers hamstring the rest]

**Quote:** וַ⁠יְעַקֵּ֤ר דָּוִד֙ אֶת־כָּל־הָ⁠רֶ֔כֶב
**Support:** rc://*/ta/man/translate/translate-unknown
When the author says that David **hamstrung** these horses, he meant that he had his soldiers use a method of disabling them by cutting tendons in their legs. This would be done carefully so that horses would still be able to walk and carry loads, but they would no longer be able to run. As a result, they could no longer be used in warfare. If your readers would not be familiar with what hamstringing is, you could use a more general term. Alternate translation: [And David had his soldiers disable all of the chariot horses by cutting their leg tendons]

## 2 Samuel 8:7

**Quote:** אֵ֚ת שִׁלְטֵ֣י הַ⁠זָּהָ֔ב אֲשֶׁ֣ר הָי֔וּ אֶ֖ל עַבְדֵ֣י הֲדַדְעָ֑זֶר
**Support:** rc://*/ta/man/translate/figs-explicit
The meaning of the word translated as **shields** is uncertain. It could be a general term for military equipment whose meaning must be understood from the context. In some contexts, such as [Jeremiah 51:11](../../jer/51/11.md), it seems to mean “quivers” or arrow-cases, and some versions also translate it that way here. However, the context seems to support the meaning of **shields**. Some ancient rulers had their bodyguard carry shields that were plated with precious metals, and Hadadezer may have done that. Alternate translation: [the gold-plated shields that Hadadezer’s bodyguard had carried]
