# Introduction to 2 Samuel

### What is the book of 2 Samuel about?

This book is about David ruling as king over Israel. He first ruled over the tribe of Judah for seven and a half years. Then the other 11 tribes agreed to have him become their king. He ruled over all Israel for 33 years.

Throughout most of this time, David led his army to fight foreign enemies who attacked Israel. However, he struggled the most against someone in his own family. Absalom, his son, rebelled and fought against him.

The book of 2 Samuel also tells about when David sinned greatly. He made Bathsheba, the wife of Uriah the Hittite, have sexual relations with him. When she became pregnant, David arranged for Uriah to be killed in battle. David and all Israel suffered much because of David’s sin. However, the writer also tells how Yahweh repeatedly showed David mercy after David repented of his sin.

### What is the purpose of the book of 2 Samuel?

The book of 2 Samuel continues to tell the story of the Israelites once they had kings. It is part of a long history that also includes the preceding book of 1 Samuel and the following books of 1 Kings and 2 Kings. The entire history shows that Yahweh was not at fault for the Israelites being conquered and exiled from their land. Instead, this happened because the people disobeyed Yahweh and broke their covenant with him.

### Who wrote the book of 2 Samuel?

The author or authors who wrote 2 Samuel do not identify themselves by name in the book. However, many interpreters believe that the prophets in Israel recorded the events in this book, as well as in the entire history of which it is a part, in order to demonstrate that the people had disobeyed Yahweh and to encourage them to obey Yahweh once more so that they could be restored to their land.

### What title should translators use for this book?

Translators can use the traditional title 2 Samuel or Second Samuel. Translators may also consider a different title such as “The Book about King David’s Reign.”

### Can I translate this book before 1 Samuel?

The book of 1 Samuel should be translated before 2 Samuel, since 2 Samuel continues from where 1 Samuel ends.

## Part 2: Important Religious and Cultural Concepts

### What covenant had its beginning in the book of 2 Samuel?

In [2 Samuel 7](../07/01.md), Yahweh made a covenant with David. God promised to make a descendant of David rule forever over Israel. Christians believe that Jesus Christ fulfills this covenant. Jesus was a descendant of David. Jesus is the true “anointed one,” the person God chose to be king forever. (See: [[rc://*/tw/dict/bible/kt/covenant]])

## Part 3: Translation Issues in This Book

### “And it happened”

The author often uses the phrase “And it happened” to introduce a new event in the story or a new development within an episode. To translate this phrase in its various occurrences, use a word, phrase, or other method in your language that is natural for introducing a new event or development. (See: [[rc://*/ta/man/translate/writing-newevent]])

### “behold”

The author and characters often use the word “behold” to focus their listeners’ attention on what they are about to say. While the word literally means “look,” the speakers do not want their listeners to look at something, but to pay careful attention. For example, the author says “behold, a man came from the camp” in [1:2](../01/02.md) in order to call the readers’ attention to this new development in the story. Characters may use the equivalent expression “look,” which similarly means “pay attention.” For example, David says “Look” to Nathan in [7:2](../07/02.md) to mean “Listen.” (See: [[rc://*/ta/man/translate/figs-metaphor]])

### “to this day”

In [2 Samuel 4:3](../04/03.md), [6:8](../06/08.md), and [7:6](../07/06.md), and [18:18](../18/18.md), the author uses the phrase “to this day.” By that phrase, he is referring to the time at which he was writing. The translator should be aware that “to this day” refers to a time that is now in the past. He must avoid giving the impression that “to this day” means the present day of the translation’s readers. The translator might decide to say “at the time when this is being written” or “at the time of this writing.”

### “in the eyes of”

The author and characters often speak of something being good or bad “in the eyes of” someone. This means that that person considered this to be a good or bad thing to do. For example, the author says in [3:18](../03/18.md) that Abner went to speak to David about “all that {had been} good in the eyes of Israel and in the eyes of all of the house of Benjamin.” He means that Abner went to tell David that all of the Israelite tribes, including Benjamin (Saul’s tribe) considered it right to make David the next king. (See: [[rc://*/ta/man/translate/figs-idiom]])

### “to the face of”

The expression “to the face of” can have different shades of meaning, depending on the context.
  * The author and characters often use the word “face” to represent the presence of a person or group, by association with the way people can see the face of someone who is present. For example, David tells Abner in [3:13](../03/13.md), “You will not see my face unless you bring Michal,” meaning, “You will not come into my presence unless you bring Michal.”
  * The focus may also be on something being in front of or before a person or thing, either in space or in time. For example, David says in [3:35](../03/35.md) that he will not eat “to the face of the going of the sun.” He means that he will not eat before a certain time, sundown.
  * Sometimes the idea of “in front of” has a further association. For example, the author says in [10:16](../10/16.md) that when the Arameans gathered a large army, “Shobach, the commander of the army of Hadadezer, was to their faces.” By saying that Shobach was in front of these troops, he means that Shobach was commanding them.
  * The focus may also be on the relationship or interaction between people or groups, as in [2:17](../02/17.md), where the author says that “Abner and the men of Israel were struck to the face of the servants of David.” He means that they were defeated in front of David’s soldiers, that is, they were defeated as they opposed David’s soldiers. In other words, David’s soldiers defeated them in battle. (See: [[rc://*/ta/man/translate/figs-metonymy]])

In other contexts, the author or a character may use the word “face,” describing part of a person, in order to represent the whole person. For example, in [2 Samuel 15:14](../../2sa/15/14.md), David tells his officials that they must all flee “from the face of Absalom.” He is using one part of Absalom, his “face,” to mean all of him in his determination to kill David. (See: [[rc://*/ta/man/translate/figs-synecdoche]])

### “into the hand of” or “into your hand”

The author and characters often use the word “hand” by association to represent power, capability, or control. For example, when Abner tells Ishbosheth in [3:8](../03/08.md), “I have not delivered you into the hand of David,” he means that he has not allowed David to overpower Ishbosheth and replace him as king.

The word “hand” may also represent possession or conveyance, as when the author says in [8:10](../08/10.md) that a king sent an ambassador to David and “in his hand were vessels of silver and vessels of gold and vessels of bronze.” The word “hand” may also represent agency, as when Abner remarks in [3:18](../03/18.md) that Yahweh had said, “By the hand of David my servant, I will save my people Israel.” Translation notes will help distinguish the difference between these shades of meaning when that may not be clear from the context. (See: [[rc://*/ta/man/translate/figs-metonymy]])

In other contexts, the author or a character may use the word “hand,” describing part of a person, in order to represent the whole person. For example, in [2 Samuel 12:25](../12/25.md), the author says that Yahweh “sent by the hand of Nathan the prophet.” He is using one part of Nathan, his “hand,” to mean all of him in the act of bringing a message from Yahweh, as if he were carrying that message in his hand. (See: [[rc://*/ta/man/translate/figs-synecdoche]])

### “listen”

The author and characters often use the terms “listen” or “hear” to mean complying with what someone has said. For example, David’s servants say in [12:18](../12/18.md), “he did not listen to our voice,” meaning that David was not willing to do what they were urging him to do. (See: [[rc://*/ta/man/translate/figs-explicit]])

### name of a person representing him and associated or accompanying people

The author and characters often use the name of a person to represent that person and others who are associated with or accompanying that person. For example, in [1:1](../01/01.md), when the author says that “David returned from striking the Amalekites,” he is using David, one person who was involved in this action, to represent everyone who accompanied him. The text makes clear that David’s men returned with him. So the author means that David and his men returned together to Ziklag. (See: [[rc://*/ta/man/translate/figs-synecdoche]])

### name of nation, tribe, or city representing its people

The author and characters often use the name of a nation, tribe, or city by association to represent its people or its army. For example, in [2:28](../02/28.md), when the author says that Joab’s soldiers “did not still pursue after Israel,” he is using the name of the nation of Israel to refer by association to soldiers from the tribes of Israel other than Judah. When a nation has the same name as its ancestor, this is the same thing as using the name of the ancestor by association to mean his descendants. (See: [[rc://*/ta/man/translate/figs-metonymy]])

### “saying”

The author frequently uses the term “saying” to introduce a direct quotation. In your translation, use a natural way of introducing direct quotations in your language. It may not be necessary to represent this term explicitly. (See: [[rc://*/ta/man/translate/writing-quotations]])

### “sons of”

The author and characters often speak of “the sons of” a person to mean the nation or tribe that consists of his descendants. For example, in [7:6](../07/06.md) Yahweh speaks of the “sons of Israel” to mean the Israelites. In [12:12](../12/12.md), Samuel speaks of the “sons of Ammon” to mean the Ammonites. In this case, “sons” does not mean first-generation offspring but later descendants. (See: [[rc://*/ta/man/translate/figs-metaphor]])

### “strike” and “struck”

In many places, the author says that soldiers “struck” an opposing army, city, or king as if they had only hit them. But in various contexts, he means that they either attacked, defeated, or killed those opponents. For example, when the author says in [8:1](../08/01.md) that “David struck the Philistines, and he subdued them,” he means that David attacked them in order to keep them from being a continual threat to Israel. When he says in [2:17](../02/17.md) that “Abner and the men of Israel were struck to the face of the servants of David,” he means that David’s soldiers defeated Abner and the Israelite soldiers. And when he says in [2:31](../02/31.md) that “the servants of David had struck from Benjamin and among the men of Abner 360 men,” he means that they had killed those men. Consider carefully how to translate the term “struck” in each context where it does not actually mean “hit.” (See: [[rc://*/ta/man/translate/figs-explicit]])

### travel descriptions marked for change in elevation (“went up,” “went down”)

In Hebrew, writers and speakers generally indicated whether people were going up to a higher elevation or going down to a lower elevation when they traveled. For example, in [2:1](../02/01.md) David asks Yahweh, “Shall I go up into one of the cities of Judah?” David says “go up” because he is in Ziklag, near the seacoast, so he would be going up in elevation if he went to a city in the hilly territory of Judah. If your language does not mark travel for change in elevation, you can translate instances such as these simply as “went” or “go.” (See: [[rc://*/ta/man/translate/figs-explicitinfo]])
