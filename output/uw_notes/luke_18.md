# Luke 18 Chapter Introduction

## Structure and Formatting

1. Jesus tells a parable about a widow and a judge ([18:1–8](../18/01.md))
2. Jesus tells a parable about a Pharisee and a tax collector ([18:9–14](../18/09.md))
3. Jesus blesses little children ([18:15–17](../18/15.md))
4. Jesus teaches about wealth and the kingdom of God ([18:18–30](../18/18.md))
5. Jesus warns about his impending death ([18:31–34](../18/31.md))
6. Jesus heals a blind man in Jericho ([18:35–43](../18/35.md))

## Religious and Cultural Concepts in This Chapter

### Judges

People expected judges always to do what God said was right and to make sure that other people did what was right. But some judges did not care about doing right or making sure others did right. Jesus called this kind of judge “unjust.” (See: [[rc://*/tw/dict/bible/kt/justice]])

### Pharisees and tax collectors

The Pharisees thought that they themselves were the best examples of good, righteous people, and they thought that tax collectors were the most unrighteous of sinners. (See: [[rc://*/tw/dict/bible/kt/righteous]] and [[rc://*/tw/dict/bible/kt/sin]])

## Textual Issues in This Chapter

### “that he had become sad”

At the beginning of [18:24](../18/24.md), in the story of the ruler who asked Jesus how he could have eternal life, some ancient manuscripts of the Bible say that Jesus saw “that he had become sad.” However, the ancient manuscripts considered to be the most accurate do not have that phrase. They say simply that Jesus looked at him. ULT does not have the phrase in its text, but it does have it in a footnote.

### “we have left everything”

In [18:28](../18/28.md), in some ancient manuscripts of the Bible, Peter says that the disciples have left “everything” to follow Jesus. In other manuscripts, the expression is “our own possessions.” ULT says “everything” in its text, but it acknowledges the variant “our own possessions” in a footnote.

In each of these cases, if a translation of the Bible exists in your region, you may wish to follow its reading. If a translation of the Bible does not exist in your region, you may wish to follow the example of ULT. (See: [[rc://*/ta/man/translate/translate-textvariants]])

## Luke 18:3

**Quote:** χήρα δὲ ἦν ἐν τῇ πόλει ἐκείνῃ
**Support:** rc://*/ta/man/translate/writing-participants
Jesus uses this phrase to introduce the other main character into the story. Alternate translation: [There was also a woman who lived in that city whose husband had died]

**Quote:** χήρα
**Support:** rc://*/ta/man/translate/figs-explicit
A widow is a woman whose husband has died and who has not remarried. Jesus assumes that his disciples will know that in this culture, she would have had no one to protect her from anyone who was trying to take advantage of her. If it would be helpful to your readers, you could state that explicitly. Alternate translation: [a woman whose husband had died, and so she had no one to protect her]

**Quote:** ἤρχετο πρὸς αὐτὸν
Here the Greek verb indicates repeated or continual action. Alternate translation: [she kept coming to the judge]

**Quote:** λέγουσα, ἐκδίκησόν με ἀπὸ τοῦ ἀντιδίκου μου
**Support:** rc://*/ta/man/translate/figs-quotesinquotes
If it would be helpful in your language, you could translate this so that there is not a quotation within a quotation. Alternate translation: [and asking him to make a fair ruling in her case against her opponent]

**Quote:** ἐκδίκησόν με ἀπὸ τοῦ ἀντιδίκου μου
**Support:** rc://*/ta/man/translate/figs-imperative
This is an imperative, but since the woman is not in a position to make a demand, it should be translated as a polite request rather than as a command. It may be helpful to add an expression such as “please” to make this clear. Alternate translation: [Please give me a fair ruling in my case against my opponent]

**Quote:** τοῦ ἀντιδίκου μου
**Support:** rc://*/ta/man/translate/translate-unknown
The term **opponent** refers specifically to the opposing party in a lawsuit. It is not clear whether the widow is suing the man to protect her interests, or the man is suing the widow to try to take things from her unfairly. If your language has a specific term for a legal adversary, you could use it here.

## Luke 18:7

**Quote:** ὁ δὲ Θεὸς οὐ μὴ ποιήσῃ τὴν ἐκδίκησιν τῶν ἐκλεκτῶν αὐτοῦ, τῶν βοώντων αὐτῷ ἡμέρας καὶ νυκτός, καὶ μακροθυμεῖ ἐπ’ αὐτοῖς

**Support:** rc://*/ta/man/translate/figs-merism

## Luke 18:8

**Quote:** λέγω ὑμῖν
Jesus says this to emphasize what he is about to tell his disciples. Alternate translation: [I can assure you]

**Quote:** ποιήσει τὴν ἐκδίκησιν αὐτῶν ἐν τάχει

Alternate translation: [he will start to answer their prayers right away] or [he will begin to send them help right away]

**Quote:** ὁ Υἱὸς τοῦ Ἀνθρώπου ἐλθὼν
**Support:** rc://*/ta/man/translate/figs-explicit
