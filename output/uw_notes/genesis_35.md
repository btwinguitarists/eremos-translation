## Genesis 35:1

**Quote:** וַ⁠יֹּ֤אמֶר אֱלֹהִים֙ אֶֽל־יַעֲקֹ֔ב
**Support:** rc://*/ta/man/translate/writing-quotations
Translate this quote margin in a way that is natural in your language and that fits with what God says. Alternate translation: [Then God told Jacob]

**Quote:** ק֛וּם
Alternate translation: [Get packed]

**Quote:** עֲלֵ֥ה בֵֽית־אֵ֖ל וְ⁠שֶׁב־שָׁ֑ם
The verb **go up** reflects the fact that the town of Bethel is higher in elevation than the city of Shechem; it does not refer to the direction they were going, which was south. Many translations leave **up** implied (to prevent confusion). Alternate translation: [and travel to the town of Bethel and live there]

**Quote:** וַ⁠עֲשֵׂה־שָׁ֣ם מִזְבֵּ֔חַ
**Support:** rc://*/ta/man/translate/figs-explicit
See how you translated **altar** in Genesis [33:20](../33/20.md).

**Quote:** לָ⁠אֵל֙
**Support:** rc://*/ta/man/translate/figs-explicit
Make sure it is clear in your translation that God is referring to himself as he speaks here to Jacob. Alternate translation: [to me as the God] or [to me, the God]

**Quote:** הַ⁠נִּרְאֶ֣ה אֵלֶ֔י⁠ךָ
See how you translated **appeared** in Genesis [26:2](../26/02.md), [24](../26/24.md). Alternate translation: [who came to you there] or [who introduced myself to you there]

**Quote:** בְּ⁠בָרְחֲ⁠ךָ֔
See how you translated “flee” in Genesis [31:20–21](../31/20.md), [27](../31/27.md).

**Quote:** מִ⁠פְּנֵ֖י עֵשָׂ֥ו אָחִֽי⁠ךָ
Alternate translation: [from the anger of your brother Esau]

## Genesis 35:2

**Quote:** וַ⁠יֹּ֤אמֶר יַעֲקֹב֙ אֶל־בֵּית֔⁠וֹ
Alternate translation: [So Jacob told the members of his family]

**Quote:** וְ⁠אֶ֖ל כָּל־אֲשֶׁ֣ר עִמּ֑⁠וֹ
This included Jacob’s servants and the people (women and children) whom his sons had just captured from the city of Shechem (Genesis [34:29](../34/29.md)). You could put some of that information in a footnote.

**Quote:** הָסִ֜רוּ אֶת־אֱלֹהֵ֤י הַ⁠נֵּכָר֙ אֲשֶׁ֣ר בְּ⁠תֹכְ⁠כֶ֔ם
**Support:** rc://*/ta/man/translate/figs-explicit
By **foreign gods**, Jacob means the idols that came to the Jews from the non-Hebrew people with whom the Jews had dwelt and even intermarried. You could indicate this explicitly in your translation if that would be helpful to your readers. See how you translated “idols” and **gods** in Genesis [31:19](../31/19.md), [30](../31/30.md), [32](../31/32.md). Alternate translation: [Get rid of the idols you have that pagan ethnic groups worship] or [Give up the idols that you have from foreigners]

**Quote:** וְ⁠הַחֲלִ֖יפוּ שִׂמְלֹתֵי⁠כֶֽם
Alternate translation: [and dress in clean clothes]

## Genesis 35:3

**Quote:** וְ⁠נָק֥וּמָה
See how you translated **get up** in [verse 1](../35/01.md). Alternate translation: [Then we must pack up our things] or [Then we must leave here]

**Quote:** וְ⁠נַעֲלֶ֖ה בֵּֽית־אֵ֑ל
See how you translated **go up** in [verse 1](../35/01.md). Alternate translation: [and go to the town of Bethel]

**Quote:** וְ⁠אֶֽעֱשֶׂה־שָּׁ֣ם מִזְבֵּ֗חַ לָ⁠אֵ֞ל
The Hebrew text has **I** here, but probably also implies that others in Jacob’s household would help him build the altar. Also see how you translated **make an altar** in [verse 1](../35/01.md). Alternate translation: [where I will build an altar to worship God] or [There I will build an altar and worship God]

**Quote:** הָ⁠עֹנֶ֤ה אֹתִ⁠י֙
Alternate translation: [because he answered me] or [who granted my prayers for help]

**Quote:** בְּ⁠י֣וֹם צָֽרָתִ֔⁠י
**Support:** rc://*/ta/man/translate/figs-idiom
The phrase “In the day of” is used here as an idiom that could refer to the time when Jacob was fleeing from Esau, or it could be more general and also include other times when Jacob was suffering, such as the years he worked for Laban. Alternate translation: [during the days when I was in trouble]

**Quote:** וַ⁠יְהִי֙ עִמָּדִ֔⁠י
**Support:** rc://*/ta/man/translate/figs-idiom
See how you translated the idiom **with me** in Genesis [31:5](../31/05.md), where it also means that God is not only present with him but also helps him and takes care of him. Alternate translation: [He has always stayed with me and taken care of me]

**Quote:** בַּ⁠דֶּ֖רֶךְ אֲשֶׁ֥ר הָלָֽכְתִּי
Alternate translation: [everywhere that I have gone]

## Genesis 35:4

**Quote:** וַ⁠יִּתְּנ֣וּ אֶֽל־יַעֲקֹ֗ב
Alternate translation: [Then they gave Jacob] or [Then they turned over to Jacob]

**Quote:** אֵ֣ת כָּל־אֱלֹהֵ֤י הַ⁠נֵּכָר֙ אֲשֶׁ֣ר בְּ⁠יָדָ֔⁠ם
See how you translated **foreign gods** in [verse 2](../35/02.md). You could translate this differently here since this is the second time they are mentioned in this paragraph. Alternate translation: [all the pagan gods that they had with them]

**Quote:** וְ⁠אֶת־הַ⁠נְּזָמִ֖ים אֲשֶׁ֣ר בְּ⁠אָזְנֵי⁠הֶ֑ם
**Support:** rc://*/ta/man/translate/figs-explicit
The implication is that these earrings were part of their idol worship and may have had images of their idols on them. You could include this information if that would be helpful to your readers. Alternate translation: [and the earrings they wore on their ears]

**Quote:** וַ⁠יִּטְמֹ֤ן אֹתָ⁠ם֙ יַעֲקֹ֔ב
Alternate translation: [Then Jacob hid everything in the ground] or [Then Jacob hid all those things in the ground]

**Quote:** תַּ֥חַת
Make sure it is clear in your translation that Jacob buried these things in the ground beside the tree, not directly under the trunk of the tree. Alternate translation: [beside] or [at the foot of]

**Quote:** הָ⁠אֵלָ֖ה
**Support:** rc://*/ta/man/translate/translate-unknown
Although this phrase refers to a terebinth tree, many translations have “oak tree” instead, or some other type of large, strong tree that is well known. See how you translated a different Hebrew word that is also translated **terebinth tree** or “oak tree” in Genesis [12:6](../12/06.md); [13:18](../13/18.md); [14:13](../14/13.md); [18:1](../18/01.md). Another Hebrew word in Genesis [35:8](../35/08.md) is also translated as “oak.” Alternate translation: [a oak tree]

## Genesis 35:5

**Quote:** וַ⁠יִּסָּ֑עוּ
Alternate translation: [Then they all left there and started traveling]

**Quote:** וַ⁠יְהִ֣י ׀ חִתַּ֣ת אֱלֹהִ֗ים עַל־הֶֽ⁠עָרִים֙ אֲשֶׁר֙ סְבִיבֹ֣תֵי⁠הֶ֔ם וְ⁠לֹ֣א
The Hebrew word “Elohim” probably means **God** in this phrase (as it usually does in the Bible); most English versions translate it that way here. Or it could be an adjective that modifies “dread/terror” and means “great/deep dread/terror” (similar to Genesis [23:6](../23/06.md), [30:8](../30/08.md)). See how you translated **dread** in Genesis [9:2](../09/02.md). Alternate translation: [and God caused the people in the nearby cities to be afraid, so that] or [and God made the people in the nearby cities so afraid that]

**Quote:** וְ⁠לֹ֣א רָֽדְפ֔וּ אַחֲרֵ֖י
Make sure your translation of **they** refers here to the people in the surrounding towns, not Jacob’s sons. Alternate translation: [no one attacked] or [none of them came after]

**Quote:** בְּנֵ֥י יַעֲקֹֽב
**Support:** rc://*/ta/man/translate/figs-metonymy
Jacob’s sons are mentioned here since they are in focus as the ones who just plundered the city of Shechem; Jacob and the rest of his family were there with his sons and were also in danger. Alternate translation: [them] or [Jacob and his family]

**Quote:** ה֖וּא וְ⁠כָל־הָ⁠עָ֥ם אֲשֶׁר־עִמּֽ⁠וֹ
**Support:** rc://*/ta/man/translate/figs-infostructure

## Genesis 35:8

**Quote:** וַ⁠תָּ֤מָת דְּבֹרָה֙ מֵינֶ֣קֶת רִבְקָ֔ה
See how you translated **nurse** in Genesis [24:59](../24/59.md).

**Quote:** וַ⁠תִּקָּבֵ֛ר
Alternate translation: [and her body was buried by Jacob in the ground] or [They buried her body in the ground]

**Quote:** מִ⁠תַּ֥חַת לְ⁠בֵֽית־אֵ֖ל
The town of Bethel was built on a hill, so the phrase **below Bethel** means that Deborah was buried near Bethel, in a valley or place that was lower in elevation than the town, and possibly to the south of it. Alternate translation: [in the valley below the town of Bethel] or [at a place south of the town of Bethel]

**Quote:** תַּ֣חַת הָֽ⁠אַלּ֑וֹן
**Support:** rc://*/ta/man/translate/figs-infostructure
See how you translated a similar phrase (“under the terebinth/oak tree”) in [verse 4](../35/04.md). Many translations translate this phrase the same way in both places (“under an oak tree”), even though different Hebrew words are used for the type of tree. Also, for some languages it is more natural to put this phrase before the previous phrase and say, “at the base of an oak tree, below/near the town of Bethel.” Alternate translation: [beside an oak tree] or [at the foot of an oak tree]

**Quote:** וַ⁠יִּקְרָ֥א שְׁמ֖⁠וֹ
**Support:** rc://*/ta/man/translate/translate-names
**So he called its name** could mean: (1) that Jacob was the one who named the place. See how you translated a similar clause in Genesis [31:38](../31/38.md). Alternate translation: [So he named the tree]; (2) that people in general called the tree or place Allon Bacuth. Alternate translation: [That is why the place is known as]

**Quote:** אַלּ֥וֹן בָּכֽוּת
**Support:** rc://*/ta/man/translate/translate-names
If you include the meaning of this name in your translation text or in a footnote, make sure it fits with how you translated **oak tree** in the previous sentence.

## Genesis 35:10

**Quote:** וַ⁠יֹּֽאמֶר־ל֥⁠וֹ אֱלֹהִ֖ים
**Support:** rc://*/ta/man/translate/writing-quotations
What God says to Jacob in [verses 10–12](../35/10.md) is the blessing. So make sure your translation of “blessed” ([verse 9](../35/09.md)) and **said** ([verse 10](../35/10.md)) refers to the same event, not two separate events. See how you translated “blessed … and said” in Genesis [1:28](../01/28.md). Alternate translation: [by saying to him]

**Quote:** שִׁמְ⁠ךָ֣ יַעֲקֹ֑ב
Make sure your translation of this clause does not sound like God was telling Jacob something he did not know.

**Quote:** לֹֽא־יִקָּרֵא֩ שִׁמְ⁠ךָ֨ ע֜וֹד יַעֲקֹ֗ב
**Support:** rc://*/ta/man/translate/translate-names
Notice that Jacob is referred to by both of his names in this chapter (**Jacob** in [verses 11–20](../35/11.md), [22–29](../35/22.md); **Israel** in [verses 10](../35/10.md) and [21](../35/21.md)). You could put that information in a footnote. Also see how you translated this clause in Genesis [32:28](../32/28.md), and see the note about Jacob’s names there. Alternate translation: [that will no longer be your only name] or [it will no longer be that name]

**Quote:** כִּ֤י אִם־יִשְׂרָאֵל֙ יִהְיֶ֣ה שְׁמֶ֔⁠ךָ
Alternate translation: [Rather, your name will also be Israel!] or [Rather, you will also have the name Israel!]

**Quote:** וַ⁠יִּקְרָ֥א אֶת־שְׁמ֖⁠וֹ יִשְׂרָאֵֽל
**Support:** rc://*/ta/man/translate/figs-explicit
This was not the first time God had given Jacob the name Israel (See: Genesis [32:28](../32/28.md)). To make that clear, you could make “once again” explicit here.

## Genesis 35:18

**Quote:** וַ⁠יְהִ֞י בְּ⁠צֵ֤את נַפְשָׁ⁠הּ֙ כִּ֣י מֵ֔תָה
Some translations use an idiom here, for example, “as she was taking her last breath” or “as her life was slipping/fading away,” but it is best to translate this in a way that preserves the biblical teaching that at death the soul or spirit leaves the body. Alternate translation: [But Rachel was dying, and as her soul was leaving her body] or [But in fact Rachel was about to die, and just before her soul left her]

**Quote:** וַ⁠תִּקְרָ֥א שְׁמ֖⁠וֹ בֶּן־אוֹנִ֑י
**Support:** rc://*/ta/man/translate/translate-names
The meaning of this name could imply that the child caused Rachel’s death. In the Hebrew text this name is two words connected with a dash (**Ben-oni**). Many translations write it that way or as one word (“Benoni”), and a few translations write it as “Ben Oni.” Any of these spellings is fine.

**Quote:** וְ⁠אָבִ֖י⁠ו
**Support:** rc://*/ta/man/translate/writing-pronouns
The pronoun **his** refers to Jacob. It may be helpful to clarify this for your readers.

**Quote:** קָֽרָא־ל֥⁠וֹ בִנְיָמִֽין
**Support:** rc://*/ta/man/translate/translate-names
The phrase “son of my right hand” is an idiom that means “favored son” or “son of strength.” Alternate translation: [named him Benjamin instead]

## Genesis 35:21

**Quote:** וַ⁠יִּסַּ֖ע יִשְׂרָאֵ֑ל
Make sure it is clear in your translation or in a footnote that **Israel** ([verses 21–22](../35/21.md)) and “Jacob” ([verses 20](../35/20.md), [22](../35/22.md)) refer to the same person, not two different people. Alternate translation: [Then Israel that is, Jacob, and his family moved on from there] or [Then Israel and his family started traveling again]

**Quote:** וַ⁠יֵּ֣ט אָֽהֳלֹ֔⁠ה
Alternate translation: [and put up his tents]

**Quote:** מֵ⁠הָ֖לְאָה
This phrase reflects the perspective of Jacob, who was traveling south. Alternate translation: [on the other side of]

**Quote:** לְ⁠מִגְדַּל־עֵֽדֶר
**Support:** rc://*/ta/man/translate/translate-names
Many translations transliterate **Migdal Eder** (like most other names). Many others translate the meaning of **Migdal** as “the tower” and transliterate “Eder.” A few translate the meaning of the entire name as “the Tower of the Flock.” This was apparently a place near Bethlehem where sheep were raised ([Micah 4:8](../../mic/04/08.md)); shepherds may have watched over their flocks from the tower. Alternate translation: [Eder Tower]
