## Chapter intro

# 2 Samuel 6 Introduction

## Structure and Formatting

This chapter continues the story of what David did as the king of Israel. It describes how he brought the Box of Yahweh to Jerusalem and placed it in an honored location in the fortress where he lived, the City of David.

## Religious and Cultural Concepts in This Chapter

### Why did David offer sacrifices after the people carrying the Box had taken six steps?

The author relates in [6:13](../06/13.md) that David had the people who were carrying the Box of Yahweh stop after taking six steps so that he could offer sacrifices. Some interpreters believe that this was a symbolic action that alluded to the commandment to work for six days and then observe a day of rest and worship. The parallel passage in [1 Chronicles 15:2–14](../15/02.md) tells how David specifically said how important it was for the Israelites to obey Yahweh’s instructions in the law. So this action could have been a symbolic affirmation of that. If it would be helpful to your readers, you could explain the significance of this action. However, since this interpretation is not entirely certain, you may wish to describe it in general terms. You might say, for example, that “when the lifters of the Box of Yahweh had stepped six steps, David had them pause reverently, and he worshiped Yahweh by sacrificing an ox and a fatling.”

## 2 Samuel 6:4

**Quote:** וַ⁠יִּשָּׂאֻ֗⁠הוּ מִ⁠בֵּ֤ית אֲבִֽינָדָב֙ אֲשֶׁ֣ר בַּ⁠גִּבְעָ֔ה
**Support:** rc://*/ta/man/translate/translate-textvariants
The Septuagint (an ancient Greek translation of the Hebrew Bible) and the parallel passage in 1 Chronicles do not have this sentence, which seems to have been repeated accidentally from the previous verse. Without it, the text of [verses 3–4](../06/03.md) would sensibly read, “And Uzzah and Ahio, the sons of Abinadab, were leading the new cart with the Box of God” (that is, the new cart that had the Box of God on it). Many versions of the Bible do not include this sentence. If a translation of the Bible exists in your region, you may wish to omit the sentence if it does.

**Quote:** וְ⁠אַחְי֕וֹ הֹלֵ֖ךְ לִ⁠פְנֵ֥י הָ⁠אָרֽוֹן
**Support:** rc://*/ta/man/translate/figs-explicit
The author assumes that his readers will know that Ahio and Uzzah were following the custom of this culture for steadying a load on a cart that animals were pulling. One person would walk ahead of the cart, leading and guiding the animals, while another person would walk next to the cart to make sure that the load did not fall off. By saying that **Ahio was walking to the face of the Box**, he also means that Uzzah was walking alongside the cart that was carrying it, as the story itself indicates in [6:6](../06/06.md). You could indicate this explicitly in your translation if that would be helpful to your readers. The UST models one way to do that.

**Quote:** לִ⁠פְנֵ֥י
**Support:** rc://*/ta/man/translate/figs-metonymy
Here, **to the face of** represents “in front of.” If it would be helpful in your language, you could use an equivalent expression or plain language. Alternate translation: [in front of]

**Quote:** הָ⁠אָרֽוֹן
**Support:** rc://*/ta/man/translate/figs-metonymy
Here, **the Box** represents the oxen that were pulling the cart that was carrying the Box. If it would be helpful in your language, you could use an equivalent expression or plain language. Alternate translation: [the oxen that were pulling the cart that was carrying the Box]
