## Genesis 19:1

**Quote:** וַ֠⁠יָּבֹאוּ שְׁנֵ֨י הַ⁠מַּלְאָכִ֤ים סְדֹ֨מָ⁠ה֙ בָּ⁠עֶ֔רֶב
See how you translated “angel” in Genesis [16:7–11](../16/07.md).

**Quote:** בְּ⁠שַֽׁעַר־סְדֹ֑ם
Cities like Sodom had stone walls built around them, with entrance gates (that were closed at night) to protect the people in the city from enemies. Alternate translation: [by the gate of the city] or [at the city gate]

**Quote:** וַ⁠יַּרְא־לוֹט֙ וַ⁠יָּ֣קָם
**Support:** rc://*/ta/man/translate/figs-youdual
Some languages have a special dual pronoun that can be used throughout [verses 1–19](../19/01.md) to refer to the two angels. Alternate translation: [When Lot saw them, he got up]

**Quote:** לִ⁠קְרָאתָ֔⁠ם
Alternate translation: [to greet them]

**Quote:** וַ⁠יִּשְׁתַּ֥חוּ אַפַּ֖יִם אָֽרְצָ⁠ה
**Support:** rc://*/ta/man/translate/translate-symaction
See how you translated a similar phrase in Genesis [18:2](../18/02.md). Alternate translation: [and then bowed before them with his forehead to the ground to show respect] or [and then kneeled in front of them and bowed his forehead to the ground to show them respect]

## Genesis 19:2

**Quote:** הִנֶּ֣ה נָּא־אֲדֹנַ֗⁠י
**Support:** rc://*/ta/man/translate/writing-politeness
See how you translated **Behold, please** in Genesis [18:27](../18/27.md). Also see how you translated “my lord” in Genesis [18:3](../18/03.md). At this point, Lot probably did not know that these were angels, because they looked like men. Alternate translation: [Listen to me please, my lords] or [Sirs please listen to this:]

**Quote:** ס֣וּרוּ נָ֠א אֶל
**Support:** rc://*/ta/man/translate/writing-politeness
Lot is being urgent here (using **please** twice), probably because he knows how dangerous the city is. Translate this quote in a way that communicates this urgency. Alternate translation: [Please stop in at]

**Quote:** בֵּ֨ית עַבְדְּ⁠כֶ֤ם וְ⁠לִ֨ינוּ֙
**Support:** rc://*/ta/man/translate/figs-123person
Lot refers to himself as **your servant** here to show respect to the two men and to politely offer to host them as his guests.

**Quote:** וְ⁠לִ֨ינוּ֙ וְ⁠רַחֲצ֣וּ רַגְלֵי⁠כֶ֔ם
See how you translated **wash your feet** in Genesis [18:4](../18/04.md). Alternate translation: [You can wash the dust off your feet and stay for the night there]

**Quote:** וְ⁠הִשְׁכַּמְתֶּ֖ם
Alternate translation: [Then early in the morning you can get up]

**Quote:** וַ⁠הֲלַכְתֶּ֣ם לְ⁠דַרְכְּ⁠כֶ֑ם
Alternate translation: [and continue on your way]

**Quote:** וַ⁠יֹּאמְר֣וּ
Alternate translation: [The two angels said to him]

**Quote:** לֹּ֔א כִּ֥י
**Support:** rc://*/ta/man/translate/writing-politeness
Abraham is responding to the angels with the courteous expression that a person in this culture would use to respond to a superior. Your language may have a comparable expression that you can use in your translation. The angels’ reply should sound polite and respectful in your translation, not rude or disrespectful. For example, in some cultures it is not polite to say **No** too directly. Alternate translation: [No, thank you, rather] or [That is kind of you, but]

**Quote:** בָ⁠רְח֖וֹב נָלִֽין
This refers to a public, outdoor place inside the city, perhaps the open place inside the city gate where people would meet. Alternate translation: [we will just stay in the city square tonight] or [we plan to stay the night in the square]

## Genesis 19:3

**Quote:** וַ⁠יִּפְצַר־בָּ֣⁠ם מְאֹ֔ד
Alternate translation: [But Lot insisted urgently] or [However he continued to urge them earnestly] or [However he continued to urgently invite them]

**Quote:** וַ⁠יָּסֻ֣רוּ אֵלָ֔י⁠ו
Alternate translation: [until he finally convinced them and they went with him]

**Quote:** וַ⁠יָּבֹ֖אוּ אֶל־בֵּית֑⁠וֹ
Alternate translation: [to his house and entered it]

**Quote:** וַ⁠יַּ֤עַשׂ לָ⁠הֶם֙ מִשְׁתֶּ֔ה
Lot probably had his wife or servants prepare the meal (as Abraham did in Genesis [18:6–7](../18/06.md)). It is common in the Bible to say that a leader or head of household (as here) did something, when in reality he had it done by someone else who was under his authority. Alternate translation: [Then he made a big meal for them] or [Then Lot had a big meal prepared for them]

**Quote:** וּ⁠מַצּ֥וֹת אָפָ֖ה
Alternate translation: [including some bread without yeast in it]

## Genesis 19:4

**Quote:** טֶרֶם֮ יִשְׁכָּבוּ֒
Alternate translation: [But after the meal, before they could lie down to sleep] or [After that, before they could go to bed for the night]

**Quote:** וְ⁠אַנְשֵׁ֨י הָ⁠עִ֜יר אַנְשֵׁ֤י סְדֹם֙
Alternate translation: [all the men of the city of Sodom]

**Quote:** מִ⁠נַּ֖עַר וְ⁠עַד־זָקֵ֑ן
**Support:** rc://*/ta/man/translate/figs-merism
The phrase **from the young and to the old** refers to all the men of the city of Sodom, including the young and the old and everyone in between. Consider what is the best way to translate this phrase in your language. Alternate translation: [from the youngest to the oldest]

**Quote:** כָּל־הָ⁠עָ֖ם מִ⁠קָּצֶֽה
**Support:** rc://*/ta/man/translate/figs-hyperbole
This phrase is generalization or exaggeration used to emphasize that a very large number of people from the city were there. Consider whether or not your language can do the same thing. Alternate translation: [In fact, all the people from every section of the city were there] or [so that people from the entire city were there] or [In fact, everyone from all parts of the city had come there]

## Genesis 19:5

**Quote:** וַ⁠יִּקְרְא֤וּ
**Support:** rc://*/ta/man/translate/writing-pronouns
Here the author is using the pronoun **they** in a general sense, not to refer to specific people. Use an expression for this that would be natural in your language. Alternate translation: [Then the mob of people called out] or [Then the men shouted]

**Quote:** אֲשֶׁר־בָּ֥אוּ אֵלֶ֖י⁠ךָ הַ⁠לָּ֑יְלָה
Alternate translation: [who came to your house tonight?]

**Quote:** הוֹצִיאֵ֣⁠ם אֵלֵ֔י⁠נוּ
Alternate translation: [Bring them to us out here] or [We demand that you bring them out here to us]

**Quote:** וְ⁠נֵדְעָ֖ה אֹתָֽ⁠ם
**Support:** rc://*/ta/man/translate/figs-euphemism
In Hebrew the verb **know** is often used as an idiom that means “have sex with” (as in Genesis [4:1](../04/01.md), [25](../04/25.md)). Here it refers to homosexual activities and raping the two men. Translate this in a way that is not too offensive to be read aloud in public. Alternate translation: [so that we can have sex with them!]

## Genesis 19:7

**Quote:** וַ⁠יֹּאמַ֑ר
**Support:** rc://*/ta/man/translate/writing-quotations
Alternate translation: [Then he said to them] or [and he pleaded with them]

**Quote:** נָ֥א אַחַ֖⁠י
**Support:** rc://*/ta/man/translate/writing-politeness
The phrase **my brothers** is used here as a polite address that implies a close relationship. Lot uses this common expression to try to get the men of Sodom to not harm his guests. The men were not his actual brothers. For some languages it is more natural to put **my brothers** (or “my friends”) first in this quote. Alternate translation: [No, please, my friends] or [I beg you, my brothers] or [No, my brothers please]

**Quote:** אַל & תָּרֵֽעוּ
Alternate translation: [do not do this evil!] or [do not do such an wicked thing!]
