## Chapter intro

# Psalm 11 Introduction

## Structure and Formatting

Psalm 11 is considered to be one of the “psalms of trust.” (See the Introduction to Psalms.) He will judge the wicked and reward the righteous. (See:[[rc://*/tw/dict/bible/other/deliverer]] and [[rc://*/tw/dict/bible/kt/good]] and [[rc://*/tw/dict/bible/kt/evil]])

  1. David asserts his trust in God (1a)
  2. The words of David’s advisors (1b–3)
  3. David’s response to the words of his advisors (4–5)
  4. David asks God to judge righteously (6–7)

## Religious and Cultural Concepts in This Psalm

### Justice

David says that wicked people try to destroy righteous people, but God knows everything that evil people try to do. David says that “Yahweh is righteous” (7) and “he hates those who love to do violence” (5). David asks God to judge the wicked (6) and says that “the upright” will be rewarded by seeing his face (7). (See: [[rc://*/tw/dict/bible/kt/justice]] and [[rc://*/tw/dict/bible/kt/save]])

## Psalms 11:1

**Quote:** לַ⁠מְנַצֵּ֗חַ
**Support:** rc://*/ta/man/translate/translate-unknown
The word translated as **chief musician** likely refers to the person in charge of music for worship. Some languages may have a term for a music leader. Alternate translation: [For the music director] or [For the leader of worship music]

**Quote:** בַּֽ⁠יהוָ֨ה ׀ חָסִ֗יתִי
**Support:** rc://*/ta/man/translate/figs-metaphor
Here David is speaking as if Yahweh were actually a physical place of safety or shelter. Your language may have a comparable expression that you can use in your translation, or you could state the meaning plainly. Alternate translation: [I trust in Yahweh to keep me safe] or [I depend on Yahweh to protect me]

**Quote:** אֵ֭יךְ תֹּאמְר֣וּ לְ⁠נַפְשִׁ֑⁠י
**Support:** rc://*/ta/man/translate/figs-rquestion
David is using the question form to challenge what people are telling him. His question continues from this verse to the end of verse 3. If a speaker of your language would not use the question form for that purpose, you could translate this as a statement or as an exclamation. Alternate translation: [You should not say to my soul]

**Quote:** תֹּאמְר֣וּ לְ⁠נַפְשִׁ֑⁠י
**Support:** rc://*/ta/man/translate/figs-synecdoche
The author is using **my soul** to represent all of himself. If it would be helpful in your language, you could use an equivalent expression from your culture or state the meaning plainly. Alternate translation: [can you say to me]

**Quote:** נ֝֗וּדִי הַרְ⁠כֶ֥ם צִפּֽוֹר
**Support:** rc://*/ta/man/translate/figs-metaphor
Here the speaker’s advisors are speaking of him as if he were actually a **bird** that should fly away to safety in the mountains. Your language may have a comparable expression that you can use in your translation, or you could state the meaning plainly. Alternate translation: [Run away and hide in the mountains] or [Escape to safety in the mountains]

## Psalms 11:4

**Quote:** יְהוָ֤ה ׀ בְּֽ⁠הֵ֘יכַ֤ל קָדְשׁ֗⁠וֹ יְהוָה֮ בַּ⁠שָּׁמַ֪יִם כִּ֫סְא֥⁠וֹ
**Support:** rc://*/ta/man/translate/figs-metaphor
Here the author is speaking of God’s presence in heaven as if he were actually in a **temple** and as if **the heavens** were a place with a **throne**. You could translate the words literally or your language may have a comparable expression that you can use in your translation. Alternate translation: [Yahweh is present in his holy sanctuary and rules as king from heaven]

**Quote:** יְהוָ֤ה ׀ בְּֽ⁠הֵ֘יכַ֤ל קָדְשׁ֗⁠וֹ יְהוָה֮ בַּ⁠שָּׁמַ֪יִם כִּ֫סְא֥⁠וֹ
**Support:** rc://*/ta/man/translate/figs-parallelism
These two phrases mean basically the same thing. The second emphasizes the meaning of the first by repeating the same idea with different words. Hebrew poetry was based on this kind of repetition, and it would be good to show this to your readers by including both phrases in your translation, if possible. If it is helpful, you could connect the phrases in a way that shows that the second phrase is repeating the first one, not saying something additional. Alternate translation: [Yahweh is in his holy temple; yes, Yahweh is in the heavens, his throne] or [Yahweh is in his holy temple; indeed, Yahweh is in the heavens, his throne]

**Quote:** יְהוָה֮ בַּ⁠שָּׁמַ֪יִם כִּ֫סְא֥⁠וֹ
**Support:** rc://*/ta/man/translate/figs-distinguish
The author is not distinguishing this **throne** from another throne that is not in heaven. The author is showing God’s greatness compared to human rulers who rule from a throne on earth, by adding that **the heavens** are the place of God’s **throne** (where he rules from). Be sure that this is clear in your translation. Alternate translation: [Yahweh is in the heavens, they are his throne] or [Yahweh’s throne is in the heavens]

**Quote:** יְהוָה֮ בַּ⁠שָּׁמַ֪יִם כִּ֫סְא֥⁠וֹ
**Support:** rc://*/ta/man/translate/figs-metonymy
Here, God’s **throne** represents his “rule.” If it would be helpful in your language, you could use an equivalent expression or plain language. Alternate translation: [Yahweh rules from the heavens]

**Quote:** עֵינָ֥י⁠ו יֶחֱז֑וּ
**Support:** rc://*/ta/man/translate/figs-ellipsis
The author is leaving out some of the words that in many languages a sentence would need in order to be complete. You can supply these words from the context if that would be clearer in your language. Alternate translation: [His eyes see the sons of men] or [His eyes see everything that people do]

**Quote:** עֵינָ֥י⁠ו יֶחֱז֑וּ עַפְעַפָּ֥י⁠ו יִ֝בְחֲנ֗וּ
**Support:** rc://*/ta/man/translate/figs-parallelism
These two phrases mean basically the same thing. The second emphasizes the meaning of the first by repeating the same idea with different words. Hebrew poetry was based on this kind of repetition, and it would be good to show this to your readers by including both phrases in your translation, if possible. If it is helpful, you could connect the phrases in a way that shows that the second phrase is repeating the first one, not saying something additional. Alternate translation: [His eyes see, yes, his eyelids test] or [His eyes see, indeed, his eyelids test]

**Quote:** עֵינָ֥י⁠ו יֶחֱז֑וּ עַפְעַפָּ֥י⁠ו יִ֝בְחֲנ֗וּ
**Support:** rc://*/ta/man/translate/figs-metonymy
Here, **eyes** and **eyelids** represent God’s ability to see and evaluate everything people do. If it would be helpful in your language, you could use an equivalent expression or plain language. Alternate translation: [He sees, he tests]

**Quote:** בְּנֵ֣י אָדָֽם
**Support:** rc://*/ta/man/translate/figs-idiom
The author is using **sons of man** as a common expression of his culture to mean “all human beings.” If this phrase does not have that meaning in your language, you could use a comparable expression from your language that does have that meaning, or you could state the meaning plainly. Alternate translation: [the children of mankind] or [every human being]

## Psalms 11:1-3

**Quote:** אֵ֭יךְ תֹּאמְר֣וּ לְ⁠נַפְשִׁ֑⁠י נ֝֗וּדִי הַרְ⁠כֶ֥ם צִפּֽוֹר׃ כִּ֤י הִנֵּ֪ה הָ⁠רְשָׁעִ֡ים יִדְרְכ֬וּ⁠ן קֶ֗שֶׁת כּוֹנְנ֣וּ חִצָּ֣⁠ם עַל־יֶ֑תֶר לִ⁠יר֥וֹת בְּמוֹ־אֹ֝֗פֶל לְ⁠יִשְׁרֵי־לֵֽב׃ כִּ֣י הַ֭⁠שָּׁתוֹת יֵֽהָרֵס֑וּ⁠ן צַ֝דִּ֗יק מַה־פָּעָֽל
**Support:** rc://*/ta/man/translate/writing-quotations
Many Bible scholars think that David’s quote of his advisors begins with the words **Flutter like a bird to your mountain** and ends at the end of verse three. Some Bible scholars think that **Flutter like a bird to your mountain** are the only words of his advisors that David is quoting and that verses 2–3 are his own words. The ULT and UST follow option one. If a translation exists in your region that follows option two, you may wish to follow that option.
