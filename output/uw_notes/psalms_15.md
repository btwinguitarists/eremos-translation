## Chapter intro

# Psalm 15 Introduction

## Structure and Formatting

Psalm 15 is considered to be one of the “wisdom psalms.” See the Introduction to Psalms for more information about the wisdom psalms. (See: [[rc://*/tw/dict/bible/kt/wise]])

  1. Two parallel rhetorical questions with the same meaning (1)
  2. Ten character traits of the type of person Yahweh lets enter his presence (2–5b)
  3. The promised reward for the person meeting Yahweh’s requirements (5c)

## Religious and Cultural Concepts in This Psalm

### The type of person who may enter into God’s presence

Verse one begins with two questions that are both asking the same thing (parallelism). They are both rhetorical questions that are meant to make the reader think intently about the type of behaviors and attitudes that God requires. Verses 2–5b provide the answer to the questions in verse one. They indicate ten character traits that God requires. The last sentence in verse five is the conclusion to this psalm and states the promised reward for meeting Yahweh’s moral requirements. (See: [[rc://*/tw/dict/bible/kt/godly]] and [[rc://*/tw/dict/bible/kt/good]] and [[rc://*/tw/dict/bible/kt/promise]])

### Moral Conditions for Worship

Scholars have noted that this psalm contains a list of ten different conditions for those who want to participate in worship at the temple. The list consists of three positives in verse two, three negatives in verse three, two positives in verse four, followed by two negatives in verse five. (See: [[rc://*/tw/dict/bible/kt/temple]])

## Translation Issues in This Psalm\r
\r
### Who do the pronouns “he,” “his,” and “him” refer to?\r
\r
All occurrences of the pronouns “he,” “his,” and “him” refer to the same type of person. The person being described is not one particular person but rather anyone who exhibits the behaviors described in verses 2–5.

## Psalms 15:1

**Quote:** מִי־יָג֣וּר בְּ⁠אָהֳלֶ֑⁠ךָ מִֽי־יִ֝שְׁכֹּ֗ן בְּ⁠הַ֣ר קָדְשֶֽׁ⁠ךָ
**Support:** rc://*/ta/man/translate/figs-parallelism
These two phrases, **who may sojourn in your tent** and **Who may dwell on your holy mountain**, mean basically the same thing. The second emphasizes the meaning of the first by repeating the same idea with different words. Hebrew poetry was based on this kind of repetition, and you may want to show this to your readers by including both phrases in your translation. If it is helpful, you could connect the phrases in a way that shows that the second phrase is repeating the first one, not saying something additional. Alternate translation: [who may sojourn in your tent, yes, who may dwell on your holy mountain]

**Quote:** מִי־יָג֣וּר בְּ⁠אָהֳלֶ֑⁠ךָ מִֽי־יִ֝שְׁכֹּ֗ן בְּ⁠הַ֣ר קָדְשֶֽׁ⁠ךָ
**Support:** rc://*/ta/man/translate/figs-rquestion
David uses these two questions to make the reader think deeply about how to live a righteous life. If a speaker of your language would not use the question form for this purpose, you could translate these questions as statements or exclamations. Alternate translation: [this is the type of person who may sojourn in your tent. This is the type of person who may dwell on your holy mountain.]

**Quote:** יָג֣וּר
**Support:** rc://*/ta/man/translate/figs-explicit
The word **sojourn** means to be living somewhere, either temporarily or permanently, as a resident alien. By **may sojourn**, David means someone who is allowed to visit and come as a guest. You could indicate this explicitly in your translation if that would be helpful to your readers. Alternate translation: [may dwell as a guest] or [may be welcomed]

**Quote:** בְּ⁠אָהֳלֶ֑⁠ךָ
**Support:** rc://*/ta/man/translate/figs-explicit
By **your tent**, David means Yahweh’s Temple that was in Jerusalem on Mount Zion. You could indicate this explicitly in your translation if that would be helpful to your readers. Alternate translation: [in your temple] or [in your dwelling place]

**Quote:** בְּ⁠הַ֣ר קָדְשֶֽׁ⁠ךָ
**Support:** rc://*/ta/man/translate/figs-explicit
By **your holy mountain**, David means Mount Zion, the place where Yahweh’s Temple was. You could indicate this explicitly in your translation if that would be helpful to your readers. Alternate translation: [on your holy mountain, Mount Zion]

**Quote:** מִי־יָג֣וּר בְּ⁠אָהֳלֶ֑⁠ךָ מִֽי־יִ֝שְׁכֹּ֗ן בְּ⁠הַ֣ר קָדְשֶֽׁ⁠ךָ
**Support:** rc://*/ta/man/translate/figs-metonymy
Here, **tent** and **holy mountain** represent Yahweh’s presence. If it would be helpful in your language, you could use an equivalent expression or plain language. Alternate translation: [who may sojourn in your presence? Who may dwell with you]

## Psalms 15:2

**Quote:** הוֹלֵ֣ךְ תָּ֭מִים וּ⁠פֹעֵ֥ל צֶ֑דֶק וְ⁠דֹבֵ֥ר אֱ֝מֶ֗ת בִּ⁠לְבָבֽ⁠וֹ
**Support:** rc://*/ta/man/translate/figs-ellipsis
David is leaving out some of the words that in many languages a sentence would need in order to be complete. You can supply these words from the context if that would be clearer in your language. Alternate translation: [The person who may enter your presence, Yahweh, is the one walking blamelessly, and doing righteousness, and speaking truth in his heart] or [The person who may sojourn in your house and dwell on your holy mountain is the one walking blamelessly, doing righteousness, and speaking truth in his heart]

**Quote:** הוֹלֵ֣ךְ תָּ֭מִים
**Support:** rc://*/ta/man/translate/figs-metaphor
David is speaking of how a person lives as if that were a way or path that the person was **walking** along. If it would be helpful in your language, you could state the meaning plainly. Alternate translation: [The one living blamelessly]

**Quote:** הוֹלֵ֣ךְ תָּ֭מִים וּ⁠פֹעֵ֥ל צֶ֑דֶק וְ⁠דֹבֵ֥ר אֱ֝מֶ֗ת בִּ⁠לְבָבֽ⁠וֹ׃
**Support:** rc://*/ta/man/translate/figs-genericnoun
David is not referring to a specific **one**. He means people in general. If it would be helpful to your readers, you could use an expression that would be more natural in your language. Alternate translation: [Those who walk blamelessly, do righteousness, and speak truth] or [Anyone who walks blamelessly, does righteousness, and speaks truth in his heart]

**Quote:** צֶ֑דֶק
**Support:** rc://*/ta/man/translate/figs-abstractnouns
If your language does not use an abstract noun for the idea of **righteousness**, you could express the same idea in another way. Alternate translation: [what is right]

**Quote:** וְ⁠דֹבֵ֥ר אֱ֝מֶ֗ת
**Support:** rc://*/ta/man/translate/figs-abstractnouns
If your language does not use an abstract noun for the idea of **truth**, you could express the same idea in another way. Alternate translation: [and speaking what is true]

**Quote:** וְ⁠דֹבֵ֥ר אֱ֝מֶ֗ת בִּ⁠לְבָבֽ⁠וֹ
**Support:** rc://*/ta/man/translate/figs-metaphor
Here, the **heart** represents where a person’s thoughts and intentions exist. The phrase **speaking truth in his heart** means “speaking the truth within himself in his thoughts” and refers to a person thinking true thoughts within and then speaking out loud to others according to that truth. You may have a comparable expression in your language, or you could state the meaning plainly.

## Psalms 15:3

**Quote:** עַל־לְשֹׁנ֗⁠וֹ
**Support:** rc://*/ta/man/translate/figs-metonymy
Here, **tongue** represents speech. If it would be helpful in your language, you could use an equivalent expression or state the meaning plainly. Alternate translation: [with his speech]

**Quote:** לֹא־עָשָׂ֣ה & רָעָ֑ה
**Support:** rc://*/ta/man/translate/figs-nominaladj
The psalmist is using the adjective **evil** as a noun to mean evil things. Your language may use adjectives in the same way. If not, you can translate this adjective with an equivalent phrase. Alternate translation: [he does not do evil things]

**Quote:** וְ֝⁠חֶרְפָּ֗ה לֹא־נָשָׂ֥א עַל־קְרֹֽב⁠וֹ
**Support:** rc://*/ta/man/translate/figs-explicit
By **an insult he does not raise up**, David means “an insult he does not speak.” You could indicate this explicitly in your translation if that would be helpful to your readers. Alternate translation: [an insult he does not speak against one close to him] or [an insult he does not speak against his neighbor]

**Quote:** קְרֹֽב⁠וֹ
**Support:** rc://*/ta/man/translate/figs-explicit
By **one close to him**, David could mean: (1) a person who has a close relationship with him. Alternate translation: [his friend] or (2) a person who lives within a close physical distance from him. Alternate translation: [his neighbor]

## Psalms 15:4

**Quote:** בְּֽ⁠עֵ֘ינָ֤י⁠ו
**Support:** rc://*/ta/man/translate/figs-idiom
The psalmist is using **In whose eyes** as a common expression meaning “in the judgment of” or “in the opinion of.” If this phrase does not have that meaning for your readers, you could use a comparable expression from your language that does have that meaning, or you could state the meaning plainly. Alternate translation: [In whose judgment] or [In whose opinion]

**Quote:** נִמְאָ֗ס
**Support:** rc://*/ta/man/translate/figs-explicit
By **one being rejected**, the author means one whom God has rejected or despised. You could indicate this explicitly in your translation if that would be helpful to your readers. Alternate translation: [one whom God has rejected]

**Quote:** נִבְזֶ֤ה ׀ בְּֽ⁠עֵ֘ינָ֤י⁠ו נִמְאָ֗ס
**Support:** rc://*/ta/man/translate/figs-activepassive
The expression **one being rejected** is a passive verbal form. If your language would not use that form, you could express the idea in active form or in another way that is natural in your language. If you need to say who does the action, the context indicates that it is God. Alternate translation: [He despises the person God rejects]

**Quote:** נִשְׁבַּ֥ע לְ֝⁠הָרַ֗ע
**Support:** rc://*/ta/man/translate/figs-explicit
The implication is that he keeps his promise even when doing so will cause him difficulty or loss. You could include this information if that would be helpful to your readers. Alternate translation: [He keeps his promises even when it costs him]

**Quote:** וְ⁠לֹ֣א יָמִֽר
**Support:** rc://*/ta/man/translate/figs-explicit
The implication is that the person does not change his oath or promise even when circumstances make it difficult or costly to keep. You could include this information if that would be helpful to your readers. Alternate translation: [and does not break his promise]

## Psalms 15:5

**Quote:** כַּסְפּ֤⁠וֹ & נָתַ֣ן בְּ⁠נֶשֶׁךְ֮
**Support:** rc://*/ta/man/translate/figs-explicit
By **put out his money at interest**, the author means charge interest when lending money. You could indicate this explicitly in your translation if that would be helpful to your readers. Alternate translation: [He does … charge interest when he lends his money]

**Quote:** נָקִ֗י
**Support:** rc://*/ta/man/translate/figs-nominaladj
David is using the adjective **innocent** as a noun to mean a certain kind of person. Your language may use adjectives in the same way. If not, you can translate this adjective with an equivalent phrase. Alternate translation: [innocent people]

**Quote:** עֹֽשֵׂה־אֵ֑לֶּה לֹ֖א יִמּ֣וֹט לְ⁠עוֹלָֽם
**Support:** rc://*/ta/man/translate/figs-metaphor
David is speaking of the person God accepts as if he were an object that could be **shaken**. Your language may have a comparable expression that you can use in your translation, or you could state the meaning plainly. Alternate translation: [Yahweh will ensure that the one doing these things will always live securely] or [Yahweh will protect and provide for the ones doing these things so that the circumstances of life do not overwhelm them]

**Quote:** לֹ֖א יִמּ֣וֹט לְ⁠עוֹלָֽם
**Support:** rc://*/ta/man/translate/figs-doublenegatives
This is a double negative, consisting of the negative particle **not** and the negative word **shaken**. If it would be clearer in your language, you could use a positive expression to translate this. Alternate translation: [will remain secure for eternity]

**Quote:** עֹֽשֵׂה־אֵ֑לֶּה לֹ֖א יִמּ֣וֹט לְ⁠עוֹלָֽם
**Support:** rc://*/ta/man/translate/figs-activepassive
See how you translated the phrase “I will not be shaken” in [10:6](../010/006.md) and the phrase “when I am shaken” in [13:4](../13/04.md). Alternate translation: [Nothing will ever shake the one doing these things]

**Quote:** לֹ֖א יִמּ֣וֹט לְ⁠עוֹלָֽם
**Support:** rc://*/ta/man/translate/figs-explicit
The author assumes that readers will know that the phrase **for eternity** means “forever.” You could say that explicitly if that would be helpful to your readers. Alternate translation: [will never be shaken]
