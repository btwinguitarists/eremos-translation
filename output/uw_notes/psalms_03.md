## Chapter intro

# Psalm 3 Introduction

## Structure and Formatting

  1. Foes “rising” against psalmist (1–2)
  2. Trust expressed to Yahweh (on his holy hill, see 2:6) (3–4)
  3. Trust in Yahweh proclaimed (5–6)
  4. Help petitioned from Yahweh (“rising” and saving refer back to 1–2) (7–8)

See 2 Samuel 15–18 for the story of David fleeing from Absalom.

## About the Psalm

  1. Purpose: to pray for salvation from enemies
  2. Content: Yahweh, save me! My enemies are many, but you are my defense.
  3. Message: Salvation is of Yahweh

## Translation Issues in This Psalm

  1. The psalm divides into four sections that are nearly the same length (14 words / 14 words / 14 words / 15 words).
  2. The second and third sections share similar beginnings; both begin with an independent personal pronoun (3a: “you;” 5a: “I”). “You” (=Yahweh) is the topic of the second section (3–4), and “I” (=David) is the topic of the third section (5–6).
  3. The first section (1–2) and the two middle sections (3–6) are connected by the ideas of “many/multitudes … against me.” See: [[rc://*/ta/man/translate/writing-poetry]]
  4. Yahweh is pictured as a shield.
  5. Safety is pictured as sleeping.
  6. Yahweh’s salvation from enemies is pictured in violent graphic language.
  7. Wicked people’s power is represented by their teeth.

## Religious and Cultural Concepts in This Psalm

  1. Selah: This psalm contains the first instances of the Hebrew word **Selah**. Refer to the introduction to Psalms for more information.

  2. Superscriptions: This psalm is the first one which starts with a superscription. Refer to the introduction to Psalms for more information.

## Psalms 3:1

**Quote:** יְ֭הוָה מָֽה־רַבּ֣וּ צָרָ֑⁠י
**Support:** rc://*/ta/man/translate/figs-exclamations
The word **how** marks this as an emotional outburst that is emphasizing to Yahweh how very desperate the author’s situation is. Use an exclamation that would communicate that meaning in your language. Alternate translation: [Oh Yahweh, I have so many enemies]

**Quote:** קָמִ֥ים עָלָֽ⁠י
**Support:** rc://*/ta/man/translate/figs-idiom
Here, **risers against me** is an common expression that means “people who rebel against me” or “people who wish to fight me.” If this phrase does not have that meaning in your language, use an expression from your language that does have that meaning or state the meaning plainly. Alternate translation: [Those getting in my face] or [Those who rebel against me]

## Psalms 3:2

**Quote:** אֵ֤ין יְֽשׁוּעָ֓תָ⁠ה לּ֬⁠וֹ בֵֽ⁠אלֹהִ֬ים
**Support:** rc://*/ta/man/translate/figs-abstractnouns
If your language does not use an abstract noun for the idea of **salvation**, you could express the same idea in another way. Alternate translation: [Surely God will not save him]

**Quote:** סֶֽלָה
**Support:** rc://*/ta/man/translate/translate-transliterate
This is the first instance of the Hebrew word **Selah**, the meaning of which is unknown. The Septuagint translates the term as διάψαλμα (diapsalma), meaning “musical interlude.” Your translation team should decide if you will (1) use this word in its Hebrew form, **Selah** or (2) omit **Selah** or (3) follow the Septuagint and translate something like “pause” into your language.

## Psalms 3:3

**Quote:** מָגֵ֣ן בַּעֲדִ֑⁠י
**Support:** rc://*/ta/man/translate/figs-metonymy
Here, **shield** represents protection. If it would be helpful in your language, you could use an equivalent expression from your language or state the meaning plainly. Alternate translation: [are my protection]

**Quote:** כְּ֝בוֹדִ֗⁠י
**Support:** rc://*/ta/man/translate/figs-metonymy
Because Yahweh is the actor in all the other actions in this verse, calling **Yahweh** his **glory** probably means that Yahweh gives the author glory or honor. Since the author has just spoken about his enemies and about Yahweh being his protector, he probably means that Yahweh gives him glory by giving him victory over his enemies. If it would be helpful in your language, you could use an equivalent expression from your language or state the meaning plainly. Alternate translation: [the one who gives me glory] or [the one who gives me victory]

**Quote:** וּ⁠מֵרִ֥ים רֹאשִֽׁ⁠י
**Support:** rc://*/ta/man/translate/figs-idiom
Lifting the author’s **head** could mean (1) giving him courage or (2) giving him victory over his enemies or (3) restoring him to being king of Israel (given the context of David fleeing from his son’s usurping). If this phrase does not have that meaning in your language, you could use a comparable expression from your language that does have that meaning or state the meaning plainly. Alternate translation: [and the one who encourages me] or [and the one who lifts me over my enemies] or [and the one who lifts me to the throne]

## Psalms 3:4

**Quote:** אֶקְרָ֑א וַ⁠יַּֽעֲנֵ֨⁠נִי
**Support:** rc://*/ta/man/translate/figs-explicit
The implication is that the author is calling to Yahweh for help and that Yahweh’s answer takes the form of giving help. You can include this information if it would be helpful to your readers. Alternate translation: [I call for help … and he helped me]

## Psalms 3:5

**Quote:** אֲנִ֥י שָׁכַ֗בְתִּי וָֽ⁠אִ֫ישָׁ֥נָ⁠ה הֱקִיצ֑וֹתִי כִּ֖י יְהוָ֣ה יִסְמְכֵֽ⁠נִי
**Support:** rc://*/ta/man/translate/grammar-connect-logic-result
The phrase **laid myself down and slept** is not a one-time occurrence but a universal statement about his daily peace because of Yahweh. If it would be more natural in your language, you could reverse the order of these phrases since the second phrase, **for Yahweh protects me**, gives the reason for the result, **I awoke** as well as the reason for the author’s confidence that he can lie down and sleep. Alternate translation: [Because Yahweh protects me, I lie down, sleep, and awake]

## Psalms 3:7

**Quote:** ק֘וּמָ֤⁠ה
**Support:** rc://*/ta/man/translate/figs-metonymy
The psalmist is using **rise up** as a common expression of the culture to ask Yahweh to take action. He is not telling him to get out of bed or stand up from a seated position. If this expression does not have that meaning for your readers, you could use a comparable expression from your language that does have that meaning, or you could state the meaning plainly. Alternate translation: [Take action] or [Do something]

**Quote:** אֱלֹהַ֗⁠י
**Support:** rc://*/ta/man/translate/figs-possession
The psalmist is not using the phrase **my God** to mean that he possesses or controls Yahweh, but rather to emphasize that Yahweh is the God he worships. See the introduction to Psalms for further discussion. Alternate translation: [oh God whom I worship]

**Quote:** הִכִּ֣יתָ אֶת־כָּל־אֹיְבַ֣⁠י לֶ֑חִי שִׁנֵּ֖י רְשָׁעִ֣ים שִׁבַּֽרְתָּ
**Support:** rc://*/ta/man/translate/figs-parallelism
These two phrases mean basically the same thing. Translate these phrases according to the decisions your team has made regarding translating parallelism in Hebrew poetry. See the Introduction to Psalms (../front/intro.md) for more information. The phrases **my enemies** and **the wicked** refer to the same group of people. Alternate translation: [you hit all my enemies on the jaw; you even break those wicked enemies’ teeth] or [you destroy all my enemies]

**Quote:** הִכִּ֣יתָ אֶת־כָּל־אֹיְבַ֣⁠י לֶ֑חִי
**Support:** rc://*/ta/man/translate/translate-symaction
Hitting an enemy on the cheek was a way of insulting people. If it would be helpful to your readers, you could explain the significance of this action in the text or in a footnote. Alternate translation: [you will insult all my enemies like someone hitting them on the jaw]

**Quote:** שִׁנֵּ֖י רְשָׁעִ֣ים שִׁבַּֽרְתָּ
**Support:** rc://*/ta/man/translate/figs-metaphor
Animals attack with their teeth. Breaking their teeth takes away their power to attack. David speaks as if Yahweh would come and physically fight against the wicked. Your language may have a comparable expression that you could use in your translation, or you could state the meaning plainly. Alternate translation: [you make the wicked unable to harm me like someone breaking the teeth of a ferocious animal]

**Quote:** רְשָׁעִ֣ים
**Support:** rc://*/ta/man/translate/figs-nominaladj
See how you translated the phrase **the wicked** in [1:1](../01/01.md).

## Psalms 3:front

**Quote:** לְ⁠דָוִ֑ד
