## §Item-A — Spot-revision detail

**Greek:** Ἰουδαίους εἶναι ἑαυτούς (2:9) / ἑαυτοὺς Ἰουδαίους εἶναι (3:9) — "say they are Jews and are not."

**The rhetorical logic the reviewers surfaced (Gemini's framing, paraphrased):** The Greek requires the title-claim to read as **honorable/noble** in-the-claim-context, because the opponents are *falsely claiming* a valid covenantal heritage. If the Thai noun itself reads as pejorative ("ยิว" with compressed-Passion-narrative weight), the rhetoric breaks: opponents wouldn't boastfully claim a derogatory title.

**Eremos's prior choice (per the JHN end-of-book audit, 2026-04-30):** "ยิว" was applied at REV 2:9 + 3:9 because the contexts looked superficially Passion-narrative-like (sharp polemic, "synagogue of Satan" framing). The audit packet flagged this as "highest-stakes test cases" precisely because the JHN-audit corpus principle was about to compound here.

**Why the consensus shift to ชาวยิว wins:**
1. Greek-rhetoric preservation — opponents' boastful claim must read as a noble-claim falsified, not a pejorative-claim asserted.
2. Match with NIV-2011 / NRSVue / BSB / ESV footnoting practice for these verses (the modern critical-text consensus also handles 2:9 + 3:9 with reader-management notes, not pejorative-form lexical choices).
3. The existing verse-level KD already supplies the historical-context framing (Smyrna's Jewish-majority-city conflict, Diaspora-Judaism background), so the verse-level "translator's note" function the JHN-audit form was performing is already discharged.
4. ChatGPT and Gemini are independent evaluators; both flagged MAJOR CONCERN with the prior "ยิว" choice — strong signal.

**JHN-audit implications:** The JHN-audit four-way split (ผู้นำชาวยิว / ชาวยิว / ยิว / deprecated-พวกผู้นำยิว) remains correct *within John* — the Passion-narrative compressed-form ยิว tracks the Jerusalem-trial-narrative register specifically. **REV 2:9 + 3:9 are NOT Passion-narrative register** — they are seven-churches local-Smyrna/Sardis polemic. The corpus-doc `docs/translator_decisions/ioudaioi_johannine_2026-04.md` (still TBD) should document this distinction explicitly when it is written.

## §Item-F — User decision needed

| Reading | Reviewer | Reasoning |
|---------|----------|-----------|
| **Normalize ταχύ → เราจะมาในไม่ช้า uniformly** (keep 2:5 distinct because it lacks ταχύ) | ChatGPT | "The repeated ἔρχομαι ταχύ formula is a major Revelation refrain. Since 22:7, 22:12, and 22:20 are climactic and memorable, varying the same formula earlier may look like drift rather than principled contextualization." |
| **Keep register-split** (โดยเร็ว for letters / ในไม่ช้า for epilogue) | Gemini | "The register-split accurately reflects the shifting illocutionary force ... In the church letters (REV 2:16, 3:11), the context is an urgent warning of sudden judgment/visitation, making the terse 'โดยเร็ว' (speedily/swiftly) highly appropriate. In Chapter 22, the formula shifts to a grand eschatological promise of imminence across a massive time horizon, where the more solemn 'ในไม่ช้า' (soon) carries the right theological gravity." |

**Both readings are translator's-judgment-defensible.** ChatGPT prioritizes Greek-formula uniformity (same Greek → same Thai); Gemini prioritizes context-illocutionary-force (urgent-warning vs eschatological-anticipation).

**Ben's adjudication (2026-05-04):** keep the split (Gemini's reading). The illocutionary-force shift is real and the Thai-flow benefits from honoring it.

**Logos AI scholarly corroboration (2026-05-04, post-adjudication):** Aune (WBC 52c:1184, 1233–1234) explicitly distinguishes the formula's force across the five occurrences: letters (2:16) refer to "a coming in judgment prior to the Parousia" — local disciplinary action; epilogue (22:7, 12, 20) is "a final, decisive eschatological coming" — Parousia. Aune calls the formula a **graduated** (not monolithic) refrain whose "illocutionary force shifts dramatically based on context — from corrective to eschatological." This is exactly the principle the Eremos register-split honors. ChatGPT's "looks like drift" concern is decisively answered by major-commentary scholarship.

**Action recorded:** Lock written, KDs added, scholarly backing cited — see `docs/translator_decisions/erchomai_tachy_revelation_register_split_2026-05.md` §4 + §5.
