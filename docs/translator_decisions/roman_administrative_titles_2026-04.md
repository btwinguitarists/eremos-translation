# Roman administrative titles — Thai rendering policy

**Scope:** All Roman political, military, and judicial titles appearing in the NT, especially the dense Acts cluster. Pauline epistles will revisit ἐξουσίαι (Romans 13:1–7), βασιλεῖς + οἱ ἐν ὑπεροχῇ (1 Tim 2:1–2), and the αἱ ἀρχαί / αἱ ἐξουσίαι in Eph 6:12.

**Decided:** 2026-04 during Acts end-of-book review (see `docs/ACT_END_OF_BOOK_REVIEW_2026-04-26.md` §2). Confirmed by Gemini 2.5 Pro + ChatGPT external review.

**Evidence base (Acts, 60+ occurrences):** 4:1; 5:24, 5:26; 10:1, 10:22; 12:1, 12:20; 13:7, 13:8, 13:12; 16:20, 16:22, 16:35, 16:36, 16:38; 17:6, 17:8; 18:12; 19:31, 19:35, 19:38; 21:31, 21:32, 21:33; 22:24–29; 23:10, 23:17–24, 23:26, 23:33; 24:1, 24:10, 24:22, 24:23; 25:1, 25:8, 25:10–13, 25:21, 25:24–26, 25:27; 26:30, 26:32; 27:1, 27:6, 27:11, 27:31, 27:43; 28:7.

## The ἀνθύπατος / ἡγεμών distinction matters

**Two distinct Roman administrative offices:**
- **ἡγεμών** (in Acts: Felix, Festus) governs an **imperial province** (provinces under direct emperor control, e.g., Judea).
- **ἀνθύπατος** (in Acts: Sergius Paulus on Cyprus, Gallio in Achaia, generic plural at Ephesus) governs a **senatorial province** (provinces administered by the Senate via proconsuls, e.g., Cyprus, Achaia, Asia).

**Why Thai distinguishes:** ผู้ว่าราชการ (governor) vs. ผู้สำเร็จราชการ (proconsul-regent) preserves the Roman political distinction. The Latin terms are technical and Luke uses them precisely (Cyprus reverted to senatorial administration in 22 BC; Gallio's proconsulship is the most precisely-dateable event in Acts thanks to the Delphi Inscription SIG³ 801).

**Earlier drift normalized 2026-04-26:** Acts 18:12 (Gallio) and 19:38 (Ephesus) had drifted to ผู้ว่าการ — one syllable shy of ผู้ว่าราชการ (the locked rendering for ἡγεμών), creating reader-confusion potential between two distinct ranks. Both AIs (Gemini + ChatGPT) flagged the drift; normalized to ผู้สำเร็จราชการ.

## Honorifics on Roman + Jewish authorities

Per `honorifics_non_divine_authorities_2026-04.md`:
- **Roman governors (Felix, Festus):** plain register. No ทรง prefix; verbs are stated, not "ตรัส." Pronouns: เขา, ท่าน. Acts compliance verified.
- **Jewish kings (Herod Agrippa I, Agrippa II):** ราชาศัพท์ when the narrator grants kingly action (12:1 ทรงเริ่มจับกุม; 25:13 เสด็จมา; 25:14 ทรงประทับ; 26:26 ทรงทราบ). Plain register when functioning as private/guilty-conscience speaker (mirrors Mark 6:14/16 Herod Antipas pattern).
- **Centurions, tribunes, magistrates:** plain register. No ทรง.
- **Jewish high priest, Sanhedrin:** plain register (Caiaphas, Ananias, etc.).

## Why not Latin transliterations

Considered. Rejected:
- Thai readers more familiar with administrative-equivalence translations (นายร้อย / นายพัน) than Latin transliterations (เซนทูริโอ / ตริบูน).
- Pauline forward-pass: Romans 13's ἐξουσίαι ("authorities") is generic — wouldn't admit a Latin-specific transliteration.
- Translation policy: render administrative-equivalents where Thai has them; transliterate proper-name titles (Καῖσαρ, Σεβαστός) where the Greek-as-name is part of the meaning.

## Caesar / Augustus distinction

Σεβαστός ≠ Καῖσαρ:
- **Καῖσαρ** is the **throne-name** (used like a personal name; technically Octavian's adopted family name preserved as imperial title)
- **Σεβαστός** is the **imperial title** ("the august / venerable one")

Thai distinguishes: ซีซาร์ (Caesar throne-name) vs. องค์จักรพรรดิ (Augustus title-translation). Festus uses Σεβαστός at Acts 25:21 + 25:25 specifically to render Latin Augustus respectfully — not the personal name. Locked.

## Forward implications

- **Romans 13:1–7** (πᾶσα ψυχὴ ἐξουσίαις ὑπερεχούσαις ὑποτασσέσθω): generic governing-authorities → use **ผู้มีอำนาจ** / **ผู้ปกครอง** (generic), not the Acts-specific titles.
- **1 Timothy 2:1–2** (βασιλέων καὶ πάντων τῶν ἐν ὑπεροχῇ): kings + those-in-authority → กษัตริย์และผู้มีอำนาจทั้งปวง.
- **1 Peter 2:13–14** (βασιλεῖ + ἡγεμόσιν): king + governors → กษัตริย์และผู้ว่าราชการ (apply the Acts ἡγεμών lock).

## Alternatives considered

- **Single rendering ผู้ว่า- prefix family for all governor-titles.** Rejected — collapses ἀνθύπατος + ἡγεμών distinction Luke deliberately preserves.
- **Latin transliteration throughout.** Rejected — Thai reader-comprehension priority.
- **ผู้ว่าการ for ἀνθύπατος (the prior drift).** Rejected — phonetically too close to ἡγεμών's ผู้ว่าราชการ.

## When to revisit

- If Pauline corpus introduces a generic ἀρχή / ἐξουσία that needs distinguishing from these specific titles, document.
- If a verse-level political-detail (e.g., the Caesarea garrison's specific Roman-cohort name) requires more granular Latin terminology, document.
