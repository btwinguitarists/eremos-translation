# Gender passages — Thai register + interpretive-crux footnote policy

**Scope:** How the Eremos translation handles biblical passages on gender, marriage, and the male-female relationship — preserving the Hebrew/Greek polysemy + ambiguity where the original languages preserve it, surfacing major interpretive cruxes in Layer-2 footnotes rather than via interpretive paraphrase, and avoiding the Thai-evangelical-default-reading collapses that flatten the text.

**Decided:** 2026-05-12 (Genesis end-of-book audit §B + external AI review — both ChatGPT and Gemini recommend writing this doc now to anchor precedent before the corpus expands).

**Companion docs:** `hebrew_idioms_and_metaphor_2026-05.md`, `ot_register_policy_2026-05.md`, `divine_anthropomorphism_thai_grammar_2026-05.md`.

**Locking precedents:** Gen 1:27–28, Gen 2:18, Gen 2:24, Gen 3:16, Eph 5:21–33, 1 Cor 11, 1 Tim 2.

---

## 1. The principle

Biblical gender passages are interpretively-contested in modern Christian discourse — both within Thai evangelicalism and globally. The Hebrew and Greek source-texts often preserve **deliberate polysemy** that allows multiple legitimate readings; modern English translations differ on which reading to lift to the surface. Eremos's policy is:

1. **Preserve source-language polysemy** wherever Hebrew/Greek allows multiple readings. Do not foreclose at the surface.
2. **Document the interpretive crux** in Layer-2 footnotes — name the readings, the manuscript-witness evidence, the major commentary traditions, and (where relevant) the THSV2011 reading for Thai-reader cross-reference.
3. **Avoid Thai-cultural-default collapses** — modern Thai-evangelical readers may default to one reading (often the romantic/affectionate reading for "desire" / the egalitarian reading for "helper" / the complementarian reading for "head"). Translations must not silently encode the default.
4. **Avoid English-translation-default collapses** — ESV-style controlling readings, NIV-style softening, NRSV-style inclusive-language adjustments. Eremos translates **from** Hebrew/Greek, not from English mediation.

## 2. Locking precedents — Genesis 1–3

### 2.1 Gen 1:27 — "male and female he created them" (זָכָר וּנְקֵבָה)

Hebrew: זָכָר וּנְקֵבָה בָּרָא אֹתָם ("male and female he created them"). The clause is the structural-anchor of biblical-gender-theology. **No collapse permitted** — both genders are preserved with parallel grammatical weight.

**Eremos:** **ชายและหญิงพระองค์ทรงสร้างพวกเขา** (male-then-female ordering matches Hebrew sequence; both gendered nouns explicit).

**Forward applicability:** every NT citation of Gen 1:27 (Mt 19:4, Mk 10:6 — Christ's marriage teaching) must use the same Thai phrase for cross-corpus visibility.

### 2.2 Gen 2:18 — "a helper-corresponding-to-him" (עֵזֶר כְּנֶגְדּוֹ)

Hebrew: אֶעֱשֶׂה־לּוֹ עֵזֶר כְּנֶגְדּוֹ ("I will make for him a helper corresponding-to-him"). The Hebrew **עֵזֶר** (ezer "helper") is **not** a subordinating term — the same lemma appears in the OT for God's help (Ex 18:4, Deut 33:7+26+29, Ps 121:1–2, 124:8). The qualifier **כְּנֶגְדּוֹ** ("corresponding-to-him" / "matching-him" / "facing-him") preserves the matched-counterpart-relationship.

**Eremos:** **ผู้ช่วยที่เคียงข้างกับเขา** ("a helper who is alongside-him"). Preserves the matched-counterpart sense. Verse-level KD names the ezer-lemma-thread (forward to Pss 121, 124).

**Forward applicability:** when Ps 121:2 ships ("My help comes from YHWH"), the Thai must use the same ผู้ช่วย / ความช่วยเหลือ vocabulary so readers see the lemma-thread that anchors the woman-as-helper question.

### 2.3 Gen 2:24 — "they shall be one flesh" (וְהָיוּ לְבָשָׂר אֶחָד)

Hebrew: וְדָבַק בְּאִשְׁתּוֹ וְהָיוּ לְבָשָׂר אֶחָד ("he shall cling to his wife, and they shall be one flesh"). The verb דָבַק ("cling/cleave") + the result-clause "one flesh" + the leave-father-and-mother frame establish the marriage-anchor. NT citations: Mt 19:5, Mk 10:7, Eph 5:31, 1 Cor 6:16.

**Eremos:** **ผู้ชายจะละจากบิดามารดาของเขาและไปผูกพันกับภรรยาของเขา และทั้งสองจะเป็นเนื้อเดียวกัน**. Preserve the leave-cleave-become structure + the "one flesh" anchor. The verse-level KD names the NT-corpus-recurrence at Mt 19:5 / Eph 5:31.

### 2.4 Gen 3:16 — "your desire will be for your husband" (תְּשׁוּקָתֵךְ)

**See `output/textual_variants/genesis_03.json` verse 16 Layer-2 footer** — Eremos preserves the polysemy of תְּשׁוּקָה (the same lemma's 3 OT occurrences: Gen 3:16, Gen 4:7, Song 7:10 carry different senses) at the surface, and the footer names the two reading-families (affectionate/relational vs. controlling/predatory).

**Surface:** **ความปรารถนาของเจ้าจะอยู่ที่สามีของเจ้า** (ambiguous). Matches THSV2011's stance. Layer-2 footer carries the editorial transparency.

### 2.5 Gen 3:15 — protoevangelium (זֶרַע + הוּא)

**See `output/textual_variants/genesis_03.json` verse 15 Layer-2 footer.** Eremos uses **ผู้นั้น** (individual-Christological reading) at the surface; Layer-2 footer documents the Hebrew's individual-vs-corporate ambiguity + the LXX αὐτός / Vulgate ipse trajectory + Rom 16:20 NT-trajectory.

The individual-Christological surface reading is intentional per project's evangelical-Protestant canonical posture; the footer ensures the manuscript-level question remains visible to readers seeking maximum MT-neutrality.

## 3. Forward applicability — NT passages

### 3.1 Eph 5:21–33 — Christ-and-church / husband-and-wife

The passage's controlling-image (Christ-as-head + church-as-bride) is theologically-loaded and rhetorically-careful in Greek. The Eremos NT-side rendering preserves the head-body metaphor + the mutual-submission frame (5:21 ὑποτασσόμενοι ἀλλήλοις) + the husband-loves-wife reciprocal command (5:25 ἀγαπᾶτε).

**Key terms:** κεφαλή ("head") locked to **เป็นศีรษะ** (preserve the body-metaphor); ὑποτάσσω ("submit/place-under") locked to **อยู่ภายใต้** (preserve the literal placement-under sense without softening to "respect"); ἀγαπάω locked to **รัก** (the standard NT-corpus love-verb).

**Layer-2 footer** at Eph 5:21–24 should name the mutual-submission-vs-hierarchical-submission reading-families + the THSV2011 reading + the NT-Greek's ambiguity vs. the modern English-translation polarization.

### 3.2 1 Cor 11:3–16 — "head" passages

The 1 Cor 11 head-covering / head-of-the-woman / glory-of-man passage carries multiple cruxes (κεφαλή as "head/source/authority"; γυνὴ as "woman/wife"; head-covering as cultural-or-permanent). Eremos preserves the polysemy via verse-level KDs; Layer-2 footers at the major cruxes (11:3, 11:7, 11:10) document the reading-families.

### 3.3 1 Tim 2:11–15 — "I do not permit a woman to teach"

The 1 Tim 2 passage is the most-disputed gender passage in modern Christian discourse. Eremos translates literally — **ไม่อนุญาตให้สตรีสอน** (do not permit a woman to teach) + Layer-2 footer naming the major reading-families (permanent-creation-ordinance reading vs. Ephesian-cultural-context reading vs. Artemis-cult-specific reading + the 1 Tim 2:15 "saved through childbearing" crux).

## 4. The Thai-cultural-context layer

Thai evangelical discourse has its own register on gender questions that interacts with the source-language polysemy in specific ways:

1. **Wife/husband terminology** — Thai has multiple word-pairs (สามี-ภรรยา formal; ผัว-เมีย colloquial; คู่สมรส legal-modern). Eremos defaults to สามี-ภรรยา (formal, biblical-appropriate register) per `ot_register_policy_2026-05.md`.
2. **The "head of the woman" question** — Thai cultural register tends toward formal-hierarchical phrasing in religious contexts, which may amplify a complementarian-leaning reading where the Greek/Hebrew is more ambiguous. Verse-level KDs should name when the Thai surface inadvertently reads more strongly than the source.
3. **The "desire" question (Gen 3:16)** — Thai ความปรารถนา is broadly polysemous (romantic-affectionate, sexual, longing, ambition, craving) — matches the Hebrew teshuqah's polysemy well. Less risk of Thai-cultural-default collapse here than English ("desire" in English now carries strong romantic-affectionate connotation by default).
4. **Submission language** — Thai ยอมจำนน / อยู่ภายใต้ / เชื่อฟัง each carry distinct registers; the choice maps to the Greek/Hebrew nuance, not to modern Thai-cultural defaults.
