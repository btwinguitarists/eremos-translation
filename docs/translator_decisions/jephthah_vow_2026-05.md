# Jephthah's vow — ambiguity-preservation policy

**Scope:** JDG 11:29–40 — the Jephthah-vow pericope, specifically the *content* of the vow (11:30–31) and the *fulfillment* line (11:39). Also governs the broader policy for translating Hebrew exegetical cruxes in narrative texts where the source preserves ambiguity that the receptor language is tempted to resolve.

**Decided:** 2026-05-20 (Ben, post-Judges-end-of-book audit). Locked **before** any 2 Kings 3:27 (Mesha-Moab child sacrifice) or Micah 6:7 (rhetorical child-sacrifice question) work, both of which face analogous translation pressure.

**Trigger:** Judges end-of-book audit Item D + 2-way external AI review (ChatGPT + Gemini, convergent FINE-with-doc-lock verdict). The shipped JDG 11:31 + 11:39 preserve the Hebrew ambiguity rather than forcing either the literal-sacrifice or perpetual-virginity reading; this doc captures the lock so future translators do not silently resolve the crux under reader-or-publisher pressure.

---

## The lock

**1. JDG 11:31 vow-content:** `וְהָיָה לַיהוָה וְהַעֲלִיתִיהוּ עֹלָה` ("…will be the LORD's, and / or I will offer it up as a burnt offering").

- **Locked Thai surface (shipped):** `จะเป็นขององค์พระผู้เป็นเจ้า และข้าพระองค์จะถวายเป็นเครื่องเผาบูชา`
- **Force of וְ ... וְ:** preserve as **"และ … และ"** ("and … and"), NOT collapsed to "or" / "หรือ".
- **Why:** the Hebrew waw-construction does carry a *possible* disjunctive reading ("X will be the LORD's, OR I will offer it as a burnt offering"), but that reading is contested and the conservative move is to preserve the more natural conjunctive force ("will be the LORD's, AND I will offer it as burnt offering"). Pushing "or" into the main text would over-translate toward the perpetual-virginity reading.

**2. JDG 11:39 fulfillment-line:** `וַיַּעַשׂ לָהּ אֶת־נִדְרוֹ אֲשֶׁר נָדָר וְהִיא לֹא־יָדְעָה אִישׁ` ("…and he did to her according to his vow which he vowed, and she had not known a man").

- **Locked Thai surface (shipped):** `เขาก็ทำต่อเธอตามคำปฏิญาณที่เขาได้ปฏิญาณ และเธอไม่เคยรู้จักผู้ชาย`
- **Non-explicating:** the Thai must say "he did to her according to his vow" — NOT "he killed her", NOT "he sacrificed her", NOT "he dedicated her to lifelong virginity". The Hebrew fulfillment-line is studiedly opaque; the Thai must be equally studiedly opaque.
- **Why:** the verse is one of the OT's most contested exegetical cruxes. Adding interpretive clarification in the main text would force readers into one of two readings without warrant from the Hebrew surface.

---

## The two major readings (preserved in notes/commentary, not in main text)

| Reading | Position | Supporters |
|---|---|---|
| **Literal sacrifice** | Jephthah literally offered his daughter as a burnt offering; the text condemns by understatement and the "two months to mourn her virginity" prepares the reader for her death | Most modern critical scholars; Josephus (Antiq. 5.7.10); the plain-sense reading of עֹלָה ("burnt offering") |
| **Perpetual virginity / dedication** | Jephthah dedicated his daughter to lifelong virginity at the tabernacle (cf. women serving at the door, Ex 38:8); the וְ in 11:31 is disjunctive; וְהִיא לֹא־יָדְעָה אִישׁ at 11:39 is the *result* of the vow, not a postscript | Many medieval Jewish commentators (Kimchi, Levi ben Gershon); some evangelical commentators concerned about the moral character of a faith-hall figure (HEB 11:32) |

**Eremos posture:** present both readings in notes; do not select one in the main text. This is consistent with the project's optimal-equivalence philosophy and with the OT canon's intentional refusal to resolve the question on the surface.

## Forward-protection: analogous cruxes

This lock establishes the precedent for several upcoming texts where the same translation pressure will recur:

1. **2 Kings 3:27** — Mesha king of Moab offers his firstborn son as a burnt offering on the wall; the Thai must preserve the surface description without commentary on whether YHWH responded (the verse abruptly says וַיְהִי קֶצֶף־גָּדוֹל עַל־יִשְׂרָאֵל "and there was great wrath against Israel" — wrath whose source is exegetically contested).
2. **Micah 6:7** — rhetorical "Shall I give my firstborn for my transgression…?"; the Thai must preserve the rhetorical question without flattening it into a declarative.
3. **Genesis 22 (the Akedah)** — already handled (Abraham/Isaac); precedent of preserving the surface tension between the command and the angel's intervention without inserting interpretive smoothing.
4. **Hosea 13:2** ("they kiss calves") — preserve the surface without commentary on whether literal idolatry or metaphor.
5. **Judges 19–21 atrocity-narratives** — preserve the surface; the editorial "no king in Israel" frame (21:25) is the Hebrew author's own interpretive lens and is sufficient.

## Heb 11:32 — separate question

HEB 11:32 names `Jephthah` (now locked as `เยฟทาห์` per the OT-faithful cross-corpus rule, see `proper_names_and_transliteration_2026-05.md`) among the faith-heroes. This creates theological tension if Jephthah literally sacrificed his daughter. **Eremos's posture:** that tension belongs in commentary, not in the main text of either Judges or Hebrews. The Hebrew author of Hebrews chose to include Jephthah without comment on the vow; the Thai must do the same. Readers and pastors can engage the moral question with reference to the broader canon (1 SAM 15:22, HOS 6:6, MIC 6:6–8), not by having the Eremos main text resolve it.

---

## What the main text does NOT do

- Does NOT add "หรือ" ("or") at 11:31 to push the disjunctive reading.
- Does NOT add "ฆ่า" ("kill") or "บูชา" ("sacrifice") at 11:39 to push the literal-sacrifice reading.
- Does NOT add "ที่พลับพลา" ("at the tabernacle") at 11:39 to push the virginity-dedication reading.
- Does NOT interpolate any clarification of "what Jephthah did" beyond what the Hebrew narrator chose to say.
