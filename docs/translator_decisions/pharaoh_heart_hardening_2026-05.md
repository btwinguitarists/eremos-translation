# Pharaoh's heart-hardening — Three Hebrew roots, single Thai idiom

**Scope:** The hardening-of-heart vocabulary cluster across the Exodus plague narrative (Exod 4-14) and its OT + NT trajectory. This doc names the deliberate decision to collapse three distinct Hebrew roots (חזק, קשה, כבד) into a single Thai idiom (แข็งกระด้าง / ทำให้ใจแข็งกระด้าง) while preserving the theologically-critical subject distinction (Pharaoh hardens himself vs. YHWH hardens Pharaoh) through Thai syntax.

**Decided:** 2026-05-13 (Ben + tri-AI review of Exodus end-of-book audit). All three reviewers agreed: FINE as shipped; forward-protection doc to lock the principle.

**Companion docs:** None directly; cross-references `divine_anthropomorphism_thai_grammar_2026-05.md`.

## 1. The three Hebrew roots

The Exodus plague cycle uses three distinct Hebrew verbal roots for Pharaoh's heart-hardening, with overlapping but slightly different lexical color:

| Root | Lexical color | Frequency in Exod 4-14 | Voice / Subject pattern |
|---|---|---|---|
| **חזק** (ḥazak) | "to be strong / hard / firm" | ~12× | Both YHWH-as-subject (Exod 4:21, 7:3, 9:12, 10:1, 10:20, 10:27, 11:10, 14:4, 14:8, 14:17) and Pharaoh-as-subject (Exod 7:13, 7:22, 8:19, 9:35) |
| **קשה** (qashah) | "to be heavy / difficult / stubborn" | ~3× | Both YHWH-as-subject (Exod 7:3 in the formula introduction) and Pharaoh-as-subject (Exod 13:15 retrospectively) |
| **כבד** (kavad) | "to be heavy / unresponsive / dull" | ~5× | Mostly Pharaoh-as-subject (Exod 8:15, 8:32, 9:7, 9:34); YHWH-as-subject only in 10:1 retrospectively |

Three roots, similar semantic field, different lexical emphasis. Modern English translations (NIV, ESV, CSB, NLT) generally flatten to a single English verb ("hardened") regardless of root.

## 2. The decision: single Thai idiom

The Eremos Thai rendering uses **แข็งกระด้าง** (or causative form **ทำให้ใจแข็งกระด้าง / ทำให้พระทัยของฟาโรห์แข็งกระด้าง**) uniformly across all three roots. The two theological dimensions are preserved by Thai grammar:

### 2.1 Subject distinction (the critical one)

The classical theological tension — "Pharaoh hardened his heart" vs. "YHWH hardened Pharaoh's heart" — is preserved by Thai syntax, NOT by lexical variation:

- **Pharaoh-as-subject** (self-hardening, reflexive): ฟาโรห์ทรงทำให้พระทัยของพระองค์แข็งกระด้าง / พระทัยของฟาโรห์แข็งกระด้าง.
- **YHWH-as-subject** (divine hardening): องค์พระผู้เป็นเจ้าทรงทำให้พระทัยของฟาโรห์แข็งกระด้าง.
- **Stative** (becomes hard, no agent specified): พระทัยของฟาโรห์แข็งกระด้าง.

The Thai grammar carries the agency. The reader can track the unfolding theological tension (Exod 4-7 mostly YHWH-as-subject in prediction; 7-8 mostly Pharaoh-as-subject in fact; 9 onward shifts back to YHWH-as-subject as judgment intensifies). That's the load-bearing thread.

### 2.2 The three-Hebrew-roots-to-one-Thai is forward-protective

The flattened Thai is the right cross-corpus choice because:

- **Romans 9:18** ("ὃν θέλει σκληρύνει — whom he wills, he hardens") uses a single Greek root (σκληρύνω). The locked NT Thai for σκληρύνω is **แข็งกระด้าง / ทำให้แข็งกระด้าง** (per Eremos NT corpus).
- **John 12:40** (citing Isa 6:10) uses the same vocabulary; same NT Thai lock.
- **Hebrews 3:8, 13, 15; 4:7** ("do not harden your hearts") — same Greek, same Thai.
- **Isaiah 6:10, 63:17; Deut 2:30; Josh 11:20; 1 Sam 6:6** — all extend the OT hardening-theology trajectory.

Uniform Thai across the entire corpus means: a Thai reader who notices the hardening-theme in Exodus can follow it directly into Paul's exegesis in Romans 9 and the Johannine Isaiah citation. Three-roots-to-three-Thai-verbs would fracture exactly the cross-corpus thread that makes the doctrine of hardening (and the related debates about divine sovereignty + human responsibility) traceable.

## 3. What this doc explicitly does NOT do

This doc does **not** flatten the subject distinction. The Hebrew/Thai grammar of "X hardens Y's heart" vs. "Y's heart becomes hard" is the load-bearing distinction, and the translator must preserve it verse-by-verse. The three-roots-to-one-verb decision is a *lexical* flattening, not a *grammatical* one.

This doc also does **not** preclude verse-level Layer-2 footnotes flagging the specific Hebrew root in theologically loaded verses (e.g., Exod 7:3 — the formula's introduction; Exod 9:12 — the first explicit YHWH-as-subject instance after a sequence of Pharaoh-as-subject). Footnotes can carry the Hebrew nuance for readers who want it; the surface Thai stays uniform.
