# Pastoral Epistles corpus-level locks

**Scope:** corpus-level editorial decisions across the Pastoral Epistles (1 Timothy, 2 Timothy, Titus). Locked during 1 Timothy end-of-book review so that 2 Timothy + Titus inherit them cleanly without re-litigation.

**Decided:** 2026-05-02 during 1 Timothy end-of-book review.
**Evidence base:** 1 Timothy 1–6 (six chapters shipped) + external AI review packet (Grok / Gemini / ChatGPT, 2026-05-02). All three reviewers converged FINE on items A, C, D, F, G; B and E carry caveats addressed below.

---

## A. πιστὸς ὁ λόγος formula → "ถ้อยคำนี้เชื่อถือได้"

**Scope:** the Pastoral citation formula that introduces or closes a saying believed to be a known maxim/creed. Occurs 5× in the Pastorals: 1 Tim 1:15; 3:1; 4:9; 2 Tim 2:11; Tit 3:8.

**The rule.** Render πιστὸς ὁ λόγος as **ถ้อยคำนี้เชื่อถือได้** ("trustworthy is this saying"). When the longer expansion πιστὸς ὁ λόγος καὶ πάσης ἀποδοχῆς ἄξιος appears (1 Tim 1:15; 4:9), extend to **ถ้อยคำนี้เชื่อถือได้และสมควรแก่การยอมรับอย่างเต็มที่**.

**Reference direction.** Where the Greek is ambiguous about whether the formula refers backward to what was just said or forward to what follows (e.g., 1 Tim 4:9), preserve that ambiguity. The Thai introduction ถ้อยคำนี้ ("this saying") is itself directionally non-committal — do not commit to forward or backward by adding directional language.

**Why not "คำนี้สัตย์จริง"** ("this word is true"). Rejected: over-theologizes πιστός as "true/faithful in a doctrinal sense" rather than "trustworthy as a saying," and loses the formulaic-citation feel that the Pastorals deliberately use to flag a known maxim being quoted.

**Why not "คำกล่าวนี้น่าเชื่อถือ"** (a more polished alternative). Rejected: the Pastorals' πιστός has a personal-trust resonance closer to เชื่อถือได้ (relational reliability) than น่าเชื่อถือ (objective credibility). Lock the relational form.

**When to revisit.** If a Thai reviewer notes that ถ้อยคำนี้เชื่อถือได้ feels stilted, consider tightening to ถ้อยคำที่เชื่อถือได้ — but only if a fresh consistency pass across all five Pastoral occurrences confirms the change reads better in every context. Do NOT vary form between occurrences.

## B. εὐσέβεια and family → "ความเคร่งในพระเจ้า"

**Scope:** εὐσέβεια / εὐσεβέω / εὐσεβής — the Pastorals' virtue-summary term. Occurs ~13× in the Pastorals (concentrated in 1 Tim and 2 Tim, plus Tit 1:1; 2:12).

**The rule.** Default render εὐσέβεια as **ความเคร่งในพระเจ้า** ("devoted-orientation toward God"). Verb forms (εὐσεβέω) → ดำเนินชีวิตด้วยความเคร่งในพระเจ้า. Adjective (εὐσεβής) → ผู้เคร่งในพระเจ้า.

**Intended sense gloss.** ความเคร่งในพระเจ้า is to be read as "reverent, God-oriented life" — embodied piety that is active and Godward, not narrow legalistic strictness. The เคร่ง element captures the Pastorals' emphasis on disciplined, principled living without flattening into mere "religious observance."

**Documented exception — 1 Tim 3:16.** The phrase τὸ τῆς εὐσεβείας μυστήριον (the mystery of εὐσέβεια) introduces the Christ-hymn and functions as a comprehensive summary of the gospel itself, not personal piety. Render as **ข้อล้ำลึกแห่งทางของพระเจ้า** ("the mystery of the way of God") at this verse only. This is a context-specific necessity: εὐσέβεια here is the *content* of the gospel-confession, parallel to "the faith" or "the gospel." All three external reviewers ratified this departure as defensible.

**Do NOT extend the 3:16 departure corpus-wide.** The 3:16 case is uniquely a hymn-introduction summary phrase. Every other Pastoral occurrence stays with ความเคร่งในพระเจ้า.

**Why not "ความยำเกรงพระเจ้า"** (fear-of-God). Rejected as a default: ยำเกรง renders φόβος-family terms (fear/awe), not εὐσέβεια (devoted practice). Using ยำเกรง here would conflate distinct Greek lexical fields and weaken later φόβος renderings.

**Why not "ความเลื่อมใสในพระเจ้า"** (devout faith). Considered. Rejected: เลื่อมใส leans toward conviction/faith-content rather than the active, embodied life-orientation that εὐσέβεια carries in the Pastorals. ความเคร่ง captures the practiced, disciplined dimension better.

**When to revisit.** If 2 Tim 3:5 (ἔχοντες μόρφωσιν εὐσεβείας — "having a form of εὐσέβεια") reads awkwardly with ความเคร่งในพระเจ้า, consider whether a paired noun (รูปแบบของความเคร่ง...) handles the μόρφωσις contrast better than literal nesting.

## C. σωτήρ applied to God the Father → "พระเจ้าพระผู้ช่วยให้รอดของเรา"

**Scope:** Pastoral Christological-shift in σωτήρ usage. Unlike the rest of the Pauline corpus (where σωτήρ is overwhelmingly Christ), the Pastorals freely apply σωτήρ to the Father (1 Tim 1:1; 2:3; 4:10; Tit 1:3; 2:10; 3:4) AND to Christ (2 Tim 1:10; Tit 1:4; 2:13; 3:6).

**The rule.** Use **พระผู้ช่วยให้รอด** as the σωτήρ lemma uniformly across the Pastorals, regardless of whether the referent is God the Father or Christ. When applied to God the Father, the standard expression is **พระเจ้าพระผู้ช่วยให้รอดของเรา** (God our Savior). When applied to Christ, **พระเยซูคริสต์ พระผู้ช่วยให้รอดของเรา** (or contextual variant).

**Why not split σωτήρ into two Thai lemmas based on referent.** Considered. Rejected: σωτήρ is the same Greek word and the same salvation-historical title; splitting into "Father-Savior" vs "Christ-Savior" would obscure the deliberate Pastoral pattern that locates salvation-action in BOTH Father and Son. Mainstream English (BSB/ESV/NIV/CSB) preserve "Savior" uniformly; we follow.

**Pre-decision for Titus 2:13** (τοῦ μεγάλου θεοῦ καὶ σωτῆρος ἡμῶν Ἰησοῦ Χριστοῦ).

The Granville-Sharp construction here (single article governing both nouns) is widely recognized by modern evangelical scholarship as referring to a single subject: "our great God and Savior, Jesus Christ." All three external reviewers (Grok, Gemini, ChatGPT) converged: **render high-Christological at Titus 2:13** rather than preserving artificial ambiguity.

Provisional Thai construction: **พระเยซูคริสต์ พระเจ้าผู้ยิ่งใหญ่และพระผู้ช่วยให้รอดของเรา** ("Jesus Christ, our great God and Savior") — Ben to confirm at Titus translation time. The Thai apposition naturally supports the single-person reading without forced syntax.

**When to revisit.** If a Thai-speaking reviewer flags awkwardness in the Tit 2:13 construction, options are: (a) restructure word order; (b) add bracketed clarification. Do NOT switch to a two-person reading — the Granville-Sharp + Pastoral high Christology consensus is stable.

## D. ἀρσενοκοίτης → "ผู้ชายที่ร่วมประเวณีกับผู้ชาย"

**Scope:** the Pauline compound coined from LXX Lev 18:22 / 20:13 (ἄρσην + κοίτη). Occurs in 1 Cor 6:9; 1 Tim 1:10.

**The rule.** Render ἀρσενοκοίτης as **ผู้ชายที่ร่วมประเวณีกับผู้ชาย** ("man who has sexual relations with a man"). This decision is held in common with the 1 Cor 6:9 corpus lock and applies forward to 1 Tim 1:10 + any future occurrences.

**Why this and not "ชายรักร่วมเพศ"** (homosexual man). Rejected: imports modern socio-sexual identity categories (orientation, identity-as-such) that the Greek compound does not encode. The compound is a behavior-descriptor derived from the Levitical prohibition formula, not an identity-classification.

**Why not "ผู้รักร่วมเพศชาย"** (male same-sex-loving person). Rejected as above; identity language is anachronistic.

**Why not "ผู้กระทำผิดทางเพศกับผู้ชาย"** (one who sins sexually with a man). Rejected: imports moral judgment into the lemma itself. The Pauline compound is descriptive of behavior; the moral judgment is supplied by the surrounding vice-list context, not embedded in the term.

**Lexical-background note.** The Thai rendering preserves the LXX Levitical derivation (ἄρσην + κοίτη = "male" + "lying/bed") in clinical, neutral register. Thai readers who want the etymological background can consult the per-verse `notes` field; we do NOT add a Tier-2 footer note for this term unless future reviewer feedback indicates a need (clinical-descriptive register avoids the political freight of modern debates without requiring footnoting).

**When to revisit.** Only if a Thai-speaking NT scholar provides evidence that the rendering is misread by Thai readers as something other than the LXX-derivation. The principled choice should hold across the corpus.

## E. Women-in-worship passages: ambiguity-preservation strategy

**Scope:** 1 Tim 2:11–15; 1 Tim 3:11; 2 Tim 3:6–7; Tit 2:3–5. The corpus's contested gender-related passages.

**The rule.** Preserve the Greek's grammatical and lexical ambiguity in Thai rather than committing to one interpretive school. This applies across the Pastoral corpus, not just 1 Timothy.

**Specific decisions locked at 1 Timothy:**

- **γυνή 2:11** → **หญิง** (ambiguous between "woman" and "wife"; Greek does not specify)
- **ἡσυχία 2:11–12** → **ความสงบเงียบ** (preserves both "peaceable demeanor" and "silence" range)
- **αὐθεντέω 2:12** (NT hapax) → **ใช้อำนาจเหนือ** (preserves both neutral-authority and negative-domineering possibilities)
- **διδάσκειν 2:12** → **สอน** (no contextual narrowing to "authoritative-teaching only")
- **σωθήσεται διὰ τῆς τεκνογονίας 2:15** → **ได้รับความรอดผ่านการคลอดบุตร** (preserves all four major interpretations including the τῆς-as-definite-article-Incarnation reading)
- **γυνή 3:11** → **หญิง** with NO possessive pronoun (preserves wives-of-deacons vs women-deacons crux)

**Theological guardrails in thai_summary.** Where the literal Thai surface might mislead Thai readers (notably 2:15's "saved through childbearing" surface reading), the verse-level `thai_summary` adds an explicit theological guardrail clarifying that the verse cannot mean salvation-by-childbearing-as-works (which would contradict Eph 2:8-9, Rom 3-4, Tit 3:5, and 1 Tim 2:5-6 in the same chapter). The guardrail does NOT close the interpretive question — it frames the boundaries within which the legitimate interpretations operate.

**Forward applicability:**
- **2 Tim 3:6–7** (silly women / γυναικάρια): preserve the rhetorical force without commenting on whether women-in-general are characterized; the diminutive γυναικάριον is the abusive term, not γυνή.
- **Tit 2:3–5** (older-women instruction): preserve the household-codes register without modernizing or moralizing.
- **Rom 16:1** (Phoebe / διάκονος): when reaching Romans, render διάκονος without forcing an ecclesiastical office; preserve the same Phoebe-style ambiguity that 1 Tim 3:11 maintains.

**Why this strategy.** Per RULES §1, optimal-equivalence preservation of Greek ambiguity is the project default. For contested gender passages, this discipline is especially important: we are translating, not adjudicating between complementarian and egalitarian readings. The ambiguity is in the Greek; the Thai must carry it forward.

**When to revisit.** If a Thai reviewer reports that Thai readers are systematically reading 2:11–12 with one interpretation pre-imposed (and the Greek does not require that), consider whether a thai_summary expansion would help — but do NOT shift the main text out of ambiguity-preservation.

## F. Phoebe-style διάκονος ambiguity

**Scope:** 1 Tim 3:11 (γυναῖκας), forward to Rom 16:1 (Phoebe).

**The rule.** Where Greek leaves the ecclesiastical-office vs general-service distinction unresolved (no possessive pronoun, structural parallelism marker like ὡσαύτως), preserve that openness in Thai. Do not force "wives [of deacons]" or "[women] deacons" by interpretive expansion.

**For 1 Tim 3:11.** **หญิง** with no possessive ("women" / "wives" — deliberately ambiguous). The thai_summary may note that many modern scholars favor the women-deacons reading, but the main text does not commit.

**For Rom 16:1 (Phoebe, διάκονος).** When reaching Romans, render διάκονος in a way that preserves the same openness — likely **ผู้ปรนนิบัติ** or **ผู้รับใช้** (general service language) rather than committing to an ecclesiastical office word. Decision finalizes at Romans translation time.

**When to revisit.** If a forthcoming Pauline corpus pattern (e.g., 1 Tim 3:8 διάκονος-as-office combined with 3:11 γυναῖκας ὡσαύτως) makes the women-deacons reading more compelling than ambiguity-preservation, the doc may be updated — but the bar for that change is high (multiple corpus-level signals, not a single proof-text).

## G. μιᾶς γυναικὸς ἄνδρα formula and feminine inversion

**Scope:** the Pastoral marital-faithfulness formula in qualification lists. 1 Tim 3:2 (overseer); 1 Tim 3:12 (deacon); 1 Tim 5:9 (widow, feminine inversion); Tit 1:6 (overseer).

**The rule.** Render μιᾶς γυναικὸς ἄνδρα literally as **เป็นสามีของภรรยาเพียงคนเดียว** ("being husband of only one wife"). For the feminine inversion at 1 Tim 5:9 (ἑνὸς ἀνδρὸς γυνή), render as **เป็นภรรยาของสามีเพียงคนเดียว** ("being wife of only one husband"). Apply to Titus 1:6 likewise.

**Why preserve the literal structure.** The Greek formula carries four major interpretations: (1) one-wife monogamy at-a-time, (2) no remarriage after divorce, (3) no remarriage after widowhood, (4) marital fidelity. Each has substantial scholarly support. The literal Thai construction sits inside all four readings without committing.

**Why not "ผู้ที่มีความซื่อสัตย์ในการแต่งงาน"** (marital-fidelity gloss). Rejected: closes off interpretations (1)–(3) by forcing the modern-majority "fidelity" reading. The literal preserves the structural openness.

**Why preserve the 5:9 feminine inversion exactly.** Paul deliberately mirrors the masculine formula. Rendering 5:9 with a non-mirrored construction would erase Paul's own structural parallelism and obscure the comparative point.

**When to revisit.** Only if a sustained Thai reviewer pattern indicates the literal structure is read as "polygamy is allowed up to one wife" (i.e., misread as a permission rather than a restriction). The current rendering is not vulnerable to that misread in normal Thai — but if reviewer feedback indicates otherwise, consider tightening to เป็นสามีที่มีภรรยาคนเดียว.

## H. 1 Tim 3:16 Christ-hymn — Ὃς vs Θεός variant

**Scope:** the famous 1 Tim 3:16 textual variant. SBLGNT reads **Ὃς** (relative pronoun, "he who"); Byzantine/TR reads **Θεός** ("God"). Mainstream evangelical critical-text translations follow Ὃς; KJV and THKJV follow Θεός.

**The rule (main text).** Render the SBLGNT Ὃς reading as **พระองค์ทรง...** ("He"; royal-prefix passive construction). Do NOT supply Θεός in the main text. Do NOT explicitly insert พระคริสต์ as the subject; the hymn-fragment grammar with the implicit Christological subject is preserved.

**Tier-2 footer note.** Because "พระเจ้าทรงปรากฏในเนื้อหนัง" is deeply entrenched in conservative Protestantism through KJV/THKJV, a silent omission of the Byzantine reading would create a reader-trust liability. A Tier-2 chapter-footer note is therefore added at `output/textual_variants/1timothy_03.json` (type: `reading_variant`) presenting the Byzantine reading + manuscript witnesses + scribal-mechanism explanation.

This is the **first Tier-2 `reading_variant` entry** in the corpus. The rendering pipeline (`render_reader.py` and `export_to_usfm.py`) was extended on 2026-05-02 to support this type alongside the existing `inclusion_variant_absent` type. The new type renders with the header "การอ่านในต้นฉบับโบราณต่างกัน" ("the readings in ancient manuscripts differ") rather than "ขาดในต้นฉบับวิจารณ์" ("absent in the critical text"), since the verse is present in both traditions — only one word differs.

**Why this case warrants Tier 2 (not silent-omission like 1:17).** The 1 Tim 1:17 σοφῷ interpolation was silent-omitted per RULES §5 because mainstream English Bibles (BSB/ESV/NIV/CSB) all silently follow the shorter reading and KJV's longer reading creates no significant theological perception delta. By contrast, 1 Tim 3:16 Θεός is the famous KJV/TR Christological proof-text — silent omission would generate active suspicion of theological tampering among Thai readers shaped by THKJV. The Tier-2 footer note neutralizes that suspicion by making the manuscript reasoning visible.

**When to revisit.** If a future Thai-speaking NT scholar reviews and recommends elevating to a Tier-3 ⟦double bracket⟧ treatment (unusual — would suggest the variant is more uncertain than scholarly consensus indicates), discuss but do not act unilaterally. The Tier-2 disposition is calibrated to KJV-shaped reader trust, not to manuscript uncertainty (the SBLGNT Ὃς reading has overwhelming early-witness support).

## I. 2 Tim 3:16 πᾶσα γραφὴ θεόπνευστος

**Scope:** the famous Scripture-inspiration verse. πᾶσα γραφή permits distributive ("every passage") or collective ("all of Scripture as a whole") readings; θεόπνευστος is a HAPAX compound θεός + πνέω ("God-breathed").

**Decided:** 2026-05-03 during 2 Timothy end-of-book external AI review (ChatGPT raised CONCERN on πᾶσα; both ChatGPT + Gemini ratified the predicate construction).

**Evidence base:** Mounce (Word Biblical Commentary) + Burk (Crossway ESV Expository) commentaries explicitly preferred "God-breathed" over "inspired" and lean collective for πᾶσα. Mounce: "Elsewhere in this passage it appears that Paul is viewing Scripture as a whole, and therefore the translation 'all' is given. In its entirety Scripture comes from God." Burk: "'God-breathed' … is more accurate [than 'inspired'], which emphasizes the divine authority of Scripture."

**The rule.**

- **πᾶσα γραφή → `พระคัมภีร์ทั้งหมด`** (collective; "all of Scripture as a whole"). The Thai distributive `ทุกตอน` was used pre-revision; revised 2026-05-03 to the collective per Mounce + external review consensus.
- **θεόπνευστος → `เป็นลมพระโอษฐ์ของพระเจ้า`** (predicate adjective; "is the breath of God's [royal] mouth"). The Thai-evangelical conventional `การดลใจ` was used pre-revision; revised to preserve the breath-imagery the etymology requires per Burk + Mounce explicit preference.
- **Construction**: predicate-adjective ("All Scripture is God-breathed and is profitable"), not attributive ("All God-breathed Scripture is also profitable"). Per Mounce + Wallace *Greek Grammar* 313-14: the πᾶς + noun + adjective pattern in equative clauses defaults to predicate. The καί is copulative.

**Final main text:** `พระคัมภีร์ทั้งหมดเป็นลมพระโอษฐ์ของพระเจ้า และมีประโยชน์สำหรับ…`

**Why `ลมพระโอษฐ์` (royal-mouth-breath) instead of `ลมหายใจ` (plain breath).** `ลมพระโอษฐ์` is established Thai-biblical idiom for divine-utterance / divine-breath (e.g., สดุดี 33:6 "by the breath of his mouth"). The royal `พระโอษฐ์` register matches the gravity of divine-utterance theology in the verse. Plain `ลมหายใจ` lacks the sacral register and reads as merely physical respiration.

**Why retain a bridge to `การดลใจ` in `thai_summary`.** Thai readers shaped by THSV (which uses `การดลใจ`) may find `ลมพระโอษฐ์` jarring on first read. The `thai_summary` explicitly notes the equivalence to give familiar readers a bridge: "ในการแปลภาษาไทยแบบดั้งเดิม คำนี้บางทีแปลว่า 'การดลใจ' ซึ่งเทียบเท่ากันในความหมายเชิงเทววิทยา แต่สูญเสียภาพแห่งลมหายใจที่อยู่ในรากศัพท์." This is the same bridging-strategy used at 1 Tim 3:16 §H footer-note (Byzantine variant flagged for KJV-shaped trust).

**Why not preserve `ทุกตอน` distributive.** The Thai distributive scales toward collective in some contexts, but the Mounce contextual reading + ChatGPT's concern that `ทุกตอน` foregrounds segmentation more than the Greek requires together justified the collective rendering. The collective is also more natural in Thai liturgical / pulpit register, where "all of Scripture is God-breathed" reads more sermonically than "every section of Scripture is God-breathed."

**Why not introduce `ลมหายใจของพระเจ้า` as a noun-phrase predicate without the royal `พระโอษฐ์` prefix.** Considered. Rejected: predicate `ลมหายใจของพระเจ้า` ("breath of God") reads more flatly than the royal-formal `เป็นลมพระโอษฐ์ของพระเจ้า` and loses connection to the established Thai-biblical breath-of-the-mouth idiom.

**Forward applicability.** This decision sets the precedent for any future θεόπνευστος-adjacent vocabulary (e.g., 2 Pet 1:21's φερόμενοι ὑπὸ πνεύματος ἁγίου "carried by the Holy Spirit"). When other inspiration-vocabulary appears in future books, default to the breath-imagery preservation principle established here.

**When to revisit.** If a Thai-speaking reviewer notes that `ลมพระโอษฐ์` reads as foreign or jarring, reconsider — but the bar is high (this rendering has biblical-Thai precedent in Psalms). If a future Thai theological-vocabulary shift makes `การดลใจ` the only readable option for evangelical-doctrinal contexts, revisit, but for now the breath-preservation is the principled choice.

## J. Metaphor-flattening principle (Pastoral hapax + idiom)

**Scope:** when a Greek metaphor's vehicle (the imagery itself) does not transfer cleanly into Thai but the target sense is recoverable, prefer target-faithfulness over vehicle-preservation. The principle is **non-determinative metaphor-flattening**: if the metaphor's vehicle is not exegetically determinative (i.e., the meaning of the verse does not turn on the specific imagery), flatten to clean Thai.

**Decided:** 2026-05-03 during 2 Timothy end-of-book external AI review (ChatGPT + Gemini both ratified the practice + recommended formalization as a corpus principle).

**Evidence base across Pastorals:**

| Greek lemma/idiom | Vehicle (literal imagery) | Thai rendering | Why flattened |
|-------------------|---------------------------|----------------|---------------|
| ὑγιαίνω (διδασκαλία) | medical health/soundness | `ถูกต้อง` (correct/right) | Thai medical metaphor doesn't transfer to doctrine register |
| ὀρθοτομέω (2 Tim 2:15) | "cut straight" (woodworking / road-building / cultic) | `จัดการ ... อย่างตรงเที่ยง` (handle correctly / straight) | Vehicle ambiguous in Greek itself; target sense (handle-rightly) preserved |
| κνήθω (2 Tim 4:3) | "scratch/itch" (literal physical itching) | `หูที่อยากได้ยิน` (ears wanting-to-hear) | Literal "itching ears" reads comically in Thai; target sense (craving novel/flattering messages) preserved |
| γάγγραινα (2 Tim 2:17) | "gangrene" (medical loanword) | `โรคเนื้อตายเน่า` (necrotic-flesh-disease) | Vehicle PRESERVED here — see exception below |

**Exception: when the metaphor IS determinative, preserve the vehicle.** γάγγραινα (gangrene) at 2 Tim 2:17 preserves the medical-disease vehicle because the **specific imagery of necrotic-spread** is what makes Paul's point — false-teaching as malignant-spreading-disease requiring excision rather than debate. The vehicle does work the verse depends on. Likewise θεόπνευστος (3:16) — the breath-imagery IS exegetically determinative (per Burk + Mounce) and so is preserved (see §I above).

**Working test.** Before flattening a Pauline-Pastoral metaphor:

1. Does the verse's argument turn on the specific imagery? If yes, preserve.
2. Does the imagery have a clean Thai cognate-domain that reads naturally? If yes, preserve.
3. Is the imagery contested in the Greek scholarship itself (e.g., ὀρθοτομέω's woodworking-vs-road-building debate)? If yes, flatten — preserving the contested vehicle would import scholarly uncertainty.

If the answer to all three is "no," flatten to Thai-natural target-sense rendering.

**Forward applicability.** Apply this test to Titus's hapax + idiom inventory at translation time. Don't over-flatten — when in doubt, preserve. The principle is a license for *judicious* flattening, not a default.

**Cross-reference.** Companion to `hygiaino_sound_doctrine_2026-05.md` which is this principle's first explicit application across a full lemma-family.

## K. Extra-canonical-tradition acknowledgment policy (Jude 9 + Jude 14-15 pre-commit)

**Scope:** when canonical NT text depends on extra-canonical (Jewish-pseudepigraphal or oral-tradition) sources that are NOT visible in the OT, surface the source-acknowledgment in `thai_summary` to preserve historical transparency for Thai readers without secondary resources.

**Decided:** 2026-05-03 during 2 Timothy end-of-book external AI review (ChatGPT + Gemini both ratified the 2 Tim 3:8 Jannes/Jambres acknowledgment + recommended pre-commit application to Jude 9 + Jude 14-15).

**Established precedent — 2 Tim 3:8 (Jannes + Jambres):** the Pharaoh's-magicians' names are **not in Exodus**; they come from extra-canonical Jewish tradition (Targum Pseudo-Jonathan, Damascus Document CD 5:18-19, Pliny NH 30.11). The 2 Tim 3:8 thai_summary explicitly states this so readers searching Exodus don't experience confusion. This responsible-critical practice is now policy.

**Forward pre-commits:**

- **Jude 9 (Michael disputing with the devil over Moses' body):** the body-of-Moses-dispute is **not in Deut 34** (which records Moses' burial by God). The tradition comes from **Assumption of Moses** / **Testament of Moses** (lost work, attested by Origen *Princ.* 3.2.1, *Hom. Jos.* 2.1; Clement *Strom.* 6.15). When Jude is translated, the 9th verse `thai_summary` must state: "เรื่องราวการโต้แย้งของมีคาเอลกับมารเรื่องร่างของโมเสส ไม่ได้บันทึกไว้ในพันธสัญญาเดิม แต่มาจากคำสอนของชาวยิวนอกคัมภีร์ — น่าจะจาก *Assumption of Moses* (เอกสารที่หายไปแล้ว แต่ปรากฏการอ้างอิงในงานเขียนของบรรพบุรุษคริสตจักร)."
- **Jude 14-15 (Enoch's prophecy):** the quotation is from **1 Enoch 1:9** — a Second-Temple Jewish pseudepigraphon. Jude treats it as authoritative-prophecy without commitment to the broader 1 Enoch corpus. When Jude is translated, the 14-15 thai_summary must state: "คำพยากรณ์ที่อ้างถึงนี้มาจาก *1 เอโนค 1:9* ซึ่งเป็นเอกสารของชาวยิวยุคที่สอง (intertestamental) ที่ไม่ได้รวมอยู่ในพันธสัญญาเดิม ยูดาห์อ้างคำพยากรณ์นี้โดยไม่ได้รับรองทั้งเล่ม แต่ยอมรับว่าเป็นคำพยากรณ์ที่แท้จริงของเอโนค."

**Why surface in summary, not main text.** The main text translates the canonical NT verse faithfully without inserting parenthetical-source-citation. The summary handles the extra-canonical-source-disclosure so readers understand the historical-context without breaking the verse's flow.

**Why this matters for the Eremos Translation specifically.** Thai readers without access to evangelical reference works (study Bibles, commentaries) would otherwise:
- Search the OT for "Jannes / Jambres" / "Michael disputing over Moses" / "Enoch's prophecy" and find nothing
- Conclude either (a) the NT text is wrong, or (b) they are missing OT books
- Lose trust in the translation or in their own Bible-search competency

The historical-transparency note prevents both failure modes.

**Boundary.** This policy applies to **named extra-canonical sources** (specific persons, events, prophecies). It does NOT apply to:
- Generic Second-Temple Jewish theological vocabulary (resurrection, two-ages, Spirit-Christology) — these are widespread and don't need source-acknowledgment
- Catechetical formulas borrowed from synagogue tradition (e.g., εἷς θεός confession echoing Shema)
- General OT-allusion / OT-paraphrase (handled by the OT-citation-DB system, not by extra-canonical-tradition policy)
