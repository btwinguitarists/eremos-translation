# נָחַם (nicham) — Divine relenting / changing mind

**Scope:** The Hebrew verb נָחַם (nicham) when applied to God as subject — "be sorry, relent, change one's mind, be moved with compassion." Used in foundational OT divine-anthropomorphism texts (Gen 6:6, Ex 32:14, Num 23:19, 1 Sam 15:11+29, Jonah 3:9-10, Jonah 4:2 [in Exod 34 addendum], Joel 2:13-14, Amos 7:3+6, Jer 18:7-10). Locks the literal Thai rendering and provides the canonical interpretive framework for the apparent tension with Num 23:19.

**Decided:** 2026-05-09 (cross-AI review concurs: Gemini + ChatGPT both verdict FINE/lock as-is). Triggered by Jonah end-of-book audit.

**Companion docs:** `hebrew_idioms_and_metaphor_2026-05.md` §1.4 (anthropomorphism preserve policy), `chesed_covenant_love_2026-05.md`, `exod_34_attribute_formula_2026-05.md` (formula contains the נִחָם addendum at Joel/Jonah recitations), `leitwort_handling_policy_2026-05.md` Rule 1.

## 1. The lock — TWO SUB-SENSES (amended 2026-05-12)

The lemma נחם (Niph'al) with God as subject carries **two distinct sub-senses** that require different Thai renderings. The split was made explicit after the Genesis end-of-book audit §G + external AI review (ChatGPT CONCERN; Gemini FINE — both confirmed the split is correct).

### 1.1 Sub-sense A — Divine RELENTING (factive-volitional)

Context: **prior announcement of judgment** + a reversal-of-action-in-response-to-changed-circumstances. The classical "God relented from the disaster he had threatened" pattern.

| Hebrew form | Thai rendering | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Niph'al wayyiqtol וַיִּנָּחֶם הָאֱלֹהִים / וַיִּנָּחֶם יהוה (factive-context) | **ทรงเปลี่ยนพระทัย** | preserves the volitional shift + Rachasap |
| Niph'al participle נִחָם / וְנִחָם עַל־הָרָעָה | **ทรงเปลี่ยนพระทัยจากการลงโทษ** | Joel 2:13 + Jonah 4:2 (addendum to Exod 34 formula) |
| Niph'al imperfect/perfect (factive-context) נִחַם / יִנָּחֵם | **ทรงเปลี่ยนพระทัย** | Same lock |

**Locking precedent:** Jonah 3:10 (God relents from announced judgment after Nineveh's repentance).

### 1.2 Sub-sense B — Divine GRIEF (reflexive-emotional)

Context: **no prior announcement of judgment**. Internal sorrow / lament over creation-state or people-state. Reinforced by adjacent grief-vocabulary (עָצַב, אֵבֶל, אָנַף).

| Hebrew form | Thai rendering | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Niph'al wayyiqtol וַיִּנָּחֶם יהוה (grief-context, no judgment-decree) | **ทรงเสียพระทัย** | preserves the reflexive-emotional sense + Rachasap |
| First-person נִחַמְתִּי (grief-context) | **เราเสียพระทัย** | First-person divine grief |

**Locking precedent:** Gen 6:6 (וַיִּנָּחֶם יהוה כִּי־עָשָׂה אֶת־הָאָדָם בָּאָרֶץ "YHWH was sorry he had made man on the earth"). The adjacent וַיִּתְעַצֵּב אֶל־לִבּוֹ ("and he was grieved to his heart") confirms the grief-subtype reading.

### 1.3 How to classify

Apply the **factive-volitional test:** is there an explicit prior announcement of judgment that is now being reversed? If yes → sub-sense A (ทรงเปลี่ยนพระทัย). If no, and the context shows internal divine sorrow over a state of affairs → sub-sense B (ทรงเสียพระทัย).

The Num 23:19 / 1 Sam 15:29 counter-formulae (לֹא יִנָּחֵם "God does not change his mind") apply to **sub-sense A only** (factive-volitional immutability of divine decree). Sub-sense B (reflexive-emotional grief) is not contradicted by them — God grieving over human sin does not impeach divine immutability.

The lock applies to the **divine-subject Niph'al** form. Other binyanim (Pi'el "comfort, console" e.g. Isa 40:1) and human-subject forms have separate renderings.

## 3. The Num 23:19 / 1 Sam 15:29 apparent tension

Two OT texts explicitly say God does **not** "relent" (נחם):
- **Num 23:19**: "God is not a man that he should lie, or a son of man that he should change his mind (יִתְנֶחָם). Has he said, and will he not do it? Or has he spoken, and will he not fulfill it?"
- **1 Sam 15:29**: "Also the Glory of Israel will not lie or have regret (יִנָּחֵם), for he is not a man, that he should have regret."

These appear to contradict the multiple texts where God does relent. Mainstream evangelical interpretation resolves the tension via **two distinct senses** of נחם:

### 3.1 Sense A — "Change one's mind in response to changed circumstances"

This is the sense in Gen 6:6, Ex 32:14, 1 Sam 15:11, Jonah 3:10, etc. Here God's response *adjusts* to human action (repentance, intercession, sin) — but the *adjustment-pattern itself* is fixed by his unchanging covenant character (mercy upon repentance; judgment upon hardness).

### 3.2 Sense B — "Change one's mind in arbitrary or unprincipled ways"

This is the sense Num 23:19 and 1 Sam 15:29 deny. God does not capriciously reverse his decisions, lie about his commitments, or change his nature. His fixity-of-character is what *makes* the responsive-relenting possible (because we know what change-of-circumstance triggers what response).

### 3.3 The Jer 18:7–10 hermeneutical control passage

Jeremiah explicitly states the principle:

> "If at any time I declare concerning a nation or a kingdom, that I will pluck up and break down and destroy it, and IF that nation, concerning which I have spoken, **turns from its evil**, I WILL **relent of the disaster** that I intended to do to it. And if at any time I declare concerning a nation or a kingdom that I will build and plant it, and if it does evil in my sight, not listening to my voice, then I WILL **relent of the good** that I had intended to do to it."

(Jer 18:7–10, NIV-style, with נחם-Niph'al at "relent")

This is the canonical interpretive framework: divine-relenting is a stable pattern of responsive justice and mercy, NOT a contradiction of divine immutability.

### 3.4 What we do NOT do

- **Do not soften the relenting verb** at the factive occurrences (3:10, Ex 32:14, etc.) to avoid the apparent tension. Translation must preserve the literal anthropomorphism — the canonical authors used נחם deliberately.
- **Do not harmonize the renderings** to make Num 23:19's "not change" verb match the Jonah 3:10 "did change" verb mechanically (e.g., by translating one as "regret" and the other as "change mind"). The Hebrew verb is the same; the rendering is the same; the interpretive harmonization happens in reader-edition footnotes, not in surface text.

## 4. Why ทรงเปลี่ยนพระทัย

- **ทรง-** preserves the Rachasap (royal Thai) register required for divine subjects per `ot_register_policy §1.1`.
- **เปลี่ยน** ("change") captures the core cognitive-volitional shift.
- **พระทัย** ("royal heart/mind/will") preserves the divine-anthropomorphism (heart/mind as the seat of decision) per `hebrew_idioms_and_metaphor §1.4`.
- The compound is established in Thai Christian discourse (THSV uses "ทรงเปลี่ยนพระทัย" or "ทรงเสียพระทัย" depending on context; we lock to "ทรงเปลี่ยนพระทัย" for consistency).

### 4.1 What we deliberately reject

- **ทรงเสียพระทัย** ("be sorry, regret") — captures the regret-sense (Gen 6:6) but loses the change-of-mind force in factive occurrences (Ex 32:14, Jonah 3:10). We use ทรงเปลี่ยนพระทัย uniformly; if reader-feedback flags Gen 6:6 specifically, document a context-variant.
- **ทรงเลิก** ("cease, stop") — too weak; loses the volitional decision-shift.
- **ทรงโปรด-** ("be pleased") — wrong sense; collides with verbs of divine pleasure/favor.
- **ทรงผันเปลี่ยน** ("turn-and-change," more poetic) — reserved for poetic-context discretion.

## 6. Reader-edition footnote handling

When נחם applied to God appears in a "loaded passage" (Gen 6:6, Ex 32:14, Num 23:19, 1 Sam 15:11+29, Jonah 3:10, Jonah 4:2, etc.), the reader-edition footnote should:

1. Identify the Hebrew verb נחם and its semantic-range (regret/relenting/being-moved-with-emotion).
2. Note the apparent tension with Num 23:19 / 1 Sam 15:29.
3. Cite Jer 18:7–10 as the canonical interpretive control passage.
4. State the resolution: divine character (immutable) ≠ divine response (covenantally responsive to human action).

A standard footnote template (in use at `output/textual_variants/jonah_03.json` for Jonah 3:10) should be reused at all loaded-passage occurrences.

---

## 7. Why this is corpus-locked, not just verse-locked

נחם applied to God is a small lemma (~30 OT occurrences) but a theologically loaded one. The interpretive tension between God-relenting and God-not-relenting cannot be resolved by translating the verb differently each time — the same Hebrew verb does the work in both directions. The lock ensures Thai readers can recognize the lemma at every occurrence and bring the canonical interpretive framework (Jer 18:7–10) to bear.
