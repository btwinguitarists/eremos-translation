# OT register policy — five voice-registers + Rachasap

**Scope:** The Thai-language register policy for the OT, covering five distinct narrator-voice + speaker-voice modes plus the Rachasap (royal Thai) escalation policy that the OT will exercise far more heavily than the NT did.

**Decided:** 2026-05-04 (Ben + Gemini #1).
**Companion docs:** `divine_names_table_2026-05.md`, `hebrew_idioms_and_metaphor_2026-05.md`.

---

## 1. The five voice-registers

OT prose + poetry uses identifiable register variations. Eremos translates each consistently:

### 1.1 Narrator (default narrative voice)

The third-person omniscient voice that drives Genesis, Exodus, Joshua, Samuel-Kings narrative.

- **Verb register:** ราชาศัพท์ (royal Thai) for **divine subjects** (ทรง / เสด็จ / ตรัส / พระ-prefix on body parts). Plain Thai for **human subjects** unless the narrator explicitly elevates a king/ruler in their public-office role (cf. Mark 6:14 Herod = ทรง precedent in `honorifics_non_divine_authorities_2026-04.md`).
- **Pronoun register:** divine = พระองค์; human kings = พระองค์ in narrator's voice; commoners = เขา; opponents/enemies = เขา (no honorific downgrade).
- **Connective:** standard Thai narrative connectives `ก็`, `และ`, `แล้ว`, `จากนั้น`, mixed per `hebrew_grammar_transfer_2026-05.md` §3.

### 1.2 Wisdom-instructional voice

The "my son, listen..." voice of Proverbs, Ecclesiastes, parts of Job, the introductions to wisdom psalms.

- **Verb register:** plain Thai. The wisdom-instructor is human; no royal honorific needed.
- **Address:** **ลูกเอ๋ย / ลูก** (intimate paternal vocative); **ผู้รักความฉลาด / คนหนุ่ม / ผู้ฟังคำของเรา** (categorical address).
- **Imperatives:** **จง- / จงฟัง / จงใส่ใจ** (clear instructional imperative; Thai natural).
- **Maxim form:** Thai aphorisms tend toward parallel-line structure even in colloquial speech; preserve Hebrew parallelism via Thai parallel-clauses.

### 1.3 Prophetic-oracle voice

The "Thus says YHWH" voice — Isaiah, Jeremiah, Ezekiel, the Twelve.

- **Verb register:** ราชาศัพท์ (royal Thai) for divine speech, since YHWH is the speaker.
- **Quotative formula:** **`องค์พระผู้เป็นเจ้าตรัสดังนี้ว่า`** locked per `hebrew_idioms_and_metaphor_2026-05.md` §5.3.
- **Direct-address vocative:** YHWH addresses Israel as second-person collective — **"เจ้า / เหล่าเจ้า / พวกเจ้า"** (forceful 2nd-person collective; matches Hebrew `אַתָּה` / `אַתֶּם`).
- **Contrast pivot register:** prophetic oracles often pivot from judgment to salvation; the Thai must mark the pivot. Use **"แต่ในวันนั้น... / แต่หลังจากนั้น..."** for shift-markers.

### 1.4 Royal voice (kings speaking; Rachasap)

When kings speak — David in Samuel-Psalms, Solomon in Kings, foreign kings (Pharaoh, Nebuchadnezzar, Cyrus), queens (Esther, Bathsheba) — the speaker uses Rachasap (royal-Thai vocabulary):

- **First-person:** **เรา** (royal "we"-of-majesty). Matches the Mark precedent for Herod's `ἐγώ` (cf. `honorifics_non_divine_authorities_2026-04.md`).
- **Verbs:** ราชาศัพท์ when describing the king's action — **ทรงตรัส, ทรงสั่ง, ทรงประทาน**.
- **Possessions:** royal-thai prefixes — **พระราชวัง, พระบัลลังก์, พระราชโองการ, พระสิริของพระองค์**.
- **Body:** royal-thai body terms — **พระเนตร, พระหัตถ์, พระบาท, พระโอษฐ์, พระกรรณ**. (Same lexemes as for divine subjects — Thai Rachasap is the same register, distinguished by context.)

**Modulation for moral failure.** When a Hebrew king is depicted in shameful action (David in 2 Sam 11; Solomon's late apostasy in 1 Kings 11; Manasseh's idolatry in 2 Kings 21), **downshift the Rachasap** — narrator drops to plain verbs for the shameful action while the public-office context still gets ทรง. This matches Mark 6:16's downshift for Herod.

### 1.5 Lament voice (psalmist / prophet in personal distress)

The "I" voice of personal psalms (3, 13, 22, 51, 88), Job's speeches, Lamentations 3, Jeremiah's confessions (Jer 11:18-23, 12:1-6, 15:10-21, 17:14-18, 18:18-23, 20:7-18).

- **First-person:** **ข้าพเจ้า** (Ben default; matches NT corpus precedent for Pauline-style first-person prayer).
- **Vocative to God:** **`ข้าแต่องค์พระผู้เป็นเจ้า`** (deferential vocative + Tetragrammaton).
- **Question-as-protest:** preserve the questioning rhetoric. Hebrew laments have many unanswered "Why?" / "How long?" — render as Thai questions; do not paraphrase the protest into resolution.

## 2. Rachasap policy (the OT's biggest register-shift from the NT)

### 2.1 Why this matters

The NT had relatively few earthly authorities — Pilate, Herod (Antipas + Agrippa), centurions, governors. The Mark precedent (`honorifics_non_divine_authorities_2026-04.md`) handled them with careful judgment per-character.

The OT is **dominated by earthly monarchs**: ~40 kings of Israel + Judah, the patriarchs as proto-kings, foreign emperors (Pharaoh, Nebuchadnezzar, Cyrus, Darius, Xerxes/Ahasuerus, Artaxerxes), divine-council scenes (1 Kgs 22, Job 1-2, Ps 82), queen-mother contexts. **Rachasap will be exercised on essentially every OT page after Genesis 12.**

### 2.2 Rachasap policy table

| Subject | Narrator-voice | Direct-speech voice |
|---|---|---|
| **YHWH (Tetragrammaton-subject)** | Full ราชาศัพท์ throughout (ทรง / เสด็จ / ตรัส / พระ-prefix). Same as NT divine-Christ pattern | YHWH speaks; full Rachasap |
| **Hebrew kings (David, Solomon, all kings of Judah/Israel) — public-office context** | ราชาศัพท์ (ทรง). Narrator dignifies the office | Rachasap (เรา 1sg, royal-thai verbs and possessions) |
| **Hebrew kings — personal/shameful context** | Plain Thai (no ทรง for the shameful action) | Plain Thai (no Rachasap during moral failure narration) |
| **Patriarchs (Abraham, Isaac, Jacob, Joseph)** | Plain narrator (treat as venerated-elders, not kings); Rachasap inappropriate for tribal-patriarch role | Plain (their speech is patriarchal-elder, not royal) |
| **Moses + the prophet-leaders (Joshua, Samuel, Elijah, Elisha)** | Plain narrator (they are servants of God, not kings); narrator doesn't grant ทรง | Plain (their speech is prophet/servant) |
| **Foreign emperors (Pharaoh, Nebuchadnezzar, Cyrus, Darius, Xerxes)** | ราชาศัพท์ (ทรง) — they are the imperial sovereigns of their narratives, even if villainous | Rachasap (with downshift if context shames them) |
| **Foreign vassal-rulers, governors (Pekah, Hazael, etc.)** | Plain (they are lower than the imperial level) | Plain |
| **Divine-council figures (sons-of-God, the satan in Job 1-2, the lying spirit in 1 Kgs 22)** | Plain narrator (they are subordinate to YHWH) | Plain (with deferential register when addressing YHWH) |
| **Queens / queen-mothers (Esther, Bathsheba, Athaliah)** | ราชาศัพท์ when in royal-office context | Rachasap |
| **Priests / Levites (in narrative)** | Plain narrator (priestly-office is not a king-equivalent) | Plain. **Exception:** the High Priest at Yom Kippur or in revelatory contexts (Eli, Samuel) may receive deferential vocabulary in the verse's specific rhetoric, documented per-verse |

### 2.3 Special case: the Tabernacle / Temple

The cultic spaces are addressed with deferential-honorific Thai (**พระวิหาร**, **พระนิเวศขององค์พระผู้เป็นเจ้า**), and the cultic objects with elevated terms (**หีบพันธสัญญา**, **โต๊ะขนมปังแห่งการทรงสถิต**). This is sub-Rachasap (the register is "sacred/cultic," not "royal-of-king"), but it parallels the same elevated-vocabulary impulse.

### 2.4 Special case: divine warrior / theophany

When YHWH appears as warrior (Ex 15, Hab 3, Pss 18/29/68/97), the verb register is full Rachasap PLUS the imagery is preserved in elevated Thai poetic vocabulary:

- `יהוה אִישׁ מִלְחָמָה` (Ex 15:3) → **องค์พระผู้เป็นเจ้าทรงเป็นนักรบ / องค์พระผู้เป็นเจ้าทรงเป็นจอมทัพ**
- `יָצָא יהוה כְּגִבּוֹר` → **องค์พระผู้เป็นเจ้าเสด็จออกมาดังวีรบุรุษ**

Don't soften the warrior imagery. Hebrew theophany rhetoric is distinctive and translatable into Thai elevated-poetic register.

## 3. Adversary register

### 3.1 The serpent (Gen 3), the Satan (Job 1-2, Zech 3, 1 Chr 21), and demonic figures

Per NT corpus (`adversary_speech_register_2026-05.md`): adversaries speak in plain register, with no honorific-deference applied. Apply the same OT-side.

- **Serpent in Gen 3:** plain verbs (พูด, ถาม, กล่าว); no honorific. The serpent's deception is rhetorically smooth but registerially low.
- **The satan in Job 1-2:** plain verbs (ตอบ, กล่าว). The satan addresses YHWH deferentially (**ข้าแต่องค์พระผู้เป็นเจ้า / องค์เจ้านาย**) but the narrator doesn't grant the satan honorifics.
- **Lying spirit in 1 Kgs 22:** plain verbs.
- **Pagan deities mentioned by name (Baal, Asherah, Marduk, Bel, Dagon, Chemosh, Milcom, Tammuz):** Use ethnic/proper-noun + เทพ for foreign deity (matches NT precedent per `pagan_deity_register_2026-05.md` lock at Acts 17). Examples:
  - `הַבַּעַל` → **พระบาอัล / เทพบาอัล** (not "พระเจ้าบาอัล" — wall off พระเจ้า for YHWH)
  - `אֲשֵׁרָה` → **เทพีอาเชราห์**
  - `דָּגוֹן` → **เทพดาโกน**

### 3.2 Foreign worship vs. covenantal worship — register distinction

Hebrew uses `עָבַד` (serve) for both Israel's worship of YHWH and idolatry-of-foreign-deity. The Thai must distinguish:

- `עָבַד אֶת־יהוה` → **ปรนนิบัติองค์พระผู้เป็นเจ้า / นมัสการองค์พระผู้เป็นเจ้า** (covenantal-cultic register)
- `עָבַד אֶת־הַבַּעַל` → **ปรนนิบัติพระบาอัล / นับถือพระบาอัล** (idolatrous-foreign register; do NOT use นมัสการ which is the worship-of-true-God term)

## 4. Pronoun register summary

| Speaker → Addressee | First-person | Second-person | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| Human (commoner) → YHWH | **ข้าพเจ้า / ข้าแต่พระองค์** | **พระองค์** | Standard prayer/lament register |
| Human (king) → YHWH | **ข้าพเจ้า / ข้าแต่พระองค์** OR **เรา (royal we-1sg)** | **พระองค์** | King's prayer; Solomon's at 1 Kgs 8 uses ข้าพเจ้า in deference |
| YHWH → human | **เรา (divine we-1sg)** | **เจ้า / เหล่าเจ้า / พวกเจ้า** | YHWH speaks with divine-majestic register |
| Human king → human commoner | **เรา (royal we-1sg)** | **เจ้า / ท่าน** | Royal-of-the-king to subject |
| Human commoner → human king | **ข้าพเจ้า / ข้าพระบาท** | **พระองค์ / ใต้ฝ่าพระบาท** | Subject-to-king deferential |
| Human commoner → human commoner | **ข้าพเจ้า / ฉัน** (context) | **ท่าน / เจ้า** | Standard inter-personal |
| Adversary (satan, serpent) → human | **เรา** (matching the human-king register the adversary mimics in some scenes) | **เจ้า** | Satan in Job 1-2 uses ข้า to YHWH but เจ้า to the human Job |
| Adversary → YHWH | **ข้าแต่องค์เจ้านาย** (deferential-but-without-narrator-honorific) | **พระองค์** | Job 1-2 satan |

## 5. Acrostic-poetry register

Acrostics (Pss 9/10, 25, 34, 37, 111, 112, 119, 145; Lam 1-4; Prov 31:10-31; Nah 1) get the marginal Hebrew-letter labels per Ben's 2026-05-04 acrostic-preservation lock:

```
א
ความสุขย่อมเป็นของผู้ที่...
ข้าพเจ้าจะสรรเสริญองค์พระผู้เป็นเจ้า...
ב
...
```

The Hebrew letter + Thai phonetic name (e.g., `א อาเลฟ`) goes as a stanza-heading marginal label. The Thai poetry inside each stanza is natural Thai (does not attempt Thai-letter equivalence).

Implementation: in the `output/translations/<book>_<chapter>.json` schema, add an optional `acrostic_marker` field per verse:

```json
{
  "verse": 1,
  "hebrew": "אַשְׁרֵי תְמִימֵי־דָרֶךְ ...",
  "thai": "ความสุขย่อมเป็นของผู้ที่ดำเนินทางไม่บกพร่อง ...",
  "acrostic_marker": {
    "letter": "א",
    "phonetic_thai": "อาเลฟ",
    "stanza_position": "start"
  }
}
```

Reader-side rendering displays the letter + phonetic name as a marginal label / stanza-heading.
