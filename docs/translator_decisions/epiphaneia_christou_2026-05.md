# ἐπιφάνεια → การทรงปรากฏ (Christ's appearing — Pastoral eschatological term)

**Scope:** ἐπιφάνεια applied to Christ across the Pastorals — both first-coming (Incarnation) and second-coming (parousia) senses. Occurs 5× in the Pastorals: 1 Tim 6:14; 2 Tim 1:10; 4:1; 4:8; Tit 2:13.

**Decided:** 2026-05-03 during 2 Timothy end-of-book external AI review (ChatGPT + Gemini both flagged CONCERN on the prior intra-corpus drift between 1 Tim 6:14's `การเสด็จมาปรากฏ` and 2 Tim 1:10/4:1/4:8's `การทรงปรากฏ`).

**Evidence base:** All 5 Pastoral occurrences of ἐπιφάνεια applied to Christ. Pre-revision: 1 Tim 6:14 used the heavier compound `การเสด็จมาปรากฏ`; 2 Tim chapters all used `การทรงปรากฏ`. External AI review identified the inconsistency as a lemma-lock break that context-disambiguation alone could resolve without lexical variation.

---

## The rule

**ἐπιφάνεια applied to Christ → `การทรงปรากฏ`** uniformly across the Pastorals, regardless of whether the referent is first-coming (Incarnation) or second-coming (parousia). Context disambiguates which appearing.

| Verse | Context | Sense | Thai |
|-------|---------|-------|------|
| 1 Tim 6:14 | "until the appearing of our Lord Jesus Christ" | second-coming | จนกว่าจะถึง**การทรงปรากฏ**ของพระเยซูคริสต์… |
| 2 Tim 1:10 | "made manifest now through the appearing of our Savior Christ Jesus" | first-coming / Incarnation | โดย**การทรงปรากฏ**ของพระเยซูคริสต์ พระผู้ช่วยให้รอดของเรา |
| 2 Tim 4:1 | "in view of his appearing and his kingdom" | second-coming | โดยอ้างถึง**การทรงปรากฏ**ของพระองค์ และอาณาจักรของพระองค์ |
| 2 Tim 4:8 | "all who have loved his appearing" | second-coming | ทุกคนที่ได้รัก**การทรงปรากฏ**ของพระองค์ |
| Tit 2:13 | "the blessed hope and appearing of the glory of our great God and Savior, Jesus Christ" | second-coming | (to be applied at Titus translation time per pastoral_corpus_locks_2026-05.md §C Granville-Sharp pre-decision) |

## Why uniform rendering across both senses

ἐπιφάνεια is the same Greek lemma in all 5 occurrences. The first-coming/second-coming distinction is **contextual, not lexical** — Paul does not use a different word for the Incarnation vs. parousia, even though the events are radically different.

If we render ἐπιφάνεια differently based on referent (e.g., `การเสด็จมาปรากฏ` for second-coming, `การทรงปรากฏ` for first-coming), we:

1. **Break the lemma-lock that the Greek deliberately maintains.** Paul's Pastoral choice to use one word for both manifestations of Christ is itself a theological move — the same Christ who was manifested in flesh will be manifested in glory; the two events are unified in Christology.
2. **Force readers into our interpretive grid for which-sense-is-which.** Every reader of 2 Tim 1:10 vs. 4:8 can see the contextual difference (incarnation-language in 1:10; eschatological-judgment-language in 4:1, 4:8). They don't need a lexical signal to know which sense applies.
3. **Create false-precision** — first/second coming is not the only distinction in ἐπιφάνεια Pauline usage. The Hellenistic background includes royal-visitation appearances; sometimes the word is used theologically for divine self-disclosure in any mode. Locking one Thai rendering to one sense would force us to pick a preferred theological taxonomy.

The single rendering `การทรงปรากฏ` (royal-prefix `ทรง` + `ปรากฏ` = "appearing/being manifested") works for both senses naturally — context supplies the eschatological vs. incarnational specificity.

---

## Why `การทรงปรากฏ` (not `การเสด็จมาปรากฏ`)

`การเสด็จมาปรากฏ` adds the verb `เสด็จมา` ("comes/comes-down") which:

- Foregrounds **second-coming travel-imagery** (Christ "coming down from heaven")
- Imports the frame of `παρουσία` (which IS rendered `การเสด็จมา` for second-coming usage; see `parousia_christou_2026-05.md`)
- Conflates ἐπιφάνεια with παρουσία semantically — but Paul deliberately uses both words in 2 Thess 2:8 (`τῇ ἐπιφανείᾳ τῆς παρουσίας αὐτοῦ`), showing they are distinguishable concepts
- Doesn't fit ἐπιφάνεια as Incarnation (2 Tim 1:10) — Christ didn't "come down" in the Incarnation in the same way as in the parousia; the manifestation was via embodiment

`การทรงปรากฏ` is:

- **Lighter** — pure manifestation/appearing, not directional-descent
- **Symmetrical** — works equally for first-coming and second-coming
- **Lexically distinct from παρουσία** (which renders `การเสด็จมา` per existing corpus lock)

---

## Distinction from παρουσία

Per `parousia_christou_2026-05.md`:
- **παρουσία** (Christ eschatological subject) → `การเสด็จมา` (royal arrival; preserves the specific "presence/arrival" force of παρουσία)
- **παρουσία** (human subject, e.g., Stephanas's coming) → `การมา` / `การกลับไป` (plain register)

Now extended:
- **ἐπιφάνεια** (Christ, both first + second coming) → `การทรงปรากฏ` (royal manifestation; preserves the visibility/disclosure force)

The two words deliberately distinguish: παρουσία = "arrival/presence"; ἐπιφάνεια = "manifestation/appearing." Thai preserves the lexical distinction.

---

## Why not preserve `การเสด็จมาปรากฏ` for second-coming + add a different rendering for first-coming

Considered. Rejected because:

- Compounds Pauline-Pastoral lexical choices that we cannot replicate cleanly in Thai
- Creates a 5-way rendering split across 5 Pastoral occurrences — unsustainable
- Forces a lexical commitment to first-vs-second-coming taxonomy in every occurrence, including Tit 2:13 which is contested
- External AI reviewers (ChatGPT + Gemini) both flagged the prior split as an unnecessary lemma-lock break

---

## When to revisit

- If a future translator-reviewer notes that Thai readers are systematically misreading 1 Tim 6:14 as a non-eschatological event (because the compound is now lighter), reconsider. The current expectation is that "until the appearing of the Lord" remains contextually clear.
- If Tit 2:13 (Granville-Sharp single-person pre-decision per `pastoral_corpus_locks_2026-05.md` §C) creates pressure to disambiguate ἐπιφάνεια specifically as second-coming there, that's a signal worth weighing — but the default-rendering-with-context-disambiguation should hold unless the Greek itself signals a need for split.
