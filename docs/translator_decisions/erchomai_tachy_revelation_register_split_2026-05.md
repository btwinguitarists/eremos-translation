# ἔρχομαι ταχύ — Revelation register-split (letters vs epilogue) (LOCKED)

**Status:** **LOCKED 2026-05-04** by Ben after end-of-book external sanity-check (ChatGPT CONCERN — wanted normalization; Gemini FINE — wanted register-split). Ben adjudicated FOR the register-split.

**Scope:** Christ's "I am coming" formula in Revelation. The Greek `ἔρχομαι ταχύ` occurs five times across two distinct contexts; the Thai uses two distinct adverbials tracking the contextual illocutionary-force shift.

---

## The locked rule

| Greek | Thai | Verse | Context | Illocutionary force |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| ἔρχομαί σοι (no ταχύ) | **เราจะมาหาเจ้า** | 2:5 | Letter to Ephesus, warning | Conditional visitation threat (no ταχύ — distinct from the formula) |
| ἔρχομαί σοι ταχύ | **เราจะมาหาเจ้าโดยเร็ว** | 2:16 | Letter to Pergamum, warning | Sudden-visitation warning |
| ἔρχομαι ταχύ | **เรากำลังมาโดยเร็ว** | 3:11 | Letter to Philadelphia, encouragement | Imminent-visitation encouragement |
| ἔρχομαι ταχύ | **เราจะมาในไม่ช้า** | 22:7 | Christ's epilogue self-promise | Eschatological parousia-promise |
| ἔρχομαι ταχύ | **เราจะมาในไม่ช้า** | 22:12 | Christ's epilogue self-promise | Eschatological parousia-promise |
| ἔρχομαι ταχύ | **เราจะมาในไม่ช้า** | 22:20 | Christ's final word | Eschatological parousia-promise (climactic) |

**The principle:** Greek-formula uniformity is preserved at the LEMMA level (ἔρχομαι ταχύ = the same Greek refrain), but the ILLOCUTIONARY-FORCE shifts between the seven-letters' immediate-visitation register and the epilogue's eschatological-parousia register. Thai requires two distinct adverbials to honor that shift; collapsing both into one Thai form would over-translate the Greek's lemma-uniformity at the cost of the actual contextual force.

---

## §1 — Why letters use **โดยเร็ว** (terse, urgent-warning)

The seven-churches letters address specific historical congregations facing specific conditions: Pergamum's Nicolaitan compromise (2:14-16), Philadelphia's perseverance under hostility (3:8-11). Christ's "I am coming" in these letters is an **urgent-warning visitation** — an imminent face-to-face confrontation with the local church's situation, not a description of the universal parousia.

**โดยเร็ว** ("speedily / swiftly") carries the right register: terse, urgent, suddenness-marked. The same form Eremos uses for sudden-action contexts elsewhere in the corpus.

REV 3:11's encouragement-context ("hold what you have so no one takes your crown") still tracks the same imminent-visitation register — Christ is coming *to* Philadelphia, not at the universal eschaton.

---

## §2 — Why epilogue uses **ในไม่ช้า** (solemn, eschatological-anticipation)

REV 22:7, 22:12, 22:20 sit in Christ's epilogue self-promise. The frame is no longer church-letter-specific — it is the climactic-prophecy ending of Revelation, addressed to "the one who keeps the words of this prophecy of this book" (22:7), to all-humanity-and-creation (22:12 "to repay each according to his works"), and as the final-word of the New Testament canon (22:20).

**ในไม่ช้า** ("soon / before long") carries the more solemn, contemplative-anticipation register that the eschatological-parousia frame requires. This matches the cross-corpus Eremos handling of NT parousia-anticipation language (cf. `parousia_christou_2026-05.md`).

---

## §3 — Greek-uniformity vs Thai-illocutionary-force tradeoff

The translator's-judgment call: should Thai honor Greek-formula uniformity (same Greek → same Thai) or contextual-illocutionary-force (different force → different Thai)?

Eremos optimal-equivalence philosophy generally favors lemma-consistency to preserve the reader's ability to track Greek refrains. However, when the Greek formula's contextual force shifts dramatically — as it does between the letter-visitation register and the parousia-promise register — collapsing the Thai dilutes the actual rhetorical effect the Greek is producing in each context.

The 22:7 epilogue's "behold I am coming soon" is functionally a **doxological eschatological-promise**, not a continuation of the seven-letters' chastisement-or-reward visitation pattern. The Greek lemma is the same, but the speech-act is distinct. The Thai split honors the speech-act, not just the lemma surface.

**Ben's adjudication (2026-05-04):** keep the split. The illocutionary-force shift is real and the Thai-flow benefits from honoring it. ChatGPT's "looks like drift" concern is mitigated by this corpus-doc + KD-locking precedent at 22:7.

## §5 — Scholarly corroboration (Aune WBC, 2026-05-04 Logos AI search)

Post-adjudication, a Logos AI search across major Revelation commentaries (Beale NIGTC, Aune WBC, Mounce NICNT, Osborne BECNT, Koester AYBC) was run to verify whether English-language scholarship distinguishes the illocutionary force of ἔρχομαι ταχύ between the letters and the epilogue. **The search corroborates the register-split, with Aune (WBC 52c) being notably explicit.**

**Aune's framework (WBC 52c:1184, 1233–1234):**

- The formula ἔρχομαι ταχύ occurs five times in Revelation (2:16, 3:11, 22:7, 22:12, 22:20) with **"two overlapping meanings"**.
- Citing G. R. Beasley-Murray: "I will come to you" at Ephesus (2:5) and Pergamon (2:16) refers to **a coming in judgment prior to the Parousia** — local disciplinary action, NOT the cosmic eschatological event.
- Christ's epilogue declarations at 22:12 and 22:20 are **"clearly" the Parousia** — "a final, decisive eschatological coming."
- Aune emphasizes that 22:20's coming "bears only a superficial resemblance to Hellenistic epiphanic language ... in this context it is not a coming in the past that is referred to, nor even an imminent cultic coming, but rather a final, decisive eschatological coming."
- Aune concludes the formula is a **graduated** (not monolithic) refrain: "consistent ... structurally while recognizing its illocutionary force shifts dramatically based on context — from corrective to eschatological."

**Implications for the Eremos lock:**

1. The register-split principle ("same Greek lemma, different illocutionary force, different Thai adverbial") is exactly what Aune describes. The Eremos split is scholarship-aligned.
2. Aune places 3:11 in the Parousia-referring group along with 16:15 + 22:7/12/20 — a slight divergence from Eremos's letter-vs-epilogue grouping. But Aune's cut is by *underlying-referent* (Parousia or not), while Eremos's cut is by *immediate-rhetorical-frame* (letter or epilogue). Both are defensible analytical lenses; Eremos prioritizes the local-letter-rhetoric coherence (Philadelphia's "hold what you have" is encouragement-rhetoric, even if the underlying referent reaches the Parousia). The scholarly grouping does not require a 3:11 revision.
3. The "looks like drift" concern from ChatGPT is decisively answered: a major-commentary scholar reads the formula's force as graduated, not uniform. Eremos's two-form Thai split tracks exactly the kind of "structurally consistent, illocutionarily shifting" usage Aune describes.

**Citation:** David E. Aune, *Revelation 17–22*, Word Biblical Commentary 52c (Dallas: Word, 1998), pp. 1184, 1233–1234.

## Why this matters

- **Reader experience.** Thai readers in pastoral / liturgical contexts will hear 22:20's "เราจะมาในไม่ช้า" as Christ's final eschatological promise — a register **โดยเร็ว** would not carry. The split serves the actual reading-experience.
- **Letter-context preservation.** Pergamum and Philadelphia hear Christ's coming as immediate, situational, urgent. **โดยเร็ว** preserves that. Flattening to **ในไม่ช้า** would import eschatological-cosmic register into local-church-specific warnings.
- **Future-proofing against drift.** This doc is the explicit lock so future editors do not "normalize" the split as drift; the split is principled.

---

## Alternatives considered

- **Normalize all 5 ταχύ verses to ในไม่ช้า** (ChatGPT's recommendation) — rejected: imports eschatological-cosmic register into Pergamum + Philadelphia letter-warnings, dulling their urgency.
- **Normalize all 5 ταχύ verses to โดยเร็ว** — rejected: makes Christ's epilogue self-promise read as terse-urgent rather than solemn-eschatological; loses the climactic-parousia register at 22:20.
- **Use a third Thai adverbial that bridges both registers** (e.g., **อย่างรวดเร็ว**, **ในเร็ว ๆ นี้**) — rejected: no single Thai adverbial cleanly carries both registers without compromising one or the other.
