# ἀμὴν λέγω ὑμῖν → เราบอกความจริงแก่พวกท่าน

**Scope:** Dominical solemn-pronouncement formula introduced by ἀμήν. Occurs 13× in Mark (3:28; 8:12; 9:1, 41; 10:15, 29; 11:23; 12:43; 13:30; 14:9, 18, 25, 30).

**Decided:** 2026-04 during end-of-book retro review of Mark.
**Evidence base:** Mark 3:28, 8:12, 9:1, 9:41, 10:15, 10:29, 11:23, 12:43, 13:30, 14:9, 14:18, 14:25, 14:30. Also Mark 11:23 rationale explicitly lists prior occurrences.

---

## The rule

**ἀμὴν λέγω ὑμῖν → "เราบอกความจริงแก่พวกท่าน (ว่า)"** — consistent across the book.

Standard forms:

- ἀμὴν λέγω ὑμῖν (plural) → เราบอกความจริงแก่พวกท่าน(ว่า)
- ἀμὴν λέγω σοι (singular; rare in Mark, cf. 14:30) → เราบอกความจริงแก่เจ้า(ว่า)
- ἀμὴν ... (emphatic-negative form, e.g., Mark 8:12 "εἰ δοθήσεται τῇ γενεᾷ ταύτῃ σημεῖον") → รendering adapts to the negative-oath syntax while preserving the solemn formula function.

The verb is ตรัสว่า (royal) when the narrator introduces the formula, as it is always Jesus speaking. The 1sg "เรา" is royal first-person (per `RULES.md §3`).

## Why not "อาเมน"

The Thai Christian tradition does use "อาเมน" at the close of prayers (matching liturgical Hebrew-Greek amēn). But Mark's ἀμήν is a sentence-initial solemn-affirmation — a distinct function. Transliterating as "อาเมน เรากล่าว..." would:

1. Foreground the foreign word rather than the solemnity it conveys.
2. Confuse the reader who hears "amen" as the close of prayer, not the opening of a pronouncement.
3. Be redundant with Jesus's name already marking who is speaking.

The idiom "บอกความจริง" (tell the truth) renders the amen-formula's semantic function (this is a truth-claim with the speaker's personal authority backing it) while sounding natural as narrative speech. This matches what the Greek does pragmatically.

## Why not "จริงๆ เราบอกท่าน"

Considered. The informal intensifier จริงๆ is too colloquial for a dominical saying. Register needs to match Jesus's other solemn speech (royal ตรัส, first-person เรา). จริงๆ would sound like a casual-emphatic aside.

## Alternatives considered

- **Transliterate "อาเมน ... " at the start of every formula.** Rejected (above).
- **Different rendering per context:** e.g., "แท้จริง" for cosmological-truth sayings (8:12, 9:1), "เราขอยืนยัน" for rebuke sayings (9:41, 10:15). Rejected as over-reading. The Greek ἀμήν is the same formula in every case; the Thai rendering should be equally stable. Contextual force comes from the saying's content, not the formula.
- **"เราบอกท่าน" without "ความจริง".** Rejected — loses the solemnity-marker. The formula needs the truth-claim.

## Johannine double-amen

For John's distinctive ἀμὴν ἀμὴν λέγω ὑμῖν (double-amen, 25× in John only), this Markan convention needs an extension. Provisional recommendation: "เราบอกความจริงแท้แก่พวกท่านว่า" — adding แท้ (genuine/real) to mark the redoubled emphasis. But John is a separate book with its own stylistic signature; decide at John translation time, not now. Locked here only for the Markan/Matthean/Lukan single-amen usage.

## Why this matters

- Across the synoptics, the amen-formula appears 30–40+ times. Drift would be noticeable to Thai readers who are learning to recognize it as "the solemn-pronouncement formula."
- The formula is doctrinally important: it is Jesus appealing to his own authority (not Scripture's authority, not a prophet's inspired authority) as ground for the saying's truth. This is an implicit Christological claim. The Thai rendering must consistently mark this.

## When to revisit

- If John's double-amen is rendered differently, revisit to ensure the single-amen Markan/synoptic form remains distinct.
- If a future scholarly reviewer (Thai-speaking NT scholar) recommends a more-Thai form of the truth-formula, discuss. The current "เราบอกความจริงแก่พวกท่าน" is a compromise between Thai naturalness and formula-recognition.
