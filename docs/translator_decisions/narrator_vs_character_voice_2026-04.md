# Narrator vs. character voice — register for Jesus

**Scope:** Whether Jesus is referred to with royal register (พระองค์, พระ-, ทรง, เสด็จ, ตรัส) depends on who is speaking.

**Decided:** 2026-04 during end-of-book retro review of Mark.
**Evidence base:** Mark 1:1, 5:7 (demon's voice), 6:14–16 (people's voice vs. narrator), 8:28 (crowd speculation), 14:45 (Judas), 15:2 (Pilate to Jesus), 15:29–32 (mockers).

---

## The rule

**Narrator-voice** (all of Mark's third-person narrative prose): Jesus is always พระเยซู / พระองค์ / เสด็จ / ตรัส / ทรง. The narrator is a confessing believer; his register never downshifts, regardless of whether the scene is sympathetic or hostile to Jesus.

**Character-voice** (speech inside quotations, or speech attributed to a specific speaker): register tracks the *speaker's* stance toward Jesus, not the narrator's.

- **Disciples, believers, pleading petitioners:** พระองค์ (2sg/3sg), ข้าพระองค์ / ข้า (1sg humble). Royal register granted.
- **Crowds, speculators, hostile Jewish leaders, scribes, Pharisees:** เขา (3sg), ท่าน (2sg). No royal register. Jesus is addressed as a peer-rabbi at best.
- **Outsiders (Pilate, centurions, pagans):** ท่าน (2sg), เขา (3sg). No royal register.
- **Demons / unclean spirits:** ข้า (1sg) + ท่าน (2sg) forced by power-recognition, but no พระองค์. Defensive-formal register; demons must acknowledge superior power but cannot give the honorific of worship.
- **Traitors (Judas):** address form may look orthodox but omits honorifics — Mark 14:45's "รับบี!" from Judas is a deliberate register flatness (see the note at that verse: "Judas does NOT use royal register addressing Jesus — a tell").

## Worked examples

- Mark 6:14: "ผู้คนพากันพูดว่า 'ยอห์นผู้ให้บัพติศมาเป็นขึ้นจากตายแล้ว เพราะเหตุนี้ฤทธิ์อำนาจอันยิ่งใหญ่จึงสำแดงในเขา'" — the *crowd* refers to Jesus as เขา (informal), while the *narrator* in the same verse calls Jesus พระนามของพระองค์. Per the verse's rationale: "the speakers are people in general — they refer to Jesus as 'เขา' (informal third-person), unlike the narrator's 'พระองค์.'"
- Mark 15:2: Pilate says "ท่านเป็นกษัตริย์ของชาวยิวหรือ" — ท่าน (peer-respectful) to Jesus, not พระองค์. The narrator then introduces Jesus's reply with ตรัส (royal): "พระองค์ตรัสตอบเขาว่า." Two registers in one verse, tracking speaker.
- Mark 15:29–32: passersby and chief priests mock Jesus in direct speech: "ช่วยตัวเองและลงมาจากกางเขนซิ." Their speech has no royal register. But the narrator's framing keeps royal register on Jesus ("พระองค์").
- Mark 5:7: the Gerasene demon says "ข้าขอสาบานต่อท่าน ... อย่าทรมานข้าเลย." — ข้า + ท่าน, not พระองค์. Yet the narrator still has Jesus issuing his exorcism command in royal register ("ตรัส").

## Why this split

Thai narrative prose cannot casually ambiguate speaker-voice the way Greek does. Every verb carries register. If the narrator flattens Jesus's register when quoting a hostile character, Thai readers will hear the narrator sharing the character's stance. Conversely, if hostile characters' speech is elevated to royal register, the theological distinctiveness of the confessing disciples' voice is erased.

Mark structures his narrative so the reader is always situated with the narrator's Christology (1:1 "the gospel of Jesus Christ [Son of God]") even when characters within the story fail to recognize it. The Thai translation preserves this by splitting narrator and character register.

## Alternatives considered

- **Universal royal register for Jesus regardless of speaker.** Rejected: flattens Mark's in-story Christological irony (where characters speak about Jesus without grasping who he is). A crowd saying "พระองค์" sounds like confession; "เขา" sounds like speculation. The Greek grammar doesn't mark the distinction; the Thai register must.
- **Narrator-level downshift during hostile scenes (e.g., trial, crucifixion).** Rejected: would undercut Mark's narrator's own confession. The narrator is not neutral.
- **No royal register anywhere, with piety signaled through vocabulary only.** Rejected: violates `RULES.md §3` and the established Thai Christian convention that Christ receives ราชาศัพท์.

## When to revisit

- If a reviewer finds the hostile-character-voice ("ท่าน" to Jesus) sounds culturally jarring — e.g., Thai readers finding it disrespectful even for Pilate — discuss. The current default prioritizes exegetical clarity over pietistic-universal-honorific.
- When translating Matthew and Luke, expect the rule to apply identically: same speaker-dependent register. Watch for sayings with direct speech reported across multiple speakers (e.g., at the empty tomb).
- Acts will stress-test this: Paul's trial speeches in Acts 22–26 have many hostile-speaker characterizations. The Markan pattern applies: narrator uses royal register for Paul (as a believer's narrator), hostile speakers in direct-quotation do not.
