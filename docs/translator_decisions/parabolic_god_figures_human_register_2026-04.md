# Parabolic characters that represent God — human register, not ราชาศัพท์

**Scope:** Human characters inside Jesus's parables who theologically *represent* God, Christ, or the Spirit — fathers, kings, masters, shepherds, vineyard owners, bridegrooms, judges. These figures are rendered in **standard Thai human register**, never in ราชาศัพท์ (royal/divine honorific register), regardless of their allegorical referent.

**Decided:** 2026-04 during Luke end-of-book review. Flagged by Gemini 2.5 Pro external review (2026-04-23) as an undocumented corpus pattern worth locking. The practice is already consistent across shipped parables in Mark, Matthew, and Luke; this doc formalizes it so future books don't drift toward premature allegorization.

**Evidence base (consistent across the shipped corpus):**
- MAT 13 (Kingdom parables); MAT 18:23–35 (unforgiving servant); MAT 20:1–16 (vineyard laborers); MAT 21:33–44 (wicked tenants); MAT 22:1–14 (wedding feast); MAT 25:1–13 (ten virgins); MAT 25:14–30 (talents); MAT 25:31–46 (sheep/goats — borderline, see below).
- MRK 12:1–12 (wicked tenants).
- LUK 7:41–43 (two debtors); LUK 14:16–24 (great banquet); LUK **15:11–32 (prodigal son — father figure)**; LUK 16:1–13 (unjust steward); LUK 18:1–8 (persistent widow — judge figure); LUK 19:11–27 (minas — nobleman / king).

---

## The rule

**Human characters inside a parable receive standard Thai human register, regardless of their allegorical theological referent.**

Apply when:
- The referent is a character inside the parable narrative (father, king, master, shepherd, vineyard-owner, judge, bridegroom).
- The character is narrated in third-person or addressed in second-person by other parable-characters.

Register rendering:
- Third-person narration: **บิดา / กษัตริย์ / นายของ- / เจ้านายของ- / เจ้าของสวนองุ่น / ผู้พิพากษา / ผู้เลี้ยงแกะ**. Plain human nouns.
- Verbs: **พูด / กล่าว / ตอบ / เรียก / สั่ง / มอบ / วิ่งไป / กอด** (NOT ตรัส / ทรงกล่าว / ทรงเรียก / ทรงวิ่ง).
- Body / action: **มือ / เท้า / ตา / หัวใจ / กอดคอ / วิ่ง** (NOT พระหัตถ์ / พระบาท / พระเนตร / พระหฤทัย).
- Pronouns by other parable-characters addressing the figure: **ท่าน / นาย / นายท่าน / นายเจ้าข้า** per social position within the parable.

**Do NOT apply when:**
- The narrator breaks frame to comment on the parable's referent *outside* the parable-narrative (e.g., Jesus's explanation in MRK 4:13–20 uses the narrator-register for Jesus and handles the sower's identity as an explanatory fact, not a dramatized character).
- The character is *Jesus himself* speaking about a parabolic framing of his own work (Son of Man / shepherd sayings where Jesus explicitly self-identifies in first-person). Those use Jesus-speech register.

## Why human register (and not ราชาศัพท์)

**Argument for human register (chosen):**
- **Parables work by analogy, not by allegorical substitution.** Jesus's own parables-method depends on the listener *inferring* the theological referent from the plot, not having it spelled out by the language's honorifics. Thai ราชาศัพท์ applied to the father in LUK 15 would immediately signal "this is God" — collapsing the parable's inviting-interpretation into a telegraph.
- **Matches the Greek.** The Greek text uses ordinary πατήρ / βασιλεύς / κύριος (human master, not divine Κύριος) / οἰκοδεσπότης / κριτής / νυμφίος throughout parables, with no lexical marker distinguishing parabolic-God-figures from ordinary human figures. The Thai should follow.
- **Preserves the disciples' position as interpreters, not code-breakers.** In LUK 8:9–10 and MRK 4:10–12 Jesus himself distinguishes between those who "hear the parable and understand" and those who don't. If the Thai rendering hands the interpretation to the reader via honorifics, the parable's epistemic work is undone.
- **Cross-gospel consistency.** MAT, MRK, and LUK all landed this way without cross-book drift, validated by 2906 verses of shipped corpus. Ex post, the pattern is the right one.

**Argument against (considered and rejected): apply ราชาศัพท์ to parabolic God-figures to honor the referent.**
- Would elevate parabolic characters above their narrative station — the prodigal's father would read as "royal father" which no reader infers from the Greek.
- Would force inconsistency within parables that mix figures: MAT 22's king *and* his servants; LUK 19's nobleman *and* his slaves; LUK 15's father *and* his hired workers. If the God-figure takes ราชาศัพท์ and the others don't, the social hierarchy inside the parable becomes theological code rather than narrative realism.
- Would erase the intentional parabolic-shock of LUK 15:20 — "the father ran" (ἔδραμεν — Middle Eastern fathers didn't run; Jesus's audience would hear this as socially startling). If we render "ทรงวิ่ง" (royal-run), the Thai reader hears "of course God ran" and the shock is lost.

**Argument against: case-by-case judgment.**
- Rejected — invites drift. The explicit corpus lock prevents future translators (including AI sessions on Acts / Pauline allegories) from selectively honoring individual figures.

## The Sheep and Goats (MAT 25:31–46) — the borderline case

MAT 25:31–46 is **not a parable** in the classic sense — the Son of Man is explicitly named ("When the Son of Man comes in his glory"). This is apocalyptic-discourse, not parable. ราชาศัพท์ applies to the Son of Man throughout; "the king" (ὁ βασιλεύς v.34, 40; τοις-with-royal-prosody) also receives ราชาศัพท์ because the referent is made explicit in the text.

Contrast MAT 22:1–14 (wedding feast parable): the king is a parabolic-character; no explicit self-reveal by Jesus inside the parable-frame. **Human register applies**.

**Rule of thumb:** if Jesus identifies the theological referent *inside* the scene (by naming himself or using divine-self-disclosure language), ราชาศัพท์ applies. If the theological referent is left implicit-inferential, human register applies.

## Worked examples (Luke)

**LUK 15:20 (prodigal father):**
- GR: δραμὼν ἐπέπεσεν ἐπὶ τὸν τράχηλον αὐτοῦ
- TH: วิ่งเข้าไปกอดคอเขา *(vernacular "ran" + "hugged his neck")*
- NOT: ทรงเสด็จวิ่ง... กอดพระศอ / โอบพระกร *(royal run / royal embrace)* ← would erase the shock.

**LUK 15:22 (father to servants):**
- GR: εἶπεν ὁ πατὴρ πρὸς τοὺς δούλους αὐτοῦ
- TH: บิดาจึงกล่าวกับบ่าวไพร่ของตน *(human "said" with "servants of his own")*
- NOT: พระบิดาจึงตรัสกับบ่าวไพร่ของพระองค์ *(royal "said" with divine-possessive)*

**LUK 18:7 (persistent widow's judge-God analogy):**
- The unrighteous judge gets plain human register throughout (ผู้พิพากษาอธรรม).
- In v.7 Jesus pivots to "Will not **God** avenge his elect?" — at that pivot, the register shifts to divine (พระเจ้า + ราชาศัพท์ verbs) because Jesus names God explicitly. The judge-character stays human register.

**LUK 19:12 (nobleman / king — minas parable):**
- The ἄνθρωπος εὐγενὴς "a noble man" and later "king" (received kingship): rendered as **ผู้สูงศักดิ์คนหนึ่ง** → **กษัตริย์** (human nobility), with human-register verbs throughout.
- NOT royal-register even in v.15 when he returns ruling.

**LUK 20:9–19 (wicked tenants' vineyard owner):**
- ἄνθρωπος (a man) planted a vineyard → **ชายผู้หนึ่งได้ปลูกสวนองุ่น** (plain human).
- The owner's son (the "beloved son") gets human register too — even though the theological referent is Christ. The parable is Jesus's own pre-passion allegory about himself; the Greek still uses plain υἱός, and the Thai follows.

## Forward implications

### Acts
Acts has **no parables** (narrative-historical genre, not teaching-discourse). This rule does not activate until Pauline epistles.

### Pauline corpus
- **Rom 11:16–24** (the olive tree allegory): Greek uses ἡ ῥίζα (the root), κλάδοι (branches), Gentiles as ἀγριέλαιος (wild olive). These are *metaphors* not *characters*; no human-register question arises. Standard Thai nouns; divine-register applies to God's sovereign voice in the same passage.
- **1 Cor 9:24–27** (runner / boxer metaphors): same — metaphor, not parabolic characterization.
- **Eph 5:22–33** (marriage / Christ-and-church): the Greek explicitly names Christ + the church. Divine register for Christ applies outside the analogy frame. The parabolic-father rule does NOT apply because the referent is named.

### Revelation
- **Rev 19:7–9** (marriage supper of the Lamb): the Lamb is explicitly Christ; divine register.
- **Rev 12** (woman clothed with the sun): symbolic-apocalyptic, not parabolic. Handle per verse.
- **Rev 17–18** (Babylon the harlot / Babylon the great): explicitly named as Babylon; human register throughout for the personification.

## Alternatives considered

- **Human register throughout (chosen).**
- **Royal/divine register for allegorical God-figures.** Rejected — erases the parable's epistemic work.
- **Royal register at the moment of interpretive climax** (e.g., the father's running-embrace at LUK 15:20 shifts to royal register to mark the theological reveal). Rejected — would create intra-parable register oscillation that no reader parses correctly.
- **Footnote the theological referent** at each parable. Rejected for main text — footnotes are already carried in the `thai_summary` and `notes` fields; the main text should read as narrative.

## When to revisit

- If a native-Thai reader reports the absence of ราชาศัพท์ on the prodigal's father or the minas-king as sounding irreverent or theologically problematic. Current hypothesis: Thai Christian readers parse parabolic-characters as characters, and register-flatness is both expected and appropriate.
- When shipping Revelation, specifically Rev 21:9 (the bridegroom-Lamb) and Rev 19:7 (wedding of the Lamb): the theological referent IS explicitly named; register application diverges from the parable-rule.
