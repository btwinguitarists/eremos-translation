# Honorifics for non-divine human authorities

**Scope:** Herod Antipas, Pontius Pilate, Roman centurions, Jewish-Sanhedrin leaders, and other human rulers who appear as secondary characters in the gospel narrative.

**Decided:** 2026-04 during end-of-book retro review of Mark.
**Evidence base:** Mark 6:14–29 (Herod); Mark 15:1–15, 43–44 (Pilate); Mark 15:39 (centurion); Mark 14:53–65 (high priest + Sanhedrin).

---

## The rule

`RULES.md §3` specifies ราชาศัพท์ (royal register: ทรง / เสด็จ / ตรัส / พระ-) for **divine subjects** and distancing register for **demons/adversaries**. It is silent on the middle tier: human authorities (kings, governors, officers, priests) who are neither divine nor adversarial-demonic. The Mark translation has settled a pattern that should be locked:

**Default for non-divine human authorities: neutral/plain Thai register.**

- No ทรง prefix on their verbs (plain Thai verbs: พูด, ถาม, สั่ง, เห็น, ประหลาดใจ).
- No เสด็จ for their movement (plain ไป, มา, เข้าไป).
- No พระ- prefix on their body parts or possessions (plain มือ, เสียง, บ้าน).
- Third-person pronoun: เขา (not พระองค์).

**Exceptions (documented per verse):**

1. **Public royal role → ทรง on narrator-voice.** Mark 6:14 gives Herod Antipas "กษัตริย์เฮโรดทรงได้ยิน" (King Herod heard) — the narrator grants ทรง because Herod is being introduced as "the king" in his public-office role.
2. **Private/guilty speech downshift to plain.** Mark 6:16 has Herod speaking his tortured guilt: "ก็พูดว่า" (he said, plain speech) — no ทรง, even though v.14 did give him ทรง. The rationale is explicit in the verse: "NO royal 'ทรง' for Herod's speaking here — we use plain 'พูด' for Herod's tortured private confession. The narrator's 'ทรง' for Herod (used in v.14) was for his public position (king); here he's speaking his guilty mind, deliberate downshift to plain speech."
3. **Royal first-person (majestic plural) for kings in direct speech.** Mark 6:16 has Herod saying "เรา" for ἐγώ — royal first-person-plural-of-majesty fitting a king speaking. Also Mark 6:22 (the oath: "เราจะให้เจ้า"). This is the king's voice, not the narrator's ทรง.

## Pilate

Pilate never receives ทรง. Verbs: ถาม, ประหลาดใจ, ตอบ, พูด, ปล่อย, มอบ — all plain. Third-person: เขา, ตัวเขาเอง. Pilate speaking to Jesus uses ท่าน (peer-level-respectful 2sg), not พระองค์. This is consistent across Mark 15:1–15 and is a deliberate register choice: as a Roman outsider administering provincial justice, Pilate is not granted the Jewish-Jewish narrator's honorific. It also signals to Thai readers that Pilate is *not* the theological peer of Jesus.

## Centurion

Mark 15:39 has the centurion speaking the climactic "ἀληθῶς οὗτος ὁ ἄνθρωπος υἱὸς θεοῦ ἦν" — a pagan gentile's confession. The rendering uses plain verbs for the centurion ("เห็น ... ก็พูดว่า") — no royal register. The confession's theological weight comes from the content, not from honorifics applied to the confessor.

## Sanhedrin and high priest

High priest Caiaphas speaking in Mark 14:60–64: plain verbs (ลุกขึ้น, ถาม, กล่าว, ฉีก). No ทรง. Jewish-religious-authority does not in itself warrant honorifics; holiness of office ≠ honorific register for the office-holder in narrative prose.

---

## Alternatives considered

- **Universal ทรง for all kings and governors.** Rejected. Flattens Mark's narrative distinction between the divine king and earthly kings. Thai readers would lose the rhetorical contrast.
- **Honorifics for human authorities only when they are introduced with their title.** Rejected as too mechanical. The register needs to track narrative weight, not surface-grammar.
- **Consistent neutral register even in public-king framing.** Rejected for Herod's introduction in Mark 6:14 specifically, because Mark's Greek (ὁ βασιλεύς Ἡρῴδης) highlights the royal office at that moment.

## Why this matters

In Matthew, Luke, Acts, and the epistles there will be far more secondary-role human authorities: Herod the Great (Matt 2), Herod Agrippa I (Acts 12), Agrippa II (Acts 25–26), Felix, Festus, Gallio (Acts 18), Claudius Lysias (Acts 23), Caesar/Augustus (Luke 2, Acts 17), and the various Caesars named in epistles. Without an explicit policy, the pattern drifts — one chapter gives Herod ทรง, the next doesn't. The Mark precedent set here should travel:

- **Default:** plain/neutral register for all non-divine human authorities.
- **ทรง allowed** in narrator-voice when the Greek explicitly foregrounds their royal-office role *and* the narrative frames them as wielding that office.
- **Royal first-person (เรา) allowed** in direct speech of kings (including tyrants) to preserve the voice of royal self-reference, without the narrator endorsing them.
- **Never ทรง** for Roman governors/procurators (Pilate pattern) — they are administrators, not kings, in the narrator's frame.
- **Never ทรง** for priests, Sanhedrin members, or synagogue leaders in narrative prose.

## When to revisit

- If a Luke or John scene gives narrative-weight to a human king that Mark does not (e.g., Luke's extended Herod-Antipas trial at Luke 23:6–12) — the ทรง / plain judgment should be made per-scene, documented in the verse's `key_decisions`, and referenced back to this doc.
- If Ben or a Thai native-speaker reviewer finds the Pilate-plain pattern sounds harsher than intended, revisit. The current default prioritizes theological-hierarchy clarity over politeness.
- If Acts' treatment of Agrippa and Festus warrants a register distinct from Pilate's (e.g., because Paul is on trial and the narrator is friendlier to the courtroom), document at that point; this Markan default is a floor, not a ceiling.
