# Historic present → Thai past tense

**Scope:** Greek historic-present verbs (narrative verbs in present indicative with past-time reference, e.g., λέγει "he says" mid-story) are systematically rendered in Thai past tense.

**Decided:** 2026-04 during end-of-book retro review of Mark.
**Evidence base:** Mark 1:40–41, 4:35, 5:15–22, 9:2, 11:1–7, 14:17–45, 15:1–27, 16:2–6. Mark uses historic present ~150× — by far the densest of the four gospels for this feature.

---

## The rule

Translate every Greek historic-present verb as Thai past tense. Do not attempt to preserve the present-tense morphology.

- λέγει ("he says") → ตรัสว่า / พูดว่า / ถามว่า (past tense, per speaker register).
- ἔρχεται ("he comes") → เสด็จมา / มา.
- ἐκβάλλει ("he casts out") → ทรงขับออก.
- φέρουσιν ("they bring") → พวกเขาพา ... มา.

**Thai does not have a historic-present construction.** Thai's verbal system does not inflect for tense at all — time reference comes from context, adverbs, and aspect particles (กำลัง, แล้ว, ได้, เคย, จะ). Attempting to signal "present-tense verb inside past narrative" with an aspect particle like กำลัง produces durative-progressive semantics ("he is in the process of saying"), which is not what Greek historic present conveys. The effect in Greek is rhetorical *vividness*, not aspect — a device for pulling readers into the scene.

Since Thai cannot reproduce the rhetorical effect morphologically, we do not try. Past-tense narrative throughout.

The rationale is made explicit at Mark 15:2: "Historic present λέγει in Greek ('he says') — Mark's characteristic narrative device ... Thai past-tense ตรัสตอบ is the natural equivalent — Thai does not use present-for-past for this effect."

## Where the vividness goes instead

When the historic-present serves a discourse function (paragraph-shift, scene-change, speaker-change, dramatic-highlight), Thai can signal the function through other means:

- **Paragraph-break / scene-shift.** Use discourse particles (แล้ว, ครั้งนั้น, ทันที) or sentence-initial adverbial frames.
- **Speaker-change.** Explicit re-identification of subject ("พระเยซูตรัสว่า", "เปโตรพูดว่า") rather than pronoun-only.
- **Dramatic-highlight.** Markan εὐθύς already carries the pace; no additional signaling needed.

## Why not keep present tense

- Thai "ตรัสว่า" vs. "กำลังตรัสว่า": the former is simple past, the latter is progressive ("was [in the process of] saying"). Neither is a historic present. Using กำลัง for every historic-present would make the narrative sound aspect-heavy and breathless in a way Thai does not support for narrative prose.
- English-speaker instinct may want to mirror historic-present as English does ("Jesus says to them...") — but Thai narrative-prose convention does not accept this. Thai-reader naturalness (`RULES.md §1` pillar) requires past-tense throughout.
- A third option — rendering historic-presents as Thai present-habitual ("พระเยซูตรัสอยู่เสมอ") — is also wrong: it implies generic-habitual, not scene-vivid.

## Alternatives considered

- **Mark the first word of a historic-present clause with a special particle.** Rejected as clutter. Would need to be documented per verse anyway.
- **Leave narration in past tense but render Jesus's speech-framing verbs as present ("ตรัสว่า" with an implied aspect marker).** Rejected: inconsistent, and Thai readers would process this as aspect, not vividness.
- **Preserve present tense only in extremely marked cases (e.g., vision scenes like 9:4 "ὤφθη αὐτοῖς Ἠλίας σὺν Μωϋσεῖ").** Rejected at this stage; the Markan transfiguration uses aorist ὤφθη, not historic present, so this case doesn't arise here. Leaving the broader question open for John's dense historic-present usage.

## When to revisit

- **John will test this.** John uses historic present less than Mark but in structurally-marked positions (e.g., the resurrection narratives in John 20). If John's historic-presents carry theological weight that flattens badly when rendered past-tense, revisit per-verse.
- **If a Thai-native reviewer finds specific scenes too flat**, consider adding scene-change adverbials (ในทันใดนั้น, ขณะนั้น) for vividness without resorting to present-tense morphology.
- **For parables**, the historic-present issue intersects with gnomic-present (timeless proverbial statements, e.g., "the sower sows"). Those are separate and use Thai habitual/timeless frames; document per-parable.
